# IDAP: An integrated literature- and knowledge-graph-driven evidence prioritization pipeline for precision oncology

## One-Line Summary
A study developing and validating IDAP, a modular pipeline that takes a cancer patient's somatic variants (MAF) and cancer type as input and integrates four evidence streams — OncoKB curation, PubMed literature, the TxGNN knowledge graph, and ClinicalTrials.gov trials — to rank patient-specific candidate therapeutic drugs while presenting the supporting evidence hierarchy alongside them.

## Research Summary
Advances in tumor sequencing technology now make it routine to detect tens to hundreds of somatic variants per patient, yet only a small fraction of these connect to established therapeutic evidence. According to analyses such as those from TCGA, roughly 40% of tumors harbor an actionable variant, but only 7–10% actually match directly to an FDA-approved therapy. Expert-curated knowledge bases such as OncoKB and CIViC provide high-quality variant–drug annotations, but they are strongly biased toward common cancer types and recurrent driver mutations, leaving the majority of rare and low-frequency variants unannotated. Furthermore, neither curated databases nor machine-learning models keep pace with the rapidly growing biomedical literature. To fill this coverage gap, the authors argue for a computational framework that integrates curated, literature-based, graph-based, and clinical-trial evidence.

To this end, the authors developed the Integrated Drug Annotation Pipeline (IDAP). IDAP is a modular framework that combines four complementary evidence streams: (1) OncoKB's curated variant–drug associations, (2) literature-based evidence in the form of gene–drug co-mention counts extracted from PubMed abstracts, (3) graph-based drug prioritization using a TxGNN-derived biomedical knowledge graph, and (4) cancer-type-specific clinical-trial evidence obtained from ClinicalTrials.gov. The inputs are a cancer type and a MAF file, and the output is a per-patient report summarizing the detected variants, ranked therapeutic hypotheses, the evidence hierarchy supporting them, and relevant clinical trials. Importantly, IDAP is designed not to replace expert judgment but to aggregate and prioritize evidence in support of downstream triage and translational interpretation.

The core methodology is a within-sample percentile normalization to correct for scale imbalance across the different sources, together with a re-weighted combined score based on fixed bonuses. After converting txgnn_score, mention_count, and oncokb_score into within-sample percentiles (tx_pct, pm_pct, ok_pct) for each sample, fixed bonuses for multi-source support (support_count), presence of OncoKB evidence, and clinical-trial matching are added to produce the ranking. This rule targets a transparent and interpretable prioritization procedure rather than a learned clinical outcome.

Evaluated across 5 cancer types (BRCA, COAD, GBM, NSCLC, PDAC) for a total of 50 samples (10 per cancer type), IDAP expanded the range of evidence-supported therapeutic hypotheses beyond curated databases alone. Even for patients for whom OncoKB returned no drug recommendation (26/50, 52%), IDAP additionally surfaced a median of 87 candidate drugs (range 2–473). Under the re-weighted ranking, 24/50 of the top candidates were supported by 2 or more evidence sources, and 44/50 had ClinicalTrials metadata. In an exploratory external comparison (CIViC), across 41 eligible samples IDAP recovered at least one CIViC-supported therapy in 28 (versus 6/41 for OncoKB alone), and 13/41 matched within the top 10.

In conclusion, IDAP integrates heterogeneous evidence into a single structured evidence-hierarchy report, increasing the visibility of potential therapeutic hypotheses for variant and cancer-type contexts that were incompletely represented in existing expert-curated resources. The authors repeatedly emphasize that this output constitutes prioritized hypotheses for expert review rather than direct treatment recommendations, and they suggest that future work should improve the specificity of literature evidence, expand external benchmarks, and pursue expert/clinical validation of the top candidates.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Input/annotation**: Cancer type + MAF file input, on the GRCh38 reference. Variant annotation via OncoKB MAF Annotator + OncoKB API (fields: ONCOGENIC, HIGHEST_LEVEL, VARIANT_IN_ONCOKB, DrugBank identifiers).
- **Drug dictionary construction**: An anticancer drug reference list curated from ChEMBL (Zdrazil et al. 2024) — integrating drug indications, mechanisms of action, ATC classification, and approval status, then normalized, merged, and de-duplicated.
- **Literature-based evidence (PubMed)**: PubMed queries per cancer type + gene ("<cancer type> AND <gene> AND (therapy OR treatment OR inhibitor)"). After parsing abstracts and matching directly against the ChEMBL drug list, gene–drug co-mention counts are generated. (Co-mentions are interpreted as an evidence layer, not causal relationships; the paper notes that article-level identifiers are not preserved in the final merged table.)
- **Graph-based drug repurposing (TxGNN knowledge graph)**: Uses a TxGNN-derived biomedical knowledge graph (Huang et al. 2024; 17,079 diseases, 7,957 drugs, 27,610 genes/proteins). Steps: (1) extract current therapies linked to the cancer type via 'indication' edges, (2) identify drugs targeting mutated genes via 'drug-protein' edges, (3) cross-reference with FDA-approved drugs (n=2,055) to detect approved drugs with non-cancer/other-cancer indications (repurposing candidates). Prioritization uses mutation-aware heuristic rules (based on indication, FDA approval status, and target relationships; with an additional bonus when a drug targets multiple mutated genes). The paper states that this is an interpretable heuristic rather than variant-specific deep-learning inference.
- **Clinical-trial evidence (ClinicalTrials.gov)**: The v2 REST API. Queries "<drug name> AND <cancer type>" retrieve up to 200 records per drug. NCT ID, title, condition, phase, and recruitment status are parsed. Per-drug summaries (n_clinical_trials; representative trial selected by highest-phase priority Phase 4 > 3 > 2 > 1; top_nct_id, top_phase).
- **Final combined-score formula**:
 `combined_score = 0.50·tx_pct + 0.40·pm_pct + 0.10·ok_pct + 0.20·max(0, support_count−1) + 0.30·I(oncokb_score>0) + 0.05·clinical_flag`
 (support_count = number of evidence layers supporting the drug; clinical_flag = whether ClinicalTrials metadata is matched). Within-sample percentile normalization is used to mitigate scale imbalance across sources.
- **Sensitivity analysis**: Comparison of 7 alternative weighting configurations (Supplementary Table S2). The default (0.50, 0.40, 0.10) maximizes the proportion of multi-source support + clinical-trial linkage. However, only 17 of 50 samples retained the same top-1 drug across all configurations (moderately sensitive to the choice of weights).
- **Validation data**: Self-generated and public somatic variant datasets (Song et al. 2024). Variant calling from recalibrated BAMs with GATK4 Mutect2 (v4.1.8.0), annotated with GATK4 Funcotator (v4.2.0.0) to generate MAFs. Additionally, the CPTAC cohort (Li et al. 2023; BRCA/COAD/GBM/PDAC) was obtained from the GDC Data Portal.
- **Performance evaluation**: 10 samples for each of 5 cancer types, spanning a variant burden range of 1–3061. Median total runtime 162 s (IQR 123–221), median peak memory 918 MB. Per-module runtimes (Table 1): OncoKB mean 2.96 s, PubMed up to 71.5 s, TxGNN 41–81 s, ClinicalTrials up to 279.96 s (the largest share of total runtime).
- **Visualization**: Reports include a bar chart of the top 20 drugs and a cancer–gene–drug network graph. The conceptual schematic was created with BioRender.

## Main Findings
- **Coverage expansion**: For the 52% (26/50) of patients for whom OncoKB returned no drug recommendation, IDAP additionally surfaced a median of 87 candidate drugs (range 2–473).
- **Evidence convergence (after re-weighted ranking)**: 48.0% (24/50) of top candidates were supported by ≥2 evidence sources; 23.6% (59/250) at the top-5 level. 88.0% (44/50) of top candidates carried ClinicalTrials metadata; 78.0% (195/250) at the top-5 level.
- **External CIViC benchmark (exploratory)**: Across 41 eligible samples, IDAP recovered ≥1 CIViC-supported therapy in 28 (OncoKB alone: 6/41). 13/41 matched within the top 10, with a median first-match rank of 20.5. (However, a match at rank 1 occurred in only 2/41 and within the top 5 in 9/41, so this is not at the level of clinical calibration.)
- **Drug-count distribution**: The mean number of drugs per patient by cancer type was highest for COAD at 447 and lowest for PDAC at 78 — strongly correlated with variant burden (TMB).
- **Top-scoring cases**: The 4 highest-scoring drug–sample pairs were all FOSTAMATINIB (an FDA-approved SYK inhibitor with an ITP indication; with a history of evaluation in colorectal and hematologic cancers) in COAD and GBM, followed by ENDOSTATIN in NSCLC (recombinant human endostatin, Endostar, approved in China in 2005; meta-analysis shows improved response in platinum-based combinations) — the concordance with previously clinically validated therapies suggests the biological and clinical plausibility of the pipeline.
- **Literature signal**: TEMOZOLOMIDE (the standard therapy for GBM) was the most frequently mentioned drug across all samples, reflecting that the biomedical literature is concentrated on established disease contexts.
- **Strong repurposing prioritization**: Among the TxGNN categories, 'repurposing-priority' drugs (unapproved for the input tumor but FDA-approved for another disease + targeting a mutation) accounted for the majority of proposals.
- **PDAC characteristics**: In the combined-score distribution, PDAC showed a higher median and wider range than the other cohorts, suggesting untapped repurposing opportunities in a difficult-to-treat cancer.
- **Performance**: Total runtime scaled linearly with variant burden (median 162 s), memory was stable regardless of variant count (median 918 MB), and API calls scaled with the number of candidate drugs (1–3104 per sample).

## Applications
- Assisting evidence triage and translational interpretation of clinical sequencing results — particularly securing therapeutic hypotheses for patients with rare and low-frequency variants where curated databases are empty.
- Automatically generating per-patient evidence-hierarchy reports for molecular tumor boards and expert review.
- Discovery of repurposing candidates: connecting FDA-approved drugs indicated for non-cancer/other-cancer conditions to a mutation context to derive early hypotheses.
- Direct linkage of each candidate drug to its highest-phase clinical trial (NCT ID) to support downstream trial search and patient matching.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- **Improving literature-evidence specificity**: The current approach is at the level of co-mention counts and does not directly establish causality, treatment sensitivity, or clinical relevance → room to advance to sentence/relation-level extraction.
- **Preserving article-level identifiers**: Restoring the abstract-source traceability lost in the merged table.
- **Variant-specific graph inference**: Currently only the structural relationships of the TxGNN-derived graph are used (no variant-specific deep-learning inference is performed) → room to integrate actual TxGNN model inference.
- **External benchmark expansion and clinical calibration**: Adding benchmarks beyond CIViC, expert/clinical validation of top candidates, and clinical calibration of the combined score.
- **Weight robustness**: The top-1 drug changed in 33/50 samples under alternative weightings → the arbitrary weight sensitivity of the ranking needs to be reduced.
- Extensible, modular structure that makes it easy to add new evidence sources and cancer types.

## Disease Relevance
The target is precision oncology broadly, validated across 5 representative cancer types (breast cancer BRCA, colorectal cancer COAD, glioblastoma GBM, non-small cell lung cancer NSCLC, pancreatic adenocarcinoma PDAC). It presents particular potential to surface untapped repurposing candidates in refractory cancers with limited treatment options, such as PDAC. That said, the output is limited to prioritized hypotheses for expert review rather than direct treatment recommendations, and the authors make clear that it has not been clinically calibrated.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Unlike prior approaches that used only a single curated database (such as OncoKB/CIViC) or a single method (graph or literature alone), IDAP combines **four evidence streams — curated, literature, knowledge graph, and clinical trials — into a single integrated pipeline**.
- GNN-based drug-repurposing models such as TxGNN had the limitations of being variant-agnostic and black-box; IDAP provides interpretable, patient-specific prioritization via a **mutation-aware heuristic + within-sample percentile normalization**.
- It secures evidence-based candidates even for patients where curated databases are empty (52%), **empirically filling the coverage gap**.
- It is explicitly designed for **evidence-hierarchy transparency and triage support** rather than replacing experts (to prevent over-interpretation).
