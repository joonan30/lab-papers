# Dysregulated RNA-binding proteins and alternative splicing: Emerging roles in autism spectrum disorder

## One-Line Summary
A review that organizes the molecular mechanisms by which dysregulation of RNA-binding proteins (RBPs) perturbs alternative splicing essential to neuronal development in autism spectrum disorder (ASD), and surveys RNA-based therapeutic strategies that target this axis.

## Research Summary
ASD is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by impaired social communication and restricted, repetitive behaviors (RRBs), arising from a complex interplay of genetic predisposition and environmental factors. Large-scale genomic studies have identified numerous risk genes that converge on synaptic function, chromatin remodeling, and early neurodevelopment, yet the diagnostic yield explained by copy number variation (CNV) and single-nucleotide variation (SNV) alone remains lower than expected. The authors highlight dysregulation of **alternative splicing and the RBP networks that control it** as a new layer that helps bridge this gap. Splicing generates diverse mature mRNA isoforms from a single pre-mRNA, precisely tuning gene expression and tissue morphogenesis, and is especially important in neuronal development.

The review first outlines the basic mechanism of pre-mRNA splicing. The spliceosome recognizes the 5′ splice site (GU), the branch point (A), the polypyrimidine tract (Yn), and the 3′ splice site (AG), and—through the coordinated action of U1, U2, and U4/U5/U6 snRNPs and associated proteins—removes introns via a lariat intermediate and joins exons. Alternative splicing is classified into exon skipping (SE), intron retention (IR), alternative 5′/3′ splice sites (A5SS/A3SS), and mutually exclusive exons (MXE); transcripts that create premature termination codons (PTCs) through frameshifts are degraded by nonsense-mediated decay (NMD), reducing their expression. The key regulators of this process are RBPs: SR proteins bind exonic/intronic splicing enhancers to promote exon inclusion, while hnRNPs bind splicing silencers to repress it, together forming a "splicing code." RBPs finely tune whether an exon is included in a combinatorial and context-dependent manner through cooperative and competitive binding, autoregulation and cross-regulation, and RNP complex formation.

The review then diagrams the many splicing factors that act across the stages of neurodevelopment (ESC → NSC → neuronal progenitor → immature neuron → mature neuron; proliferation, migration, neurite outgrowth, and synaptogenesis), including SRSF2, SRRM2/4, hnRNPH2, PTBP1/2, RBM4, WBP4, CELF2, RBFOX1/2/3, NOVA1/2, SAM68, SLM1/2, ELAVL1/2, and MBNL1/2. As representative examples, it describes how splicing of the α/β exons and exon 18 of DLG4 (PSD-95), and of exons 15/16 of SHTN1 (Shootin 1), regulates synaptic maturation and axon growth/specification. It also emphasizes that highly conserved **microexons** of 3–30 nt create protein–protein interaction surfaces that contribute to neuronal diversity, and that this microexon program is broadly disrupted in ASD.

The core of the review organizes the RBPs whose regulation is disrupted in ASD by family (Table 1). SR/SR-like proteins (SRSF6, SRRM4), hnRNPs (A1, U/HNRNPU, H), PTBPs (PTBP1/2), CELFs (CELF2/3/4), RBFOX (1/2/3), NOVA (1/2), other KH-domain proteins (SAM68, SLM1/2, FMRP), and ELAVL (ELAVL2) regulate the splicing of synaptic and neurodevelopmental genes such as NLGN3/4X, NRXN1, SHANK3, CACNA1C, CPEB4, DLG4, TSC2, CAMK2G, and GRIN1. The authors synthesize that imbalance in the expression and activity of these RBPs disrupts excitatory/inhibitory synaptic balance and neural circuit formation, contributing to the ASD phenotype. Because RBPs form an intrinsic network that regulates one another's expression and splicing, perturbation at a single point can propagate across post-transcriptional regulation as a whole.

Finally, the authors introduce ASD therapeutic strategies, divided into DNA-targeted (CRISPR gene correction) and RNA-targeted approaches (ASOs, splice-switching oligonucleotides, CRISPR-Cas13, and small molecules targeting RNA/RNP). Concrete examples include splice-switching oligonucleotides that modulate microexon inclusion in CPEB4, restoration of FOXP1 and AUTS2 protein levels, and RNA editing/Cas13 approaches in Angelman (UBE3A-ATS) and Rett (MECP2) models. In the conclusion, they anticipate that building fetal-to-adult brain isoform databases, understanding the splicing impact of brain somatic mutations and short tandem repeats, and long-read sequencing together with large language model (LLM)-based prediction will drive future precision-medicine interventions.

## Methods & Technologies
(As this is a literature synthesis review rather than an original experimental study, the technologies and resources introduced/cited in the text are organized below.)
- **RNA-sequencing–based isoform analysis**: alternative-splicing profiling of fetal-to-adult postmortem brain transcriptomes; microexon discovery via high-throughput RNA-seq
- **RBP binding-site mapping techniques**: HITS-CLIP (e.g., identifying more than 16,000 NOVA binding sites in the mouse genome); RIP-seq (analysis of Celf2-knockout cortex)
- **Microexon detection tools**: targeted PCR followed by sequencing, ATMap, OLego, MicroExonator (microexon quantification in mouse embryonic development)
- **RBP binding-specificity prediction and databases**: RBPDB, RBPmap, RNAmotifs, RBPsuite (deep learning), rBPDL (deep learning), and other user-friendly web-browser–based resources
- **Integrated RBP resources**: BrainRBPedia (a database integrating functional, tissue-specificity, and disease-association annotations for 1,072 RBPs); SFARI Gene (ASD risk-gene and phenotype resource)
- **Model systems**: SRRM4-depleted Neuro-2a cell line; C. elegans (Unc-75/Exc-7); knockout/overexpression mice (Celf2-null, Nova1/2-null, Ncam-null, CPEB4 overexpression, etc.)
- **Therapeutic-modality techniques**: antisense oligonucleotide (ASO), splice-switching oligonucleotide (SSO), CRISPR gene correction, Class II type VI CRISPR-Cas13 (hfCas13x.1), A-to-I RNA editing, small molecules targeting RNA/RNP
- **Future methodological outlook**: long-read sequencing, single-cell transcriptomics, LLM-based prediction of splicing motifs trained on large-scale transcriptomic and genomic data

## Key Genes / Proteins
(RBPs and their splicing targets addressed centrally in the review)
- **SRRM4 (nSR100)**: SR-like factor; regulates inclusion of neural-specific microexons (~50 nt); binds UGC motif; its reduction in ASD causes microexon abnormalities. Targets: ZFYVE27 (protrudin), CPEB4
- **SRSF6 (human ortholog of B52)**: SR protein; splicing of axon branching and choline acetyltransferase; associated with bipolar disorder
- **hnRNP A1 / hnRNP U (HNRNPU) / hnRNP H**: splicing-repressive factors; HNRNPU maintains 3D chromatin architecture and is associated with neurodevelopmental disorders and epilepsy
- **PTBP1 / PTBP2**: bind the polypyrimidine tract; mutual cross-regulation controls the NPC → neuron transition. Targets: FLNA (filamin) exon N, DLG4 exon 18; PTBP1 is regulated by miR-124
- **CELF2, CELF4 (CUGBP Elav-like family)**: regulate neural splicing; CELF4 haploinsufficiency (18q12.2 deletion) is directly associated with ASD; Celf2-null mice show autism-like behaviors
- **RBFOX1 / RBFOX2 / RBFOX3 (NeuN)**: bind the UGCAUG motif and antagonize PTBP1; involved in early neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity; expression is reduced in autistic brain; 48 SFARI targets. Targets: NIN (ninein), SHANK3, CACNA1C, TSC2, CAMK2G, NRCAM, GRIN1, CPEB4
- **NOVA1 / NOVA2**: KH-domain proteins; neuron-specific splicing and 3′-end processing; regulate GlyRα2 (GLRA2), GABA_A γ2L (GABRG2), and many synaptic genes (Gephyrin, Girk2); NOVA2 variants are associated with ASD and ID, and NOVA2 regulates 265 retained introns
- **SAM68 (KHDRBS1)**: regulates inclusion of exon 20 in NRXN1 pre-mRNA (calcium/calmodulin dependent)
- **SLM2 (KHDRBS3)**: regulates splicing of CADM1, TENM2, ADGRL3; synaptic adhesion and cerebellar morphogenesis
- **FMRP (FMR1)**: cause of Fragile X syndrome; regulates MBNL1 translation and autosplicing; abnormalities in FMR1's own microexon (exon 9a)
- **ELAVL2 (HuB)**: binds AU-rich elements; interacts with RBFOX1 to regulate CADM1, LMNB1, RTN4; LMNB1 is a candidate ASD biomarker
- **CPEB4**: mRNA polyadenylation regulator; SRRM4/RBFOX1 control inclusion of microexon 4; a key target regulating poly(A) length of ASD risk-gene sets
- **DLG4 (PSD-95), SHANK3, NRXN1, NLGN3/NLGN4X, CADPS2**: representative synaptic genes showing aberrant splicing in ASD
- **UBE3A, MECP2, FOXP1, AUTS2**: therapeutic targets (related to Angelman, Rett, and ASD risk genes, respectively)

## Main Findings
- The gap in ASD genetic diagnostic yield that CNV and SNV alone leave low can be substantially explained by a post-transcriptional regulatory layer of alternative splicing and RBP-network dysregulation.
- The **microexon program (3–30 nt, especially inclusion of neural-specific ~50 nt classes)** is broadly disrupted in ASD, with SRRM4 and RBFOX as its key regulators. SRRM4 reduction leads to abnormal microexon inclusion and neuronal dysfunction.
- RBFOX1 expression is reduced in autistic brain, and its 48 direct targets in the SFARI database are all involved in synaptic transmission, ion channels, and the cytoskeleton. Loss of two or more RBFOX proteins has a stronger impact on splicing regulation.
- PTBP1 ↔ PTBP2 mutual cross-regulation and miR-124–mediated regulation form the splicing switch of the NPC → neuron transition, and exon 10 skipping in PTBP2 pre-mRNA creates a PTC that is degraded by NMD.
- NOVA2 variants (frameshift/truncating) impair RNA binding and splicing, hindering neurite outgrowth and axon pathfinding; NOVA2 represses 265 retained introns and acts as a scaffold associated with PTBP2.
- Inclusion of CPEB4 microexon 4 is regulated by SRRM4 and RBFOX1, and mice overexpressing a CPEB4 isoform lacking the microexon have shortened poly(A) tail length and reduced expression of ASD risk genes—a convergence point of ASD pathology.
- CELF4 haploinsufficiency (18q12.2 deletion) is accompanied by low IQ, developmental delay, and behavioral disorders and is directly associated with ASD, while Celf2-null mice show social deficits, anxiety, and structural brain changes.
- RBPs form an **intrinsic regulatory network** that controls one another's expression and splicing, and BrainRBPedia integrates 1,072 RBPs to enable systematic prioritization of ASD- and ID-related RBPs.
- Advances in RNA-targeted therapy: a CPEB4-targeting SSO reduces microexon inclusion by blocking hnRNP/PTBP binding, and targeting the 3′ splice site downstream of exon 5 dose-dependently restores microexon inclusion and FOXP1/AUTS2 protein levels. hfCas13x.1 reactivated paternal Ube3a in an Angelman model, improving motor deficits and obesity over four months.

## Applications
- Presents the RBP–splicing axis as a new diagnostic and therapeutic target layer for understanding the molecular mechanisms of ASD and other neurodevelopmental disorders.
- Prioritization and functional interpretation of ASD-related RBP and splicing targets using resources such as SFARI Gene and BrainRBPedia.
- Provides a basis for target selection in RNA-based precision therapies, including ASO/splice-switching oligonucleotides, CRISPR-Cas13, RNA editing, and RNA-targeting small molecules.
- Design of personalized (precision-medicine) therapies based on brain-region– and developmental-stage–specific splicing isoform information.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Building a **public brain isoform data repository** spanning fetal to adult brain—essential for accurately identifying the dominant isoforms that would be ASO targets.
- Beyond de novo germline variants, elucidating the effects of **brain somatic mutations** on splicing regulators and cis-regulatory elements via single-cell and long-read sequencing.
- Characterizing the splicing-regulatory effects of **short tandem repeats (STRs)**, which are common in the human genome, using long-read sequencing.
- Research on unresolved targets in individual families, such as elucidating direct links between specific hnRNPs and ASD genes (e.g., hnRNP H and SHANK3).
- Using **LLM/deep-learning models** trained on large-scale transcriptomic and genomic data to predict splicing-regulatory motifs and the phenotypic effects (subtype, severity, sex differences) of genetic variants.

## Disease Relevance
The review frames the core pathophysiology of ASD as disruption of excitatory/inhibitory synaptic balance and synthesizes how aberrant splicing of the synaptic genes that determine this balance (NRXN1, NLGN3/4X, SHANK3, DLG4, CACNA1C, GRIN1, CPEB4, etc.) originates from RBP dysregulation. Splice-site variants produce frameshifts that activate NMD or yield dysfunctional proteins, perturbing synaptic signaling. Moreover, RBP dysregulation connects not only to ASD but also to intellectual disability (ID), epilepsy, and neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's, and shares mechanisms with adjacent neurodevelopmental disorders including Angelman (UBE3A), Rett (MECP2), and Fragile X (FMR1).

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- A comprehensive review that reframes ASD not from the perspective of single-gene variants or CNVs, but through the integrative post-transcriptional regulatory framework of the **RBP–alternative splicing network**.
- Consolidates the major RBP families—SR/hnRNP/PTBP/CELF/RBFOX/NOVA/KH-domain (SAM68, SLM, FMRP)/ELAVL—into a single table (Table 1) in the ASD context, down to their target genes, spliced exons, and functions.
- Its timeliness lies in jointly surveying cutting-edge topics such as microexons, retained introns, brain somatic mutations, and STRs, together with integrated resources like BrainRBPedia and the latest advances in RNA-targeted therapies such as CRISPR-Cas13 and splice-switching oligonucleotides.
