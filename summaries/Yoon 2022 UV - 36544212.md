# Chronic skin ultraviolet irradiation induces transcriptomic changes associated with microglial dysfunction in the hippocampus

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Kyeong-No Yoon, Yujin Kim (co-first authors, †), Yidan Cui, Jungeun Ji, Gunhyuk Park, Jin Ho Chung, **Yong-Seok Lee** (corresponding), **Joon-Yong An** (corresponding, joonan30@korea.ac.kr — An Lab), **Dong Hun Lee** (corresponding)
- **Journal / Year**: Molecular Brain, 2022; 15:102 (published online December 21, 2022)
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 36544212 / DOI 10.1186/s13041-022-00989-6
- **Article Type**: Micro Report (a short-format original report — mouse experiments plus RNA-seq transcriptomic analysis)

## One-Line Summary
In photo-aged mice subjected to 6 weeks of chronic ultraviolet (UV) irradiation of the skin, hippocampal cognitive function was impaired, and the hippocampal transcriptome showed large-scale downregulation of neuroimmune and microglia-related genes together with an increased number of Iba-1-positive microglia, thereby identifying UV-induced microglial dysfunction mediated through the skin-brain axis.

## Research Summary
The skin and the brain communicate bidirectionally to regulate local and systemic homeostasis of the body. As the first barrier in contact with the external environment, the skin senses and collects stimuli such as oxidative stress, ultraviolet/infrared radiation, and pollutants, converts them into cellular signals, and transmits these to other organs including the brain. In particular, UV is a key component of sunlight and the most important external stressor causing photo-aging, and it affects signaling pathways not only in the skin but also in other organs such as the brain. Previous studies reported that UV exposure raises skin-brain β-endorphin levels, inducing opioid-related analgesia and UV addictive behavior, and that moderate UV exposure elevates blood urocanic acid and enhances learning and memory through the glutamate biosynthesis pathway. Because the hippocampus is the central brain region for cognitive function and plays a pivotal role in learning and memory formation, it is a key target for investigating the effects of UV on the brain.

The authors had previously reported that repeated skin UV exposure reduces hippocampal neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity; however, the molecular mediators and cellular actors underlying these UV-induced neurobehavioral changes remained largely unknown. Accordingly, this study analyzed the hippocampal transcriptome in mice with photo-aging induced by chronic UV irradiation, aiming to identify the molecular and cellular signatures linked to changes in brain function, including learning and memory.

The investigators used an established skin-aging model in which UV dose was stepwise increased 3 times per week over 6 weeks (total 3240 mJ/cm²). The dorsal skin of anesthetized mice was UV-irradiated (treatment group) or sham-irradiated (control group), and epidermal thickening was confirmed by H&E staining, verifying that chronic UV induces skin aging. In the hippocampus-dependent Object Place Recognition (OPR) behavioral test, the discrimination index of the UV-irradiated group was significantly lower (P=0.049), confirming cognitive impairment. RNA-seq of hippocampi from the UV and sham groups then identified a total of 338 differentially expressed genes (DEGs; 10 upregulated, 328 downregulated).

Pre-ranked gene set enrichment analysis (pre-ranked GSEA, using mouse GO biological pathway terms from MSigDB) showed that the downregulated DEGs were significantly enriched in immune-related pathways such as immune response (GO:0006955, NES=−1.46, FDR=2.2×10⁻¹⁰), regulation of immune system process (GO:0002682, NES=−1.33, FDR=1.4×10⁻⁴), and immunoglobulin production (GO:0002377, NES=−1.71, FDR=5.6×10⁻⁴), indicating that UV exposure decreases the function of the neuroimmune system in the central nervous system and suggesting glia-related perturbation. In cell-type enrichment analysis based on a single-cell transcriptomic dataset (mouse cortex and hippocampus), among the 8 major cell types **microglia** were significantly enriched in the downregulated DEGs (NES=−1.31, FDR=1.5×10⁻²), suggesting that UV lowers microglia-specific gene expression and may impair microglial function. Meanwhile, immunohistochemistry revealed a significant increase in the number of Iba-1-positive microglia in the hippocampal region of the UV group, a finding of microglial activation and neuroinflammation contrasting with the reduced gene expression. Taken together, the authors concluded that chronic skin UV irradiation induces significant changes in the neuroimmune system of the hippocampus, accompanied by microglial dysfunction and cognitive decline.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Animal model**: Mouse skin-aging (photo-aging) model — after anesthesia, dorsal skin was UV-irradiated 3 times per week for 6 weeks with stepwise increasing dose (total 3240 mJ/cm²). The control group received sham irradiation after anesthesia. Sample sizes: Control n=4, UV n=5.
- **Histology**: H&E staining to verify epidermal thickening (skin aging).
- **Behavioral test**: Object Place Recognition (OPR) test — assessment of hippocampus-dependent cognitive function; discrimination index = (exploration time of novel location N − exploration time of familiar location F)/(N + F), with a 24-hour delay.
- **Sequencing**: Bulk RNA-seq of hippocampal tissue (UV vs sham). A total of 338 DEGs (10 up, 328 down) were identified.
- **Pathway analysis**: Pre-ranked GSEA, using mouse GO biological pathway terms provided by MSigDB.
- **Cell-type enrichment analysis**: From a publicly available single-cell transcriptomic dataset (mouse cortex and hippocampus, Zeisel et al. Science 2015), cell-type-specific marker genes were collected for 8 major cell types (hippocampal CA1 pyramidal neurons, interneurons, microglia, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, endothelial cells, ependymal cells, mural cells) and GSEA was performed.
- **Validation (RT-PCR)**: Quantitative reverse-transcription polymerase chain reaction to validate expression of Pla2g5, Lbp, and Wfdc2 against RNA-seq (Additional file 3, Fig. S1).
- **Immunohistochemistry (IHC)**: Anti-Iba-1 immunofluorescence staining to quantify hippocampal microglia number (activation marker).
- **Statistics**: Each graph shows mean ± SEM; criteria *P<0.05, **P<0.01. GSEA reports NES and FDR.

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **Iba-1 (Aif1)**: Microglial activation marker. The number of Iba-1-positive cells was significantly increased in the UV-group hippocampus (however, mRNA expression showed no change at the 6-week time point — a discrepancy between immunostaining and mRNA).
- **Pla2g5**: A DEG commonly appearing in the downregulated immune-related pathways. Validated by RT-PCR.
- **Lbp (Lipopolysaccharide-binding protein)**: A downregulated immune-related DEG. Validated by RT-PCR.
- **Wfdc2**: A downregulated immune-related DEG. Validated by RT-PCR.
- (In addition, leading-edge genes in Fig. 1F such as ENPP2, BMP8, ACE, TRPV4, AQP1, HTR2C, KL, MMP8, BST2, SFRP2, and THBD appear in immune and tissue morphogenesis pathways, but the four genes above are the core genes individually highlighted in the main text.)

## Main Findings
- In the OPR test, the discrimination index of the UV group was significantly lower than that of the control group (P=0.049) → chronic skin UV impairs hippocampus-dependent cognitive function.
- Hippocampal RNA-seq identified a total of 338 DEGs (10 up, 328 down) — overwhelmingly dominated by downregulation.
- In GSEA, the downregulated DEGs were significantly enriched in neuroimmune pathways such as immune response (NES=−1.46, FDR=2.2×10⁻¹⁰), regulation of immune system process (NES=−1.33, FDR=1.4×10⁻⁴), and immunoglobulin production (NES=−1.71, FDR=5.6×10⁻⁴).
- In cell-type enrichment analysis, among the 8 cell types, microglia were significantly enriched in the downregulated DEGs (NES=−1.31, FDR=1.5×10⁻²) → reduced microglia-specific gene expression.
- In IHC, the number of Iba-1-positive microglia in the UV-group hippocampus was significantly increased (**P<0.01) → microglial activation and neuroinflammation.
- Opposing directionality between gene expression (downregulated) and cell number (increased): Iba-1 protein (cell number) increases, but mRNA showed no change at the 6-week time point, revealing a discrepancy between immunostaining and mRNA results (a phenomenon also reported in prior studies).
- The downregulation of Pla2g5, Lbp, and Wfdc2 was independently validated by RT-PCR.

## Applications
- Provides molecular and cellular mechanistic understanding of the remote neural effects of UV through the skin-brain axis.
- Experimental evidence linking chronic ultraviolet exposure (photo-aging) to hippocampal neuroimmune/microglial dysfunction and cognitive decline — suggesting implications of UV protection and skin photoprotection for brain health and cognitive preservation.
- A starting point for intervention research on UV-induced cognitive impairment targeting microglial activation and neuroinflammation.
- The hippocampal transcriptome data (Table S1) can serve as a reference resource for follow-up neuroimmune and microglia research.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Further elucidation is needed of the correlational/causal relationship between the downregulation of specific microglia-related genes and microglial activation (increased cell number) (explicitly stated in the main text).
- Temporal tracking of the mRNA-protein discrepancy and microglial state changes at various time points beyond the 6-week point.
- Identification of UV-responsive microglial subtypes and neuroimmune circuits using high-resolution approaches such as single-cell/spatial transcriptomics.
- Verification of the mechanistic link between mediating signals of UV-induced neuroinflammation (e.g., blood-borne factors, urocanic acid, β-endorphin) and hippocampal transcriptomic changes.
- Loss-of-function/rescue experiments to establish the causal relationship between behavioral/cognitive phenotypes and molecular changes.

## Disease Relevance
- Microglial activation and neuroinflammation are common pathological mechanisms of cognitive impairment and neurodegenerative diseases, suggesting that chronic UV exposure may remotely contribute to such brain pathologies.
- The link between skin photo-aging and hippocampal cognitive decline contributes to understanding the interaction between aging/skin diseases (e.g., photo-aging) and brain health.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Beyond the authors' prior work (repeated UV reducing hippocampal neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity), this study is the first to identify, at the transcriptomic level, the **molecular mediators and cellular actors** of chronic UV-induced neurobehavioral changes.
- By combining hippocampal transcriptome analysis with cell-type enrichment analysis, it is the first to demonstrate that UV reduces the expression of neuroimmune-system and microglia-related genes.
- The dual finding of gene expression downregulation and increased Iba-1-positive cell number supports microglial dysfunction from multiple angles.
