# Evaluation of familial phenotype deviation to measure the impact of de novo mutations in autism

## One-Line Summary
The study introduces the "Within-Family Standardized Deviation (WFSD)," which normalizes the phenotype scores of children with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) against the scores of unaffected family members, to remove confounding from shared familial (polygenic) background and more accurately estimate the pure effect of de novo variants (DNVs); using it, the authors identify 18 novel ASD-associated genes and 11 genes with large between-family phenotypic variability.

## Research Summary
In autism spectrum disorder (ASD), germline de novo variants (DNVs) that are absent in parents and arise newly in the child are strong genetic causes, but a long-standing problem is that even the same gene and same variant type can lead to highly variable phenotypic outcomes. The authors argue that a substantial part of this phenotypic variability arises from the polygenic background and environment shared within a family, and that interpreting an ASD patient's phenotype scores using only population means or absolute values can over- or under-estimate the effect of a DNV. For example, when a parent's social score is already low (i.e., a heavy familial background), the DNV effect in the child may be underestimated.

To address this, the authors defined the Within-Family Standardized Deviation (WFSD). WFSD subtracts the score of the same family's unaffected members (the mid-parent average of the parents, or an unaffected sibling) from the ASD child's clinical phenotype score, and standardizes this by dividing it by the standard deviation of the general population. This approach cancels out the shared familial background and quantifies the pure phenotypic shift that a particular DNV induced within that family.

The researchers applied this method to whole-genome sequencing (WGS)/whole-exome sequencing (WES) data from 21,735 full-trio families across three cohorts of diverse ancestry — Korean Autism (n=2,605), the Simons Simplex Collection (SSC, n=9,082), and SPARK (n=67,098) — totaling 78,685 individuals (24,050 children with ASD, 11,165 unaffected siblings, and 43,470 parents). Using two axes — ASD core symptoms (social communication deficits, restricted/repetitive behaviors, and total symptom severity) and developmental abilities (cognitive, adaptive, and motor coordination) — they compared phenotypes according to the presence or absence of disruptive DNVs (dnDIS: de novo protein-truncating variants [dnPTV] in genes with LOEUF<0.37, and de novo missense variants [dnMIS] with MPC≥2).

As a key result, children with ASD carrying disruptive DNVs showed more severe behavioral symptoms and lower adaptive function than the unaffected members of the same family. WFSD revealed the association with DNVs more clearly than raw phenotype scores (odds ratio for carrying dnDIS at SRS WFSD≥2.6 was 1.39, versus 1.13 for raw SRS≥76) and performed better in identifying DNV-enriched genes. By the WFSD criterion, 201 genes were significantly enriched for dnDIS (versus 167 genes by the raw-score criterion); of these, 38 were detected only by WFSD, and among those 20 were previously ASD-associated (SFARI) while 18 were novel. In addition, an outlier analysis of within-family deviation per gene identified 11 genes with large between-family phenotypic variability, and this variability depended on the location of the mutation within functional domains.

In conclusion, family-based DNV analysis (WFSD) minimizes confounding from familial background and provides a reliable basis for accurate effect-size estimation, clinical outcome prediction, and precision support. The method reduces phenotypic noise to improve identification of ASD-associated genes while also capturing true between-family phenotypic heterogeneity arising from mutation location. This broadens the understanding of ASD phenotypic variability and points to potential targets for intervention.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Cohorts / scale**: Three cohorts totaling 78,685 individuals across 21,735 full-trio families — Korean Autism (n=2,605; 250 by WES, 2,255 by WGS), SSC (n=9,082; all WGS), and SPARK (n=67,098; 54,711 by WES, 12,387 by WGS). 24,050 children with ASD, 11,165 unaffected siblings, and 43,470 parents.
- **Sequencing**: Illumina platforms — Korean WGS on HiSeq X, SPARK WES on NovaSeq 6000, SSC WGS on HiSeq X10. DNA was extracted from whole blood for Korean and SSC samples, and from saliva for SPARK.
- **Alignment / variant calling**: Alignment to GRCh38 with BWA-mem. GATK best practices (Korean WES v4.1.8.1; SPARK and SSC v3.5) plus VQSR. Korean WGS used Illumina DRAGEN (v4.0.3). Joint genotyping used an iterative gVCF genotyper (Korean WGS), GLnexus v1.4.1 (SPARK), and GATK (SSC). PASS and quality filters (GQ≥20, DP≥10, etc.) were applied, and low-complexity regions, multiallelic sites, and large INDELs (≥50bp) were removed.
- **DNV identification**: The `de_novo()` function of Hail 0.2, with AF<0.01% in gnomAD v3.1 non-neuro. For Korean WGS, the de novo probability threshold was adjusted from 0.5 to 0.1 to align the number of DNVs with the GATK-processed set. Cohort-specific detailed quality cutoffs were applied (QUAL, GQmean, AB, AC=1, removal of outliers exceeding 9× the median standard deviation, etc.).
- **Variant annotation / classification**: Ensembl VEP v109.3. Variants were classified as protein-truncating variants (PTV), missense (MIS), and synonymous. **dnPTV** was restricted to genes with LOEUF (loss-of-function observed/expected upper bound fraction) <0.37, and **dnMIS** was restricted to MPC (missense badness, PolyPhen-2, constraint) ≥2. dnDIS = dnPTV + dnMIS (disruptive DNVs).
- **Polygenic score (PS)**: PRScs (global shrinkage phi=1×10⁻², gamma=1,0.5), with HapMap3 SNP LD reference (European UK Biobank). ASD PS used the Grove et al. GWAS (which includes SSC and iPSYCH → applied only to the Korean subset); educational attainment PS used the Lee et al. GWAS. The **pTDT (polygenic transmission disequilibrium test)** tested the deviation of the child's PS relative to the mid-parent by a one-sample t-test, and between-group differences by a two-sample t-test.
- **Clinical measures**: Two domains — (1) ASD core symptoms: social communication deficits, restricted/repetitive behaviors, and total symptom severity, measured with ADOS-2 CSS (SA, RRB, total), SRS T-score, SCQ (lifetime, current), and ADI-R (A, B, C). (2) Development-related: cognitive ability FSIQ, nonverbal IQ (Leiter), adaptive ability VABS-II (5 domains), and motor coordination DCDQ.
- **WFSD calculation**: The child's score minus the unaffected family average (mid-parent average or unaffected sibling), standardized by the general population SD. SDs used: SRS=10, VABS=15, FSIQ=15. For measures without a population norm, the SD was derived from the dataset (SCQ=10.45, ADOS Total=2.17, ADOS SA=2.13, ADOS RRB=2.61).
- **Statistics**: ANOVA across genetic subgroups; GLM regression controlling for age, sex, and cohort; normality tests (Shapiro–Wilk, Anderson–Darling, Kolmogorov–Smirnov); odds ratios by Fisher's exact test; Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment with the clusterProfiler R package v4.12.6, GO Biological Process, Benjamini–Hochberg correction (adjusted P<0.05); and enrichment for cell-type-specific differentially expressed genes (DEGs; 9 cell types and 39 clusters) in the developing human brain.
- **Outlier analysis**: The Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) of phenotype outcomes was computed per gene. Outlier-gene criteria — (1) exceeding the overall mean MAD for that variant type/phenotype by 2 SD, (2) more than 2 samples carrying the variant, and (3) (for WFSD only) the observed WFSD exceeding the 95th percentile. Position within functional domains/exons was analyzed with the UCSC Genome Browser.

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **PTEN**: Representative gene with large between-family variability in SRS for dnMIS; effects differ by position in the phosphatase domain, TI loop, and substrate-binding region (p.Thr131Ile 4.1 WFSD, p.Thr167Asn 4.2 WFSD are severe, vs. p.Asp92Asn 2.4 WFSD is mild; p.Arg15 and p.Ile101 show low VABS). Associated with ASD and cancer.
- **SCN2A**: Gene with large VABS variability for dnMIS; cited as a classic example of position-dependent phenotypic differences (voltage-sensing domain [GOF] → epileptic encephalopathy, pore/PTV [LOF] → ID/ASD).
- **ELAVL3, TRRAP**: Outlier genes with large between-family VABS variability for dnMIS.
- **CHD2**: Gene with large SRS variability for dnPTV; mutations enriched at the C-terminus (which promotes DNA-binding and ATPase activity). p.Glu966SerfsTer2 (in the earliest exon, P-loop ATPase; 4.7 WFSD, the most severe), p.Arg1637Ter (4.1), p.Gly1651TrpfsTer16 (2.4).
- **NCKAP1**: Gene with large VABS variability for dnPTV; p.Gly169ValfsTer14 (exon 5, −3.9 WFSD) is more severe than p.Glu1082Ter (exon 30, −2.3 WFSD) — an earlier-exon frameshift causes greater disruption. This variability is not readily apparent from raw VABS scores (64 vs 70).
- **MED13L, WDFY3**: Genes with large SRS (and, for WDFY3, VABS) variability for dnPTV.
- **SHANK3, WAC, TNRC6B, WDFY3**: Genes with large between-family VABS variability for dnPTV. SHANK3 (synaptic scaffolding) is also cited as a background example of exon-position-dependent severity differences.
- **Chromatin regulators uniquely enriched in the WFSD-specific severe group**: CBX4 (a component of Polycomb Repressive Complex 1), KMT2E, ZMYND11, and PRPF8 (an interactor of the high-confidence ASD gene STXBP1). "Modification-dependent protein binding" pathway.
- **ATP1A1, ATP2B2**: Genes in the "P-type/ATPase membrane transport" pathway of the WFSD-specific severe group. Associated with neurodevelopmental disorders (ID, epilepsy, ASD).
- **SPAG5, UNK, TNFRSF8**: Novel candidates in the top decile of dnPTV WFSD but not listed in SFARI and not included in prior effect-size estimates (reported associations with cancer and neurogenesis).
- Background mentions: FGFR3 (achondroplasia GOF missense, an example of variable expressivity correlated with parental height), STXBP1.

## Main Findings
- About 10% of children with ASD carried disruptive DNVs (dnDIS: dnPTV or dnMIS) (Table 1). These children showed significantly lower developmental abilities across cognition, adaptation, and motor coordination, and higher SRS T-scores (more severe symptoms), than non-carriers (Table 2; e.g., VABS total P=5.00×10⁻⁸, FSIQ P=1.41×10⁻⁶, nonverbal IQ P=5.71×10⁻⁹).
- Clinical outcomes of dnDIS carriers were highly heterogeneous: 45.5% had comorbid ID, 8.3% had intelligence above the population average, 66.6% had severe symptoms, and 33.4% had normal-to-moderate symptoms.
- Within-family SRS correlation: R=0.12 (P=2.8×10⁻¹⁰) for children with ASD and R=0.37 (P<2.2×10⁻¹⁶) for unaffected siblings — familial background contributes significantly to phenotype. Among the 26% of dnPTV/dnMIS carriers who showed normal-to-moderate SRS, 87% of their parents also had below-average SRS → without background correction, there is a risk of underestimating the DNV effect.
- SRS WFSD: children with ASD were on average +3.3 WFSD higher than their parents, whereas unaffected siblings were −0.21 WFSD. By subgroup, dnPTV 3.5 > dnMIS 3.3 > non-carriers 3.2 WFSD. ASD-associated genes showed larger deviation than DD genes (dnPTV 3.7 vs 3.6; dnMIS 3.8 vs 3.5, not statistically significant).
- pTDT: non-carrier probands showed over-transmission of ASD PS (P<0.001), whereas DNV carriers showed no over-transmission. Carriers of dnPTV in ASD-associated genes had significantly higher pTDT than non-carriers (P=0.02) → supporting a polygenic architecture in which rare and common variants act together.
- The WFSD distribution was closer to normality and had fewer outliers than raw scores. Association strength: odds ratio for dnDIS at SRS WFSD≥2.6 was 1.39 (95% CI 1.02–1.91), greater than the raw-score odds ratio of 1.13 (0.86–1.50) at SRS≥76.
- Gene discovery: the WFSD definition yielded 201 dnDIS-enriched genes (167 by raw score), with 38 WFSD-specific. Of these, 20 were previously in SFARI and **18 were novel**.
- Pathway enrichment (GO MF): both definitions shared transcriptional regulation, chromatin remodeling, and histone modification (well-known ASD pathways). WFSD-specific pathways — modification-dependent protein binding (CBX4, KMT2E, ZMYND11, PRPF8) and P-type/ATPase membrane transport (ATP1A1, ATP2B2).
- Cell-type DEG enrichment: raw and WFSD were similar across developing-brain cell types, but **enrichment of microglia and oligodendrocyte clusters (FDR<0.05) was observed only in the WFSD-defined severe SRS group**.
- 2D space (SRS symptoms × VABS adaptation): dnPTV showed a diagonal shift of 4.5 WFSD (symptoms up and adaptation down simultaneously), ASD genes 4.6, DD genes 4.6 WFSD. dnMIS was smaller overall at 4.2, and 4.5 WFSD for ASD genes. The greatest neurodevelopmental impact was in DD-predominant genes.
- Deviation relative to unaffected siblings was similar: dnPTV 3.9 > dnMIS 3.5 = non-carriers 3.5 WFSD (dnPTV 4.0 for ASD/DD genes) → validating that siblings can serve as a proxy when parental data are unavailable. For VABS: dnPTV −2.1, dnMIS −2.2, non-carriers −2.0 WFSD; for SCQ: dnPTV/dnMIS +1.8, non-carriers +1.7 WFSD.
- Genes with large between-family variability: of the 1,328 genes tested, **11** (4 dnMIS: PTEN [SRS], SCN2A, ELAVL3, TRRAP [VABS]; 7 dnPTV: MED13L, WDFY3, CHD2 [SRS], SHANK3, NCKAP1, WAC, TNRC6B, WDFY3 [VABS]). The variability is explained by domain and exon position (e.g., whether nonsense-mediated decay is escaped, or the greater disruption of earlier-exon frameshifts).

## Applications
- **Clinical outcome prediction / genetic counseling**: Using WFSD, which controls for familial background, the phenotypic shift of a specific DNV can be estimated more accurately, supporting prediction of the clinical course in children with ASD and the design of intervention, prediction, and treatment strategies tailored to individual genetic profiles.
- **Personalized variant interpretation**: By separating the pure effect of a DNV even when the familial background is heavy or light, it enables a framework for individualized variant interpretation and genetic counseling.
- **Gene-discovery tool**: By reducing phenotypic noise, it increases the power to identify ASD-associated genes (including 18 novel ones) that raw scores missed.
- **Suggesting intervention targets**: It presents information on mutation location and functional domains that drive between-family phenotypic heterogeneity as potential targets for precision intervention.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- The authors propose that future studies incorporate within-family deviation (WFSD) to correct for familial background effects.
- Investigating the sources of familial-background influence (inherited variants, environmental factors, and their interactions) would deepen understanding.
- Extending the scope beyond the two phenotype domains (SRS, VABS) to broader neurodevelopmental phenotypes would allow a more comprehensive understanding of ASD profiles.
- Functional validation studies are needed for the genes with large domain/exon-position-dependent variability flagged by the outlier analysis (PTEN, NCKAP1, CHD2, etc.).
- A limitation is that the subset with complete second-generation/sibling phenotype data is small → expanding family-based phenotype assessment data (standardized measures such as SRS and VABS) is needed.

## Disease Relevance
This work has direct relevance for elucidating genotype–phenotype relationships in autism spectrum disorder (ASD). It explains why disruptive DNVs produce very different clinical outcomes even within the same gene (e.g., comorbid ID 45.5% vs above-average intelligence 8.3%) along two axes — familial (polygenic) background and mutation location. It also presents 18 novel neurodevelopmental-disorder-associated genes and a gene group (PTEN, CHD2, NCKAP1, etc.) whose phenotypic impact strongly depends on domain/exon position, improving the molecular understanding of ASD etiology and clinical variant interpretation. The microglia- and oligodendrocyte-related cell-type enrichment suggests that neuroimmune and myelination pathways may be associated with severe phenotypes.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Most prior studies compared DNV carriers with non-carrier families but **did not formalize within-family comparison as a standardized deviation metric.** This study introduces WFSD to quantitatively cancel out familial background.
- Consistent application to a **large multi-cohort of 78,685 individuals** spanning diverse ancestries (Korean, SSC, SPARK).
- Direct comparison demonstrated that WFSD reveals DNV associations more clearly and robustly than raw scores (odds ratio 1.39 vs 1.13, improved normality).
- Validated that **unaffected siblings can serve as a proxy** for parents (nearly identical patterns).
- Using per-gene within-family deviation outlier analysis, identified **11 genes with large between-family phenotypic variability** and showed that this variability is explained by functional domain/exon position (NMD escape, frameshift location, etc.).
- Checked for possible ascertainment bias (participating families' high educational attainment and low sociability) via analysis of parent/sibling phenotype–DNV-carrier interactions and correlation with educational-attainment PS, confirming no significant impact.
