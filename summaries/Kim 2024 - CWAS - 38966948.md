# CWAS-Plus: estimating category-wide association of rare noncoding variation from whole-genome sequencing data with cell-type-specific functional data

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Yujin Kim, Minwoo Jeong (co-first authors), In Gyeong Koh, Chanhee Kim, Hyeji Lee, Jae Hyun Kim, Ronald Yurko, Il Bin Kim, Jeongbin Park, Donna M. Werling, Stephan J. Sanders, **Joon-Yong An (corresponding author; Korea University)**
- **Journal / Year**: Briefings in Bioinformatics, 2024, 25(4), bbae323 (Problem Solving Protocol)
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 38966948 / https://doi.org/10.1093/bib/bbae323
- **Article Type**: Methodology / software resource (development and application of a Python package)

## One-Line Summary
The study develops CWAS-Plus, a Python package that tests the category-wide association of rare noncoding variants from whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data by integrating cell-type-specific functional data, running roughly 50 times faster than the original CWAS while identifying noncoding risk variants within regulatory elements for autism (ASD) and Alzheimer's disease (AD).

## Research Summary
Noncoding regions of the genome are densely populated with tissue-specific gene-expression regulatory elements such as promoters and enhancers, and disruption of these elements can lead to disease through perturbation of gene expression. Advances in whole-genome sequencing (WGS) technology have made it possible to discover rare noncoding variants (RNVs) in disease, but traditional GWAS tests association at the locus level and therefore has insufficient statistical power for rare variants. The authors' previously developed CWAS (category-wide association study) overcame this limitation by grouping variants into many "categories" defined by combinations of genomic and functional annotations and testing association at the category level.

This paper presents CWAS-Plus, a reimplementation of CWAS as a Python package that substantially improves accessibility, extensibility, and speed. CWAS-Plus provides simplified parameter settings and an efficient multiple-testing correction method, and it runs the CWAS workflow approximately 50 times faster than CWAS on the same computing resources (36 cores, 256 GB RAM), reducing total runtime from 142 hours to 170 minutes. It can integrate arbitrary user-supplied functional annotation datasets—especially cell-type-specific regulatory elements such as those from single-cell ATAC-seq—enabling noncoding variant analysis within a cell-type context.

Applying CWAS-Plus to autism spectrum disorder (ASD) WGS data (de novo variant analysis, n=7280 samples), the authors identified noncoding de novo variant associations within transcription factor binding sites in conserved loci. In a separate application to Alzheimer's disease (AD) WGS data (n=1087 samples), they detected rare noncoding variant associations in microglia-specific regulatory elements.

Taken together, the study demonstrates that CWAS-Plus is useful and scalable for uncovering noncoding risk in genomic disorders from large-scale WGS data. In particular, by integrating cell-type-specific functional data it can reveal novel associations between regulatory elements and noncoding variants, and it provides a new method for estimating the number of effective tests for multiple-testing correction. The source code and user manual are publicly available on GitHub (https://github.com/joonan-lab/cwas/) and ReadTheDocs.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Input format**: The variant list is provided as VCF (genomic position, ref/alt allele, sample ID), and the sample list includes phenotype labels (case/control, etc.). Adjustment factors are supported to correct for confounders (in this study, correction for bias in de novo variants due to parental age and cohort).
- **Annotation**: VEP (Variant Effect Predictor, version 110) is used to annotate the most severe consequence based on Sequence Ontology, plus user-supplied functional annotations (e.g., epigenetic states) and functional scores (e.g., conservation/pathogenicity).
- **Categorization**: Categories are defined by combinations of 5 features—(1) variant type (SNV/indel), (2) genomic region (coding: PTV, frameshift, missense, damaging missense, in-frame, silent; noncoding: 5'UTR, 3'UTR, promoter, splice site, intron, intergenic, lncRNA, etc.), (3) gene set (disease-relevant gene sets, text format), (4) functional annotation (bed format), and (5) functional score (bed format). PTV is defined as LOFTEE 'HC', damaging missense as MPC ≥ 2, and promoter as 2 kb upstream of the TSS.
- **Burden test**: Category-level case-control association is tested in two ways.
  - *Variant-level*: The relative risk (RR) is computed from the ratio of case/control variant counts within a category, and the P-value is derived by a binomial test.
  - *Sample-level*: RR is computed from the ratio of case/control samples carrying a variant within a category. De novo variants are suited to variant-level testing, whereas rare variants are suited to sample-level testing to reduce false positives arising from individual differences in genetic background.
  - P-values are generated by case-control label swapping (permutation).
- **Risk score analysis**: A Lasso regression model. Focused on rare categories (fewer than 2 variants in controls), using per-category variant counts as features and disease phenotype as label. Lambda is optimized by 10 repeats of 5-fold cross-validation, selecting the lambda with the minimum mean cross-validation error. Performance is assessed by R², and significance is evaluated by 1000 rounds of label-swapping. The training set is 80% of all samples.
- **Burden shift analysis**: Phenotype is randomly reassigned by permutation (n=10,000), and the number of significant categories among categories of interest is compared to identify over-represented annotations.
- **Category clustering and estimation of the number of effective tests**: A correlation matrix is built from inter-category correlations (based on the variance of shared variants) → converted to a negative Laplacian metric → eigen decomposition determines the number of eigenvalues that explain ≥99% of the variance as the number of effective tests, which sets the genome-wide significance threshold. This correlation-based approach replaces the original CWAS method of generating 10,000 random variant sets, greatly reducing computational cost.
- **DAWN analysis (Detecting Association With Networks)**: Subnetwork detection within noncoding risk categories. Risk-associated categories are placed in 2D coordinates by t-SNE (using the top 50 eigenvalues) → k-means/Leiden clustering → disease association is tested with a hidden Markov random field model. Permutation P-values are converted to z-scores, and Sparse PCA identifies risk variants within each cluster. Variants in significant clusters with FDR < 5.0×10⁻² are classified as RNVs.
- **Downstream analyses**: Enrichment of human transcription factors (n=1622) (Fisher's exact test + FDR correction), protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks, and gene recurrence analysis.
- **Comparison tools**: Performance in prioritizing risk variants is compared against the deep-learning prediction tools Sei and Enformer, and STAARpipeline.
- **Datasets**:
  - Benchmarking: 1902 ASD families, 255,106 de novo variants, and 27,462 categories from a prior WGS study.
  - ASD variant-level: SSC + SPARK joint genotyping of 4270 ASD families (4354 ASD cases, 2926 unaffected siblings). After QC, 7280 samples and 474,788 de novo variants, with burden testing over a final 142,498 categories.
  - AD sample-level: ROSMAP 1196 individuals, 63,667,178 variants → after QC, 1087 samples and 25,052,701 variants. The risk variant analysis used 734 AD cases and 353 controls.
- **Scale of annotation datasets**: The ASD cell-type-specific analysis used 111 annotation datasets (derived from single-cell RNA-seq/ATAC-seq of fetal-to-childhood human brain)—4 functional scores, 19 gene sets, and 88 functional annotations.

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **CHD8**: ASD risk gene; used as an example category where an intergenic SNV lies near CHD8 target genes, in excitatory-neuron-specific CREs, and in conserved loci.
- **NCKAP1**: Candidate risk gene implicated in ASD and neurodevelopmental delay, carrying de novo RNVs.
- **CDKN1C**: A gene identified only through RNVs, previously reported as a neurodevelopmental risk gene.
- **NFE2L2, NOTCH1, SPEN, MTOR, BIN1, SRC, DVL2, SIN3A**: Candidate ASD risk genes involved in transcriptional regulatory pathways and carrying RNVs/PTVs (PPI network).
- **SMPD3**: AD significant recurrent risk gene [OR=Inf; P=2.0×10⁻²]. Encodes neutral sphingomyelinase-2—involved in Golgi lipid remodeling, accumulation of neurotoxic proteins such as APP/Aβ/pTau, and exosome/tau propagation. A candidate AD therapeutic target.
- **SCD**: AD significant risk gene [OR=Inf; P=2.0×10⁻²]. Encodes stearoyl-CoA desaturase—involved in lipid metabolism, immune response, and synaptic function. Its inhibition has been reported to restore synaptic density and reduce microglial activation; a candidate AD therapeutic target.
- **TMEM144, CERS6, PODXL, KALRN, NETO2, CYTH3, AJAP1, SLC38A2**: Genes appearing recurrently in AD RNVs.

## Main Findings
- CWAS-Plus reduced total runtime relative to CWAS on the same resources from **142 hours → 170 minutes (~50-fold reduction)**. Estimation of the number of effective tests improved dramatically from 138 hours (random correlation) → 40.2 minutes (correlation-based) (Table 1, Fig. 2A).
- **Linear scalability** with increasing samples and categories: about 15 minutes added per 400 additional samples, and about 6 minutes added per new functional annotation. With ~4000 samples, more than 27,000 comparisons were processed within 3 hours.
- Validation of the correlation-based effective-test estimation: correlation clusters (n=1477) were fewer than random variant clusters (n=2477), and 67.1% of random clusters converged into a single correlation cluster → the correlation-based method effectively replaces random variant sets.
- **ASD**: Among 142,498 categories, no category reached study-wide significance (P < 1.4×10⁻⁵), but in the risk score analysis the intergenic (R²=0.20%, P=1.3×10⁻²) and 5'UTR (R²=0.15%, P=1.1×10⁻²) categories were significantly enriched. Case-enrichment was observed in intergenic (P=1.0×10⁻⁴).
- ASD DAWN analysis: In intergenic variants, **8 clusters (FDR < 5.0×10⁻²)** were associated with ASD. Constrained loci, conserved loci, and ASD-associated transcriptional regulator binding sites were densely interconnected. Specific clusters were highly correlated with medial ganglionic eminence (MGE)-derived cells and fetal-brain-derived astrocytes.
- ASD transcription factor analysis: Transcription factors were **5-fold enriched (OR)** in RNV-affected genes relative to non-RNV genes, suggesting that RNVs can affect not only transcription factor binding sites but also transcription factors themselves, thereby perturbing transcriptional regulation.
- **AD**: In sample-level testing, no category reached study-wide significance, but in the risk score analysis noncoding, intergenic (R²=1.26%, P=4.3×10⁻²), intron (R²=1.67%, P=3.8×10⁻²), and **3'UTR (R²=1.51%, P=7.0×10⁻³)** were significant.
- AD 3'UTR DAWN analysis: **6 significant clusters (FDR < 5.0×10⁻²)**—enriched in microglia-specific CREs and constrained loci, suggesting a regulatory role for RNVs in neuroinflammatory pathways.
- AD gene recurrence: RNVs were observed in 705 cases, with 82 genes observed in 5 or more cases (OR>1). **SMPD3 and SCD** were significant recurrent risk genes (both OR=Inf, P=2.0×10⁻²).
- Risk variant prioritization comparison: CWAS-Plus showed significant case enrichment (OR=1.3, P=5.7×10⁻³, 95% CI 1.1–1.6), whereas Sei, Enformer, and STAARpipeline did not show significant case enrichment.
- As the scale of annotation datasets expanded (Set 1 48,680 → Set 2 69,702 → Set 3 142,498 categories), model performance for noncoding categories improved, but performance was governed not only by sheer scale but also by the **composition** of the additional annotations (some genomic regions actually reduced performance).

## Applications
- Provides a general-purpose pipeline for testing noncoding and rare variant associations in WGS by integrating arbitrary user-supplied functional annotations (e.g., cell-type-specific data such as single-cell RNA-seq/ATAC-seq).
- Applicable to both de novo variants (variant-level) and rare variants (sample-level), making it useful for WGS studies of diverse diseases.
- Lasso-based risk score analysis selects genomic regions and annotations that contribute to phenotype prediction (feature selection), and DAWN uncovers risk variant networks and candidate risk genes.
- Prioritizes disease-specific variants better than deep-learning tools (Sei/Enformer) or STAARpipeline by incorporating phenotype information and cell-type-specific data.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Cohort-level computation at the scale of hundreds of thousands of samples has not yet been performed, so optimizing resource usage under limited memory and computing environments is a future task.
- Plans to integrate additional functional datasets such as topologically associating domain (TAD) and 3D chromatin interaction data to strengthen identification of regulatory signals.
- Plans to expand CWAS-Plus application beyond ASD and AD to diverse diseases such as cancer.
- For diseases with a large degree of freedom, choosing appropriate annotation datasets and category compositions can be difficult, so accessibility will be improved by providing baseline annotation datasets such as putative promoters/enhancers.

## Disease Relevance
- **ASD**: Noncoding de novo variants are enriched in fetal brain regulatory regions and ASD-associated transcriptional regulator binding sites, and by perturbing transcription factors they may disrupt transcriptional regulation during fetal brain development, potentially contributing to ASD neurobiology. High correlation with sequence conservation suggests ASD noncoding associations acting through evolutionarily conserved regulatory elements.
- **AD**: Rare noncoding variants in 3'UTRs are associated with microglia-specific CREs, linking them to perturbation of neuroinflammatory pathways. Recurrent risk genes such as SMPD3 (sphingomyelin metabolism/tau propagation) and SCD (lipid metabolism/synapse) suggest potential therapeutic target candidates. This is consistent with prior findings from GWAS that microglia-expressed genes are enriched among candidate causal genes.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- The authors' prior CWAS (R/Python2) was reimplemented as a single **Python3 package**, adding multiprocessing, batching, annotation harmonization, and pip installation to improve usability and speed (~50-fold).
- For multiple-testing correction of rare variants, a **novel method for estimating the number of effective tests using correlations** among closely related categories (tests) was introduced—addressing the absence of a locus-correlation-based method as used in GWAS and greatly reducing computational cost relative to repeatedly generating random variant sets.
- By integrating single-cell regulatory datasets, it tests **cell-type-specific** noncoding associations, differentiating it from pre-trained deep-learning tools (Sei/Enformer) that do not reflect phenotype/cell-type context and from STAARpipeline, which focuses on narrowly defined region- and gene-centric tests.
- Supports both variant-level and sample-level testing, making it applicable to both de novo variants and rare inherited variants.
