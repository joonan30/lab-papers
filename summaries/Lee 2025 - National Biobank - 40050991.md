## One-Line Summary
A review that comprehensively compares the WGS-based approaches, technical infrastructure, and scientific discoveries of the world's major national biobanks (UK Biobank, All of Us, PRECISE, Biobank Japan, and Korea's National Project of Bio-Big Data, NPBBD-Korea), and highlights NPBBD-Korea's recent contributions to research on Korean- and East Asian population-specific genetic variants and rare diseases.

## Research Summary
Large-scale national genome projects are transforming genomic research and, driven by the falling cost of sequencing and a growing recognition of the value of population-level genetic information, are now producing whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data on the scale of hundreds of thousands to millions of individuals. The distinctive strength of these projects lies in integrating high-resolution WGS data with rich phenotypic, environmental, and clinical information. Unlike traditional genetic studies confined to specific diseases, they enable systematic exploration of the full spectrum of human genetic variation, including rare variants, structural variants, and regulatory elements. However, existing genomic resources have been heavily skewed toward populations of European ancestry, which limits the generalizability of findings and risks widening health disparities. National projects across diverse geographic backgrounds are working to address this imbalance.

This review is organized as follows: (1) an overview of the methodologies and characteristics of the world's major national biobanks, (2) the technological advances that have enabled sequencing, data analysis, and storage, (3) the key scientific discoveries generated by these resources, (4) the recent achievements of NPBBD-Korea, and (5) future directions for precision medicine and global health equity. The projects reviewed include the U.S. All of Us (245,000 WGS, target of 1 million), the UK Biobank (about 500,000 participants, with over 490,000 WGS), Singapore's PRECISE (SG10K → SG100K → 500,000), Biobank Japan (14,000 WGS, 270,000 SNP-arrays), and Korea's NPBBD-Korea (1 million people over 9 years, with a WGS target of 550,000).

The technology section compares projects across variant calling (GATK, DRAGEN, Sentieon, DeepVariant), multi-sample VCF generation (joint calling vs. aggregation, and the N+1 problem), data representation and storage (CRAM, sparse VCF, Hail VDS), cloud/HPC computing environments, downstream analysis with tools such as Hail and Glow, and tiered data access and security frameworks (Table 1). The discovery section covers eQTL locus discovery, rare-variant discovery and disease risk stratification, and the most recent findings from NPBBD-Korea.

In conclusion, the authors emphasize that WGS-based national genome projects are fundamentally advancing the prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of disease by comprehensively capturing coding and non-coding regions, structural variants, and ultra-rare population-specific alleles. In particular, multi-ethnic inclusiveness that overcomes the European bias is both a scientific and an ethical imperative: it improves the accuracy of polygenic risk scores (PRS) and helps identify population-specific disease variants. The authors anticipate that NPBBD-Korea will advance the understanding of the genetic diversity and rare diseases of Korean and East Asian populations, thereby contributing to global genomics, precision medicine, and health equity. They also note that the computational, logistical, and ethical challenges arising from this vast scale (data sharing, privacy, consent, and securing diversity) must be continuously addressed.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Study type**: Literature review. No newly generated or analyzed datasets (Data availability: "No datasets were generated or analysed").
- **Sequencing**: Primarily short-read WGS (mostly 30× coverage; 60× for cancer tissue), with mention of long-read sequencing (for analysis of complex variants, epigenetic modifications, and repeat expansions such as NOTCH2NLC).
- **Variant calling tools**:
  - GATK (BWA-MEM alignment + GATK variant calling) — the standard for UK Biobank, NPBBD-Korea, PRECISE, and BBJ
  - DRAGEN (FPGA hardware acceleration) — for ultra-large-scale processing in All of Us and UK Biobank
  - Sentieon (GATK-compatible acceleration) and DeepVariant (deep-learning-based improvement of variant accuracy) — All of Us
- **Multi-sample VCF generation**: joint calling (GATK GenotypeGVCFs, Graphtyper graph-based) vs. aggregation (DRAGEN Iterative gVCF Genotyper/IGG, All of Us's Genomic Variant Store/GVS, GLnexus). Aggregation solves the N+1 problem by allowing new samples to be merged without reanalysis.
- **Data representation and storage formats**: BAM → CRAM (reference-based compression, saving roughly 50% of storage), and dense VCF → sparse VCF (Hail Variant Dataset/VDS [All of Us], DRAGEN IGG multi-sample VCF [UK Biobank], Savvy, Sparse Project VCF).
- **Computing environments**: Cloud-based — UK Biobank RAP (DNAnexus + AWS), All of Us Workbench (Google Cloud Platform), PRECISE RAPTOR; local HPC-based — NPBBD-Korea (KISTI National Supercomputing Center), BBJ. Containers (Docker, Singularity), workflows (Nextflow, WDL), and the Terra platform.
- **Downstream analysis**: Hail and Glow (Apache Spark-based distributed processing), region-based association testing, and pre-subsetting of the genome to accelerate large-scale association analysis.
- **Data management systems**: UK Biobank "category-field" structure, All of Us DRC (Raw Data Repository/RDR + Curated Data Repository/CDR), NPBBD-Korea GIMS (Genomic Information Management System, developed by the Korea Research Institute of Bioscience and Biotechnology).
- **Data access and security**: Tiered access frameworks (UK Biobank 3 tiers, paid; NPBBD-Korea 4 tiers, free; BBJ 3 tiers; All of Us "data passport"; SG10K_Health RAPTOR), with measures against reidentification risk (anonymization, encryption, multi-factor authentication, access control, and real-time monitoring).
- **Statistical and analytical applications**: GWAS and meta-analysis, eQTL and TWAS mapping, polygenic risk scores (PRS), machine learning (genotype-to-phenotype prediction models such as DeepGAMI), CNV/structural-variant/short-tandem-repeat (STR) expansion analysis, trio-based de novo variant analysis, and proteomics/proteogenomics integration.

## Main Findings
- **Comparison of national biobank scale (Fig 1)**: By target cohort — All of Us 1 million*, NPBBD-Korea 1 million*, UK Biobank 500,000, BBJ 270,000, PRECISE-SG100K 100,000*. By available WGS (Fig 1b) — All of Us 245,000 (multi-ethnic), UK Biobank 500,000 (European ancestry), PRECISE-SG100K 50,000 (Chinese, Malay, Indian ancestry), NPBBD-Korea 25,000 (population-based + disease), Biobank Japan 14,000 (Japanese ancestry).
- **UK Biobank**: About 500,000 participants (ages 40–69), of whom 452,264 (93.5%) are of European ancestry; WGS on 490,640; over 1.1 billion SNPs and about 1.1 billion indels. Female 54%, male 46%.
- **All of Us**: As of February 2024, WGS on 245,388 participants released (target of 1 million). 77% of WGS participants belong to groups underrepresented in biomedical research (African ancestry 22%, Hispanic/Latino 18%, Asian 2%, European ancestry 51.1%). Over 287,000 with EHR; one-quarter with up to 10 years of longitudinal EHR.
- **PRECISE (Singapore)**: 3 phases (2017–2027). Phase 1 SG10K_Health 9,770 participants (Chinese 58.4%, Indian 21.8%, Malay 19.5%); Phase 2 PRECISE-SG100K over 100,000; Phase 3 target of 500,000.
- **Biobank Japan**: First phase about 200,000 participants (2003–2008), second phase 70,000 (2012–2017). WGS on 14,000; SNP arrays on 270,000 (two cohorts). Metabolome on 4,000 (additional 60,000 planned), proteome on 3,000 (+3,000 in progress).
- **NPBBD-Korea (National Project of Bio-Big Data)**: A national R&D project targeting 1 million people over 9 years (2024–2032). Phase 1 (2024–2028) recruiting 772,000 (rare disease 47,000, severe/cancer 140,000, general 585,000), of whom 240,000 receive 30× WGS. Cancer patients: 60× WGS on 41,000 samples (13 cancer types), and multi-omics on 3,000 samples across 5 cancer types. Phase 2 (2029–2032) adds more. Overall, the goal is to secure clinical and public data on 1 million people plus WGS on 550,000, with data provision beginning in 2026. **In the pilot, sequencing of 25,000 individuals was completed** (rare disease about 15,000, other diseases about 3,000, healthy individuals about 7,000).
- **Technology trends**: GATK remains the standard for its reliability, but its computational intensity is a limitation at ultra-large scale → speed and accuracy improved with DRAGEN (FPGA), Sentieon, and DeepVariant. CRAM saves roughly 50% of storage relative to BAM. NPBBD-Korea and BBJ use local HPC, while UKB, All of Us, and PRECISE adopt the cloud.
- **eQTL discovery**: Gamazon et al. integrated eQTLs across 44 tissues; an eQTL-based score for psychiatric disorders in UK Biobank (Barbu) and a mood-instability GWAS with 46 loci (Ward); osteoarthritis therapeutic targets (Tachmazidou); a TWAS of calcific aortic valve stenosis; 13 proteins and causal variants associated with colorectal cancer risk from FinnGen + UK Biobank; DeepGAMI elucidating genotype–phenotype relationships in schizophrenia and AD; the African-ancestry SIREN stroke cohort; Mendelian-disease eQTLs from the Qatar Biobank; and population-specific chronic kidney disease variants in the Indigenous Australian Tiwi community.
- **Rare variants and risk stratification**: Finnish population-enriched deleterious alleles in FinnGen (Kurki); deep WGS rare variants in Japanese (Nagasaki); protein-coding loci in UK Biobank (Sun); genetics of 35 blood and urine biomarkers; **73 disease-associated CNVs** (epilepsy, hypertension, chronic kidney disease, etc.) from integration of the National Project of Bio-Big Data + UK Biobank + Estonian Biobank; LRRK2 and IL10RA in IBD–PD comorbidity; differences in Crohn's disease allele frequency in African ancestry; rare variants in beta-thalassemia, congenital factor XI deficiency, and immune thrombocytopenia; and protein-truncating and damaging missense variants affecting educational attainment and reaction time identified from about 500,000 UK Biobank WES.
- **NPBBD-Korea recent discoveries (4.3)**: **122 novel associations** discovered from GWAS of 76 phenotypes in KoGES; **an additional 379 novel associations** and improved PRS predictive power from a meta-analysis of 32 phenotypes in KoGES + BBJ; a **Korean Reference Genome (1,490 individuals)** that substantially improved the imputation accuracy of rare and Korean-specific variants (superior to existing panels); de novo variants disrupting chromatin interactions and short-tandem-repeat expansions identified in ASD; a **diagnostic yield of 33%** achieved with trio-based WGS in children with neurodevelopmental disorders; diagnoses of Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease, inherited retinal disease, Marfan syndrome, and an RP1 Alu insertion; blood-group genotyping and prediction of transfusion phenotypes; MYH7 cardiomyopathy variant assessment; the finding that in the **largest ASD WGS dataset in East Asia, the burden of de novo protein-truncating variants is higher in females (a sex difference)**; and NOTCH2NLC GGC repeat expansion with long-read applications.

## Applications
- Improvement of precision medicine and disease risk prediction through the construction of population-specific reference genomes and imputation panels (especially for Korean and East Asian populations).
- WGS-based improvement in diagnostic yield for rare diseases and neurodevelopmental disorders (e.g., ASD) and resolution of undiagnosed diseases (by capturing structural, intronic, and non-coding variants).
- Improved multi-ethnic accuracy of polygenic risk scores (PRS) → prediction of complex diseases such as coronary artery disease, type 2 diabetes, and COVID-19 severity.
- Pharmacogenomics and discovery of therapeutic targets (proteogenomics integration, phospho-kinase targets, etc.).
- A practical comparative reference for designing national biobank data infrastructure (variant calling, storage, cloud, tiered access).

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Expansion of **long-read sequencing** to improve the resolution of complex, structural, and epigenetic variants and repeat expansions.
- **Multi-omics integration** (transcriptome, proteome, metabolome, epigenome) and **proteogenomics** for discovery of pQTLs and proteomic biomarkers.
- Advancement of **AI/machine learning** to elucidate genotype–phenotype correlations, predict disease prognosis, and identify novel therapeutic targets.
- **Real-time genomics** and EHR integration to directly support clinical decision-making.
- **Expansion of diversity and inclusiveness**: overcoming the European bias and recruiting underrepresented populations to realize global precision-medicine equity.
- International collaboration and accelerated development of Korean-specific drugs through linkage of NPBBD-Korea data (opening in 2026) with global genomic databases.
- Ongoing challenges: computational, logistical, and ethical infrastructure; governance of data sharing, privacy, and consent; and standardized methodology.

## Disease Relevance
The review has implications for elucidating the genetic architecture and population-specific risk variants across a wide range of rare and complex diseases, including brain disorders such as Alzheimer's disease and schizophrenia (APOE, TMEM106B, GRIN2D), cardiovascular and lipid disorders (PCSK9, CELSR2), inflammatory bowel disease–Parkinson's disease comorbidity (LRRK2, IL10RA), cancers such as colorectal cancer (PANK1) and breast cancer, cardiomyopathy (MYH7), Marfan syndrome (FBN1), inherited retinal disease (RP1), neuronal intranuclear inclusion disease (NOTCH2NLC), autism spectrum disorder (ASD) and neurodevelopmental disorders, chronic kidney disease, epilepsy and hypertension, and blood disorders (thalassemia, factor XI deficiency, immune thrombocytopenia). In particular, the sex difference in ASD (an increased burden of de novo protein-truncating variants in females) and the discovery of East Asian population-specific variants are emphasized.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- It is an integrated review that provides a **cross-sectional comparison of UK Biobank, All of Us, PRECISE, BBJ, and NPBBD-Korea across methodology, technical infrastructure, and discoveries** — rather than covering a single biobank.
- It organizes the **entire pipeline lifecycle** — from variant calling → multi-sample VCF → storage formats → computing → downstream analysis → management, access, and security — **in a per-project table (Table 1)**.
- It provides an in-depth, dedicated section illuminating **NPBBD-Korea's recent achievements** (122 + 379 novel associations, the 1,490-individual Korean Reference Genome, the ASD sex difference, the 33% diagnostic yield, etc.) from the perspective of East Asian and Korean populations.
- It emphasizes the scientific and ethical imperative of multi-ethnic, population-specific resources from the standpoint of overcoming the European bias and achieving global health equity.
