# Genome-wide de novo risk score implicates promoter variation in autism spectrum disorder

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Joon-Yong An (co-first author and lead author), Kevin Lin, Lingxue Zhu, Donna M. Werling (co-first authors, * equal contribution), Shan Dong, Harrison Brand, … Michael E. Talkowski†, Bernie Devlin†, Kathryn Roeder†, Stephan J. Sanders† (corresponding authors, marked with †)
- **Journal / Year**: Science, vol. 362, no. 6420, eaat6576 — December 14, 2018
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 30545852 / DOI 10.1126/science.aat6576
- **Article Type**: Research Article, Psychiatric Genomics, PsychENCODE special issue

## One-Line Summary
Using a "de novo risk score" built from whole-genome sequencing of 1,902 quartet families, this study provides the first genome-wide significant evidence that noncoding de novo variants contribute to autism — a signal invisible to any single annotation category alone — concentrated in evolutionarily conserved promoter regions, and specifically in distal transcription factor binding sites far from the transcription start site (TSS).

## Research Summary
It is well established, from studies focused on the protein-coding regions of the genome (about 1.5% of the whole genome), that de novo variants (variants absent in the parents but newly arising in the child) play an important role in autism spectrum disorder (ASD) and several other childhood developmental disorders. However, whether the remaining ~98.5% of the genome — the noncoding regions — contributes to developmental disorders, and which noncoding elements are most vulnerable to variation, was almost entirely unknown. Analysis of noncoding variation has been difficult because of (1) the vast size of the noncoding genome, (2) the limited ability of methods to predict functional regions and disruptive variants, and (3) the statistical challenge of interpreting a large number of tested hypotheses in an unbiased screen. Indeed, prior to this paper, no WGS analysis — even at cohort sizes up to 519 families — had demonstrated a significant noncoding de novo contribution after correction for multiple testing.

The authors used WGS data from 1,902 quartet families (mother, father, ASD-affected child, and unaffected sibling), totaling 7,608 individuals, from the Simons Simplex Collection (SSC). This family design enables detection of rare but large-effect de novo variants and allows direct comparison of ASD-affected children against their unaffected siblings as controls. In total, 255,106 high-quality de novo variants were obtained, corresponding to roughly 67 per child (67.3 in cases vs 66.8 in controls; no case-control difference after adjusting for paternal age). Each variant was assigned to one of 55,143 non-overlapping annotation categories, defined by combinations of 70 annotation terms across 5 groups (3 conservation definitions, 33 functional annotations, 14 gene lists, 17 GENCODE regions, and 3 variant types), and case-control variant burden was compared within each category using a category-wide association study (CWAS) framework.

The central approach was the "de novo risk score." When individual categories were tested separately (multiple-testing-corrected threshold P = 7.5×10⁻⁶, with 6,711 effective tests), 579 categories were significant, all in coding regions (mostly protein-truncating variants, PTVs, cRR = 1.92; some missense variants in ASD-associated genes, cRR = 2.90), and **not a single noncoding category was significant on its own.** The authors therefore integrated multiple annotation categories into a risk score that discriminates cases from controls: using a 519-family training set, they applied Lasso regression to assign coefficients to 238 categories, and applying this score to the remaining 1,383 families significantly predicted case status (R² = 1.67%, P = 5×10⁻¹²). This signal came not only from coding regions (R² = 1.08% excluding PTVs) but also from noncoding regions (R² = 0.54%, P = 0.02), and the noncoding signal originated mostly from promoter categories (R² = 0.50%, P = 0.01 with only promoters included).

To determine which kinds of variants and regions the association signal came from, the authors performed DAWN (hidden Markov random field) clustering and promoter structure analysis. This showed that the promoter signal splits along two axes: (1) an "Active TSS" group, defined by experimental epigenetic marks (RR ≈ 1.03, non-significant), and (2) a "Conserved Loci" group, defined by cross-species conservation (PhastCons/PhyloP) (RR = 1.28, P = 0.0002), with the clearest evidence of ASD association coming from evolutionarily conserved nucleotides. Furthermore, this conserved promoter signal was strongest not at the core promoter close to the TSS (e.g., the TATA-box, usually within 80 nt of the TSS) but in **the distal promoter located 750–2,000 bp away from the TSS** (RR = 1.32), with relative risk rising markedly at transcription factor binding sites (TFBS) defined by JASPAR (RR = 2.05). The authors proposed that these de novo promoter variants likely impair gene expression by perturbing interactions with enhancer elements within the promoter rather than by directly blocking transcription initiation, and concluded that promoter de novo variants with evolutionary and functional features contribute to ASD.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Sequencing**: Whole-genome sequencing (WGS). Data were generated at the New York Genome Center, with mean coverage of about 35.5×. Cohort: SSC 1,902 quartet families (7,608 samples total; 1,902 ASD cases and 1,902 unaffected sibling controls).
- **De novo variant detection and validation**: High-quality variant filters from prior work (Werling et al. 2018) were applied → 255,106 de novo variants (roughly 61.5 SNVs and 5.6 indels [≤50 bp] per child). All 4 members of families were validated by PCR and Sanger sequencing (validation rate 97.1% for SNVs, 82.7% for indels); 424 multi-variant loci in promoter regions received additional validation.
- **Annotation**: Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor (VEP) v90.4a44397 + GENCODE v27. Nucleotide conservation across 46 vertebrate species (PhyloP, PhastCons) and regulatory element annotations (transcription factor binding sites, chromatin states).
- **CWAS (category-wide association study)**: 70 annotation terms were combined to generate 55,143 non-overlapping categories → per-category case-control burden was tested by a two-sided binomial test (adjusted for paternal age). The effective number of tests was estimated by eigen-decomposition of P values from 10,000 simulated datasets (6,711 → threshold 7.5×10⁻⁶).
- **De novo risk score**: Starting from 8,418 categories after excluding categories containing de novo PTVs and rare categories with ≤3 variants in controls. Using a 519-family training set, the regularization parameter was estimated by 5-fold cross-validated Lasso regression → a 238-category model. Validation was in 1,383 families. Predictive significance was assessed by 1,000 permutations that randomly swapped case-control labels.
- **DAWN (Detecting Association With Networks)**: A hidden Markov random field model. The P-value correlation network of 1,310 promoter categories with ≥20 variants (based on 10,000 simulations) was clustered by K-means (K = 70) → 47 clusters; after PCA-based conversion to z-scores, 9 clusters were derived at Bayesian FDR 0.01.
- **Promoter structure analysis**: Promoters were partitioned into 4 segments relative to the TSS (core ≤80 bp, proximal 81–250 bp, distal A 251–1,000 bp, distal B 1,001–2,000 bp). A 200-bp sliding window combined with a generalized additive model (GAM) was used to estimate relative risk as a function of distance to the TSS. Transcription factor binding sites were defined by JASPAR CORE motifs.
- **Statistical methods**: Two-sided binomial test, permutation test, Fisher's exact test, Wilcoxon signed-rank test (WSRT), gnomAD control-burden comparison, and GO gene-set enrichment (FDR).

## Key Genes / Proteins
(This paper is a genome-wide analysis that addresses noncoding annotation categories and promoter regions rather than newly nominating individual risk genes, so it does not have a small set of "central genes." The items that figured centrally in the analysis are listed below.)
- **CHD8** — A chromatin remodeler and a prototypical ASD risk gene. In this paper, a "CHD8 binding target gene set" was used as an annotation/cluster (C28, C49) and showed overlap with the conserved promoter signal.
- **Developmental delay gene set** — A gene list derived from DDD and others. Cluster C42 showed enrichment for promoter variants (in the RR = 1.51 range).
- **Transcription factors (JASPAR CORE TFBS)** — In the distal region of conserved promoters, de novo variants concentrated at their binding sites (RR = 2.05). In contrast, no enrichment was observed at RNA polymerase II binding motifs (e.g., the TATA-box).
- **Conserved Loci group genes (886 genes)** — 53% protein-coding, 15% processed pseudogene, 14% lncRNA. GO enrichment: regulation of cell differentiation (GO:0045595), DNA-templated transcription (GO:0006351), and regulation of RNA pol II transcription (GO:0006357).

## Main Findings
- **No individual noncoding category is significant on its own**: The 579 categories significant after multiple-testing correction were all coding (575 PTV, cRR = 1.92, P = 2.9×10⁻¹¹; 4 missense in ASD-associated genes, cRR = 2.90, P = 5.7×10⁻⁴), and 0 noncoding categories were individually significant (Fig. 1E).
- **De novo risk score demonstrates the noncoding contribution**: In the 1,383-family validation set, R² = 1.67% (P = 5×10⁻¹²). The noncoding component R² = 0.54% (P = 0.02); the coding component excluding PTVs R² = 1.08% (P = 4×10⁻⁹).
- **Promoters are the core of the noncoding signal**: The three most frequently selected noncoding categories in the risk score were PhastCons conserved regions (68 of 163), PhyloP conserved nucleotides (49), and promoters within 2 kb upstream of the TSS (45). Including the 45 promoter categories, there was a 2.45-fold enrichment over expectation (P = 6×10⁻⁷).
- **Of 1,855 promoter categories, 112 were nominally enriched in cases** (cross-category burden P = 0.03), whereas enrichment in controls was seen in 6 (P = 0.94).
- **DAWN separates two axes**: an "Active TSS" group (based on epigenetic marks, RR ≈ 1.03, P = 0.32) vs a "Conserved Loci" group (based on conservation, RR = 1.28, P = 0.0002). Of 931 de novo variants at conserved loci, 557 (60%) overlapped with Active TSS.
- **Excess burden in cases at conserved promoters**: At conserved loci within promoters, cases 522 vs controls 409 (cRR = 1.26, P = 0.0003, permutation test). No excess was seen in other noncoding regions.
- **Signal is strongest at the distal promoter**: Variants at conserved loci showed the strongest enrichment in the distal region (1,001–2,000 bp) (RR = 1.32, P = 0.005). That is, the key region is not the core/proximal promoter but the region 750–2,000 bp from the TSS.
- **Specific enrichment at transcription factor binding sites**: When restricted to JASPAR TFBS, the relative risk rose (RR = 2.05, P = 0.0003). No enrichment at RNA pol II motifs.
- **Elevated risk of coding variants unobserved in gnomAD**: Coding de novo variants absent from the general population (gnomAD) were more strongly associated with ASD (470 vs 350, cRR = 1.34, P = 3×10⁻⁵).
- **Phenotypic associations**: After excluding cases carrying PTVs, cases carrying Active TSS (P = 0.02, WSRT) or Conserved Loci (P = 0.01, WSRT) promoter variants had a median nonverbal IQ about 4 points lower than non-carrier cases (Fig. 3F). Conserved promoter variants showed a trend toward increase in female ASD cases (OR = 1.13, P = 0.31) and afebrile seizures (OR = 1.46, 95% CI 0.90–2.36, P = 0.07), though not significant.

## Applications
- Provides an analytic framework (CWAS + de novo risk score + DAWN) for **integrating noncoding and promoter de novo variants as risk factors** in ASD and neurodevelopmental disorder research, and establishes a concrete, testable hypothesis for follow-up cohorts (the risk contribution of variants in distal TFBS of conserved promoters).
- The risk score has potential to serve, analogously to a polygenic risk score, as a basis for sample-level risk stratification and the development of early diagnostic indicators.
- By releasing code and criteria (PhastCons ≥0.2 and/or PhyloP ≥2 across 46 vertebrates, 2 kb upstream of the TSS, GENCODE v27, VEP), other research groups can reanalyze and extend the same 522 conserved promoter variant loci.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Replicate, refine, and extend the association using larger WGS cohorts (e.g., the WGSPD consortium), more refined noncoding annotations (e.g., PsychENCODE), WGS-specialized analytic methods, and functional validation such as massively parallel reporter assays (MPRA).
- Identifying which subset of the 522 conserved promoter variants actually confers risk will require functional validation in human cells (to confirm whether the regulatory effects observed in non-human models are conserved).
- Narrowing the association down to specific transcription factors and loci could lead to high-resolution neurobiological insights into the spatiotemporal and cell-type-specific developmental regulation that is disrupted in ASD.
- Extension beyond promoters to other regulatory elements such as enhancers, and to inherited regulatory variants.

## Disease Relevance
- Provides the first genome-wide-scale evidence that in the genetic architecture of ASD, **noncoding de novo variants — particularly variants in the distal transcription factor binding sites of evolutionarily conserved promoters — contribute to risk.** This suggests that ASD etiology is not explained by protein-coding variation alone and that the layer of gene-expression regulation is involved.
- Conserved promoter variants are associated with lower nonverbal IQ and show mild trends toward association with afebrile seizures and a female bias, suggesting a possible link to clinical phenotype.
- Ultimately, this has implications for developing diagnostic and therapeutic strategies (e.g., regulatory-element-targeted therapies), which may extend to other neurodevelopmental and psychiatric disorders.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Prior WGS studies (up to 519 families; e.g., 39–516 ASD families) failed to reach significance for any candidate noncoding category after multiple-testing correction. This study is novel in that it robustly demonstrated the noncoding contribution using a large cohort of 1,902 families and a **de novo risk score approach that integrates multiple annotation categories instead of testing individual categories.**
- Rather than being candidate-hypothesis-driven, it used an **unbiased genome-wide (CWAS) framework** to handle 55,143 categories simultaneously, and used the DAWN network to cluster correlated categories and dissect the biological identity of the signal (conservation vs epigenetic marks).
- It presented the unexpected result that the promoter signal is strongest not at the core/proximal promoter but at **the transcription factor binding sites of the distal promoter** (favoring a model of perturbed enhancer interactions over direct interference with transcription initiation).
