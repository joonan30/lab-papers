# Genomic patterns in the schizophrenia brain — Somatic noncoding mutations likely contribute to schizophrenia development

## Bibliographic Information

- **Journal / Year**: Science (Insights · Perspectives), 11 October 2024, Vol. 386, Issue 6718, pp. 146–147
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 39388575 / DOI 10.1126/science.ads4149
- **Article Type**: Perspective (commentary). This is a Perspective that discusses and evaluates the original research article by Maury et al. published in the same issue at p. 217 (Maury et al., Science 386, 217 (2024)). It is not an original research article producing new experimental data.

## One-Line Summary
A Science Perspective arguing that somatic (postmortem) noncoding single-nucleotide variants found in the brains of schizophrenia patients concentrate in transcription-factor binding sites and promoters of the developing brain with a distinctive mutational signature (CpG transversions and T-to-G substitutions), and that somatic mosaicism in these regulatory regions may contribute to the development of schizophrenia.

## Research Summary
Human germline mutations are inherited, are present in all cells, and participate in early development, whereas somatic mutations arise after fertilization and may or may not influence the developmental trajectory. Neurons accumulate hundreds to thousands of somatic mutations across the entire developmental process, and these mutations build up over time particularly in transcriptionally active regions, contributing to the disruption of neuronal function. This uneven distribution of somatic mutations suggests that specific genomic regions and developmental time windows are selectively affected, which can lead to developmental and functional abnormalities. Indeed, it has been reported that in the brains of autism patients, noncoding mutations are enriched in the binding regions of the transcription factor MEIS (MEIS1).

The study by Maury et al. discussed in this Perspective investigated noncoding somatic mosaicism in 61 postmortem brains from patients diagnosed with schizophrenia and 25 control brains from healthy donors. Unlike germline variants associated with schizophrenia and other neurodevelopmental disorders, somatic mutations in the schizophrenia brain showed no significant enrichment in protein-truncating coding variants or copy-number variants (CNVs). Instead, somatic single-nucleotide variants (SNVs) were enriched within gene promoter regions, and especially within transcription-factor binding sites (TFBS). These regions largely overlapped with DNase hypersensitivity sites of the normal fetal brain, suggesting that the regulatory landscape of the developing brain may contribute to the neurodevelopmental abnormalities observed in schizophrenia.

Maury et al. propose that a distinct mutational signature underlies somatic mosaicism in the schizophrenia brain. At active transcription-factor binding sites, CpG (cytosine-guanine) transversions and T-to-G (thymine to guanine) substitutions appear characteristically. CpG is a dinucleotide that mutates at a high frequency because cytosine is vulnerable to deamination; at active TFBS near promoters, the CpG-to-GpG transversion was increased 24.0-fold relative to the genome-wide expected C-to-G ratio (0.083). In addition, at TFBS near promoters, T-to-G substitutions were increased 82.6-fold over expectation. This effect was highly localized, with the frequency dropping sharply beyond 100 bp from the midpoint of the transcription-factor binding site. Furthermore, recurrent T-to-G mutations at the same positions were found in three unrelated schizophrenia brains, and this pattern was not observed in autism brains, raising the possibility that T-to-G substitutions at TFBS are a shared signature of schizophrenia risk development.

In functional validation, a massively parallel reporter assay (MPRA) showed only marginal differences between the effects of somatic SNVs in schizophrenia brains versus control brains. Nevertheless, some mutations downregulated the expression of neurodevelopment-related genes such as NFIX (nuclear factor I X) and ELAVL3 (ELAV-like RNA binding protein 3), or disrupted regulatory elements targeting schizophrenia risk genes identified in genome-wide association studies (GWAS). This emphasizes not only the nonrandom nature of somatic SNVs in the schizophrenia brain but also functional convergence in schizophrenia pathogenesis.

The authors (An and Kim) foresee that these genomic patterns of brain somatic mosaicism have the potential to dissect the complex genetic architecture of schizophrenia, and that mutational signatures could contribute to distinguishing schizophrenia subtypes and to understanding phenotypic heterogeneity. Just as mutational signatures in cancer have led to the identification of new subtypes and therapeutic interventions, they propose that in schizophrenia, integrating brain somatic mosaicism with polygenic risk scores could improve individualized risk prediction and the identification of patients suited for tailored therapies. Finally, they argue that deep learning and large language models (LLMs) will be useful for decoding these complex genomic and regulatory signatures, and for integrating epigenetic marks and chromatin accessibility data to elucidate the mechanisms by which somatic mutations arise during neurogenesis.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Article format**: Not an original article but a Perspective (commentary); it performs no experiments of its own and instead synthesizes and evaluates Maury et al. (Science 386, 217, 2024) and related literature.
- Methods cited from the commented-upon study (described indirectly):
  - **Whole-genome sequencing (WGS)-based somatic mosaicism analysis** of postmortem brain tissue — 61 schizophrenia brains + 25 healthy control brains
  - **Somatic single-nucleotide variant (SNV)** calling and enrichment analysis in promoters and transcription-factor binding sites (TFBS)
  - **DNase hypersensitivity site** overlap analysis (compared with regulatory regions of the normal fetal brain)
  - **Mutational signature analysis** — position-specific profiling of CpG transversions and T-to-G substitutions (midpoint ±100 bp)
  - **Massively parallel reporter assay (MPRA)** — validation of the regulatory (expression) function of somatic SNVs
  - Neurodevelopmental risk associations of promoter/regulatory regions were compared against existing GWAS and MPRA literature
- **Tools proposed by the authors for the future**: deep learning models, large language models (LLMs), and integration of epigenetic marks and chromatin accessibility data (multi-omics)

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **MEIS (MEIS1, myeloid ectopic viral integration site 1)**: Transcription factor. Noncoding somatic mutations are enriched in its binding regions in the brains of autism patients (cited from prior work).
- **NFIX (nuclear factor I X)**: Neurodevelopment-related gene. Its expression is downregulated by some somatic mutations in the schizophrenia brain.
- **ELAVL3 (ELAV-like RNA binding protein 3)**: Neuron-specific RNA-binding protein / neurodevelopment-related gene. Its expression is downregulated by somatic mutations in the schizophrenia brain.

## Main Findings
- Unlike germline variants, somatic mutations in the schizophrenia brain are **not enriched in protein-truncating coding variants or CNVs**; instead they are **enriched in noncoding promoter and TFBS SNVs**.
- The regions enriched for somatic SNVs **broadly overlap with DNase hypersensitivity sites of the normal fetal brain** → implicating the developing regulatory landscape.
- **CpG-to-GpG transversion**: increased **24.0-fold** at active TFBS near promoters relative to the genome-wide expected C-to-G ratio (0.083).
- **T-to-G substitution**: increased **82.6-fold** over expectation at TFBS near promoters.
- The mutational effect is **highly localized**: the frequency drops sharply **beyond 100 bp from the TFBS midpoint**.
- **Recurrent T-to-G mutations at the same positions were found in three unrelated schizophrenia brains**; not observed in autism brains → a possible schizophrenia-specific shared signature.
- **MPRA**: the difference in regulatory effect of somatic SNVs between schizophrenia and control brains was marginal, but some caused **downregulation of NFIX and ELAVL3 expression** or disrupted regulatory elements of GWAS schizophrenia risk genes.

## Applications
- **Classification of schizophrenia genomic subtypes** using the mutational signatures of brain somatic mosaicism, and interpretation of phenotypic heterogeneity.
- Refining individualized risk prediction and identifying patients for tailored therapy through **integration of polygenic risk scores (PRS) with brain somatic mosaicism**.
- Applying **prioritization of noncoding regulatory regions** such as promoters and transcription-factor binding sites to the interpretation of neurodevelopmental disorder risk.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Using **deep learning and large language models (LLMs)** to detect the sequence patterns and features of regulatory regions where somatic mutations frequently occur (applying to schizophrenia the approaches used in cancer tumor classification).
- Pursuing mechanistic understanding of how somatic mutations arise during neurogenesis by integrating diverse data such as **epigenetic marks and chromatin accessibility**.
- Detecting regulatory-region sequence patterns via LLM fine-tuning to predict the functional consequences of somatic mutations and to **discover novel therapeutic targets**.
- Studies linking schizophrenia subtype stratification with developmental pathways and environmental interactions.

## Disease Relevance
- The target disease is **schizophrenia** (a neurodevelopmental / neuropsychiatric disorder). The Perspective presents a pathogenic model in which noncoding somatic mutations may contribute to the development of schizophrenia by disrupting regulatory regions in the fetal brain.
- Through comparison with autism, it highlights the possible existence of a schizophrenia-specific mutational signature (T-to-G at TFBS), discussing shared and distinct mechanisms across neurodevelopmental disorders.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- In contrast to schizophrenia research that has largely focused on germline variants and coding regions, this commentary highlights the promoter/TFBS enrichment of **brain somatic (postmortem tissue) noncoding mutations** and their specific mutational signature (CpG transversions, T-to-G substitutions).
- In contrast to autism (MEIS enrichment), it proposes the possibility of a **schizophrenia-specific signature (recurrent T-to-G)**, and offers a novel perspective on the future direction of precision medicine through the integration of brain mosaicism + PRS + deep learning/LLMs.
