# Co-occurrence of rare variants implicates gene pairs in cytoskeletal pathways and is associated with increased severity in autism spectrum disorder

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Hyeji Lee (first author) and others. Corresponding authors are **Hee Jeong Yoo (hjyoo@snu.ac.kr)** and **Joon-Yong An (joonan30@korea.ac.kr)**. Co-authors include So Hyun Kim, Woong Sun, Eunjoon Kim, Anders D. Børglum, and Jakob Grove.
- **Journal / Year**: *Genome Biology* (2026) 27:147. Received 2025-05-14 / Accepted 2026-03-11 / Published online 2026-03-23. Open Access (CC BY-NC-ND 4.0).
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 41872878 / DOI 10.1186/s13059-026-04041-x
- **Article Type**: Research (original) — large-scale multi-ancestry genomic analysis + in vitro functional validation

## One-Line Summary
Using large-scale WGS/WES data from East Asian and European ancestries, the authors used RareComb to detect gene pairs that are **co-disrupted (co-occurring)** by rare damaging variants, showing that these pairs are enriched in **cytoskeleton pathways**, are co-expressed in neural progenitor cells, and are associated in particular with **increased symptom severity in male autism patients**.

## Research Summary
Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a highly heritable condition with heritability exceeding 80%, yet large-scale genomic studies have explained only a portion of its genetic component. Research to date has focused mainly on single de novo variants (DNVs), single genes, or the polygenic burden of common variants, but these alone do not fully account for the genetic architecture of ASD. Recent studies have reported that ASD patients tend to carry two or more rare damaging variants, raising the hypothesis that **combinations of multiple rare variants (additive or synergistic effects)**, each individually of modest significance, may modulate disease risk. The authors hypothesized that the **simultaneous occurrence** of rare damaging variants in specific gene pairs would be associated with ASD.

To test this, the authors integrated the Korean ASD cohort (SNUBH·SCHBC), the Simons Simplex Collection (SSC), SPARK, and a Korean general-population cohort (KoGES), analyzing a total of **17,573 WGS + 39,095 WES samples** (59,168 participants across 15,599 families). Focusing on rare inherited variants, ultra-rare variants, and DNVs in coding regions, they filtered for PTVs and damaging missense (damaging MIS) variants. Because at the single-variant level the frequency of rare inherited variants did not differ significantly between cases and controls (with similar results even when restricted to constrained genes), the authors shifted to an approach that considers **co-occurrence** of variants, applying the statistical tool **RareComb / pyrarecomb** independently within each ancestry.

As a result, they identified **6 significant disrupted gene pairs in East Asians (EAS) and 156 in Europeans (EUR)** (with no overlapping pairs between the two ancestries, reflecting the difference in sample sizes), yielding 10 (EAS) and 296 (EUR) candidate genes respectively. These candidate genes showed almost no overlap with DNV genes but were significantly enriched among SFARI genes, and they were under weaker evolutionary constraint (LOEUF) than DNV genes — consistent with a **modest-effect** character in which a single variant alone does not strongly affect survival or function. In GO analysis, like DNV genes the candidate genes were enriched for synaptic transmission, while as a distinctive feature separating them from DNV genes they were enriched in the **cytoskeleton organization** pathway (in both EAS and EUR). In single-cell atlas analysis, the candidate genes were strongly expressed and co-expressed in excitatory neurons and in neural progenitor cells such as radial glia and neuroblasts.

For functional validation, the authors performed single/double knockdown by siRNA of the representative cytoskeleton/cilia-related pair **TIAM2–WDR19** in hTERT-RPE1 cells. Double knockdown inhibited ciliogenesis far more strongly than single knockdown: the proportion of cilia-bearing cells fell from 34.6% in controls to 9.0% under double knockdown, and mean cilium length decreased from 5.69 μm to 2.88 μm. This is consistent with the hypothesis that the two genes **jointly govern cellular function**.

Clinically, carrying co-occurring rare variants was strongly enriched in cases at the individual level (EUR OR 7.65, EAS OR 8.97), and **male patient carriers** had significantly higher total ADOS CSS scores (symptom severity) than non-carriers, whereas this was not observed in females. In addition, **non-affected parents** carrying these variants tended to show elevated autistic traits (e.g., BAPQ rigidity), suggesting effects extending beyond diagnosed individuals. pTDT analysis indicated that patients carrying co-occurring variants may also carry additional polygenic burden. The authors concluded that this study — by using large-scale multi-ancestry data to characterize the **modest effect of rare-variant combinations** undetectable at the individual-variant level — fills an unresolved gap in the genetic architecture of ASD.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Sequencing**: WGS (Korean ASD ~40X, HiSeq X; KoGES ~30X NovaSeq 6000; SSC HiSeq X10; SPARK NovaSeq 6000, PCR-free), WES (Korean ASD ~150X). Aligned to GRCh38.
- **Variant calling pipeline**: DRAGEN Germline Pipeline v4.0.3 (WGS), BWA-mem v0.7.17 (WES), GATK best-practice v4.1.8.1 + VQSR, GLnexus v1.4.1 (SPARK joint genotyping), iterative gVCF joint genotyping.
- **Dataset/cohort scale**: 59,168 total participants / 15,599 families. EAS 1,122 affected · 2,553 unaffected (+ KoGES n=2,500 controls), EUR 16,241 affected · 36,752 unaffected. The RareComb analysis used EAS 1,057 affected / 5,053 unaffected and EUR 14,464 affected / 36,752 unaffected.
- **Quality control**: Hail 0.2, Peddy (sex and relatedness), Somalier (kinship), removal of LCRs, filters including GQ≥20 / DP≥10 / AB 0.2–0.8 (het) / AB≥0.95 (hom) / HWE. Internal AC filters to equalize variant counts across ancestries (Korean AC<18, other EAS AC<5, etc.).
- **DNV calling**: Hail de_novo(), gnomAD v3.1 non-neuro AF<0.01%, cohort-specific quality cutoff optimization.
- **Variant annotation/prioritization**: VEP v110.1 + LOFTEE v1.0.3 + CADD v1.6 + dbNSFP v4.4. PTVs (frameshift, splice, stop_gained, high-confidence LOF); damaging MIS (mean rank >0.7 across 9 scores — REVEL, MetaRNN, PrimateAI, FATHMM, MPC, PROVEAN, VEST4, BayesDel, gMVP — AND CADD phred ≥20).
- **Gene-pair detection**: Python `pyrarecomb` + RareComb statistical model (association rule mining + binomial test). Parameters combo_length=2, min_indv_threshold=5, max_freq_threshold=0.25, pval_filter_threshold=0.2, adj_pval_type='BH'. Conditions: in cases P<0.05, adjusted P<0.2, power>0.7, while non-significant in controls (P>0.2), and bi-parental inheritance ≥60%.
- **Robustness validation**: single-gene burden binomial test, Kerner et al.–style logistic regression + likelihood-ratio test (LRT) post-hoc (covariates: sex, cohort, sequencing method, PC10, quality metrics), label-swapping–based simulation test (10,000 iterations, empirical P), simplex quad-only family-based re-analysis.
- **Cross-ancestry evaluation**: Poisson-standardized effect size (rate-ratio difference / pooled SE) comparison, linear-regression correlation.
- **Polygenic score (PS)**: PRScs (v11-03-2019), iPSYCH ASD GWAS (58,948 individuals) summary statistics, HapMap3 reference, GRCh38→GRCh37 conversion. **pTDT** (polygenic transmission disequilibrium test) + ANCOVA adjustment.
- **Expression/cell-type analysis**: Kim et al. human brain-development single-cell atlas, pseudobulk (Leiden cluster) log-normalized means, Pearson correlation, Ward.D2 hierarchical clustering (3 clusters, gap statistic), empirical null (10,000-iteration bootstrap).
- **GO/disease genes**: clusterProfiler (v4.10.1, enrichGO, hypergeometric), Fisher's exact gene-set enrichment, BH correction. Disease gene sets: Fu et al. (DNV/NDD), SFARI, Kaplanis (DDD), Akcimen (ALS), Jin (CHD), Werling (MDD, MS, EA, ADHD), Kim et al. (ABM, AD, ANX, BP, EP, PD, RTE, SCZ, VBI).
- **Functional experiments**: siRNA of TIAM2 and WDR19 (single/double, 20 nM total) in hTERT-RPE1 cells via Lipofectamine RNAiMAX transfection → serum starvation (0.2% FBS, 48 h) to induce primary cilia → immunofluorescence (ARL13B for cilia, γ-tubulin for basal body, acetyl-α-tubulin) imaged on a Leica TCS SP8 confocal microscope → GraphPad Prism 8, one-way ANOVA + Tukey.

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **ANK2** (Ankyrin-B): maintains neuronal cytoskeleton and localizes Kv7.2/KCNQ2 channels at the axon initial segment (AIS); high-confidence ASD gene; central to cytoskeleton-related candidate pairs.
- **KCNQ2 / KCNQ3**: potassium channels regulating neuronal excitability; KCNQ3 has prior ASD association.
- **TIAM2** (STEF): Rac1-activating GEF that promotes neuronal migration and neurite outgrowth; one arm of the representative validated pair.
- **WDR19** (IFT144): IFT-A complex component essential for cilium formation/maintenance and neural progenitor polarity; variants associated with global developmental delay and intellectual disability; validated via TIAM2–WDR19 double knockdown impairing ciliogenesis.
- **CPLANE1**: CPLANE complex; recruits IFT-A proteins to the ciliary base; loss causes Joubert syndrome (a neurodevelopmental disorder); highlighted in the CPLANE1–IFT122 pair.
- **IFT122**: IFT-A complex component; paired with CPLANE1.
- **BBX**: physically interacts with ANK2 (reported by crosslinking mass spectrometry); the BBX–ANK2 pair.
- **TTN, SYNE1**: candidate genes with very long CDS (in the context of the long-gene-bias discussion).
- **GRIN2B**: significant at FDR<0.2 in the EUR single-gene burden analysis but not included in any gene pair.

## Main Findings
- At the single rare-inherited-variant level, case and control frequencies were similar (in both EAS and EUR). By contrast, DNVs showed strong enrichment in constrained genes (EUR PTV OR 8.29, damaging MIS OR 2.42).
- Disrupted gene pairs: **6 in EAS, 156 in EUR** (no overlap). Candidate genes: 10 in EAS / 296 in EUR.
- Candidate genes barely overlapped DNV genes (EAS 0/10, EUR 2/296) but were significantly enriched among SFARI genes (EUR 30/296, P=2.4×10⁻⁵).
- LOEUF: candidate genes are more constrained than other protein-coding genes (EAS median 0.573, EUR median 0.824) but less constrained than DNV genes (median 0.144) → a **modest-effect** characteristic.
- **Cytoskeleton organization** is a candidate-gene-specific enriched pathway (GO:0003774 cytoskeletal motor activity, GO:0008092 cytoskeletal protein binding; including ANK2 and KCNQ2). This feature is absent from DNV genes.
- Cell types: both candidate and DNV genes are enriched in excitatory neurons, as well as in oligodendrocyte precursor cells and astrocytes. Of 162 pairs, 97 showed significant positive co-expression in at least one cell type (centered on radial glia and neuroblasts). Cluster 2 was enriched for GO terms related to neurites, synapses, polarity, microtubules, and cilia.
- Higher-order combinations: 66 gene triples identified in EUR (none in EAS). The 104 triple genes significantly overlapped the gene-pair genes (OR 23.38).
- **TIAM2–WDR19 double knockdown**: proportion of ciliated cells 34.6%±3.7% → 9.0%±3.9% (dual, adjusted P=3.20×10⁻³), mean cilium length 5.69±0.15 μm → 2.88±0.37 μm (adjusted P=4.26×10⁻²). Clearly stronger inhibition than single knockdown.
- Individual-level carrier rates: EAS cases 33/1,122 (2.9%) vs non-cases 17/5,053 (0.3%) → **OR 8.97, P=9.8×10⁻¹⁴**; EUR cases 968/16,241 (6.0%) vs non-cases 302/36,752 (0.8%) → **OR 7.65, P=1.6×10⁻²⁵¹**.
- **Sex-specific phenotype**: male patient carriers had elevated total ADOS CSS scores (OR 1.14, P=2.2×10⁻²; nominal), with none in females. Direct male-vs-female carrier comparison OR 1.40 (P=1.9×10⁻²). Even among males, only bi-parental carriers showed elevated severity (OR 1.15).
- **Non-affected parents**: parents carrying co-occurring variants showed elevated BAPQ rigidity (OR 1.69, P=4.7×10⁻²; nominal). PS was positively associated with parental BAPQ overall, aloofness, pragmatic, and SRS.
- **pTDT**: cases showed significantly elevated pTDT deviation (particularly uni-parental carriers mean 0.165, adjusted P=4.8×10⁻²) → co-occurring variants may increase genetic burden together with polygenic burden. Supports a multifactorial model.

## Applications
- Provides a case example of applying a statistical framework (RareComb-based) that detects individually non-significant **rare-variant combinations** to large-scale multi-ancestry ASD data.
- Presents multi-layer genetic–expression–functional evidence that cytoskeleton/cilia pathways are a **convergent biological pathway** in ASD → a basis for follow-up functional studies and target discovery.
- Associates co-occurring-variant carrier status with the clinical severity (ADOS) of male patients → suggests candidate variant combinations relevant to genotype-phenotype stratification and prognosis.
- Links variant carriage to autistic traits in undiagnosed parents → usable in broad autism phenotype (BAP) research.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- The EAS sample is smaller than the EUR sample, limiting statistical power → expanding to more diverse ancestries and larger cohorts could clarify ancestry-specific/shared candidate genes and the role of rare inherited variants.
- Because RareComb's pre-mining strategy (requiring at least 5 co-occurrences) introduces a **long-gene bias**, detecting short-gene pairs will require much larger cohorts.
- Extension to higher-order variant combination analysis (triples and beyond).
- Follow-up functional studies of the specific roles of cytoskeleton-related genes in neurodevelopment and function (explicitly proposed by the authors).
- Development toward integrated risk prediction under a multifactorial model (co-occurring rare variants + polygenic common variants).

## Disease Relevance
- Characterizes the contribution of **rare-variant combinations undetectable at the individual-variant level** to the genetic architecture of ASD, filling an unresolved gap.
- Suggests that cytoskeleton/cilia pathway disruption may contribute to ASD pathogenesis by affecting early brain development (control of neural progenitor cell morphology, migration, and polarity). Pairs such as TIAM2–WDR19 and CPLANE1–IFT122 also connect to neurodevelopmental disorders such as Joubert syndrome and developmental delay.
- Candidate genes frequently overlapped congenital heart disease (CHD) genes → consistent with an ASD-CHD interface (mediated by primary cilia and microtubules).
- The male-biased association with clinical severity contributes to the discussion of sex differences in ASD (e.g., the female protective effect), though the authors explicitly note interpretive caution regarding possible male over-representation in the sample.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Whereas prior studies focused mainly on DNVs, single genes, targeted regions, or polygenic burden, this study **systematically identifies gene pairs disrupted by rare-variant co-occurrence in large-scale WES/WGS across multiple ancestries (EAS + EUR)**.
- Discovers that candidate genes show a **cytoskeleton-pathway-specific enrichment** distinct from DNV genes → proposes that they represent a genetic category different from existing ASD gene sets.
- Combines **multi-layer validation** from genomic statistics (RareComb) → expression (single-cell co-expression) → in vitro function (ciliogenesis validation) → clinical (ADOS, BAPQ, pTDT).
- Provides multi-ancestry evidence by integrating East Asian data including a Korean ASD cohort (addressing the limitation that most resources are EUR-centric).
