# Chronic ultraviolet irradiation induces memory deficits via dysregulation of the dopamine pathway

## One-Line Summary
A mouse-model study demonstrating that chronic UV irradiation raises dopamine levels in skin, adrenal gland, and brain (prefrontal cortex and hypothalamus), and that this elevated dopamine impairs hippocampus-dependent memory, synaptic plasticity (LTP), and adult neurogenesis through **dopamine D1 receptor (D1R)** signaling, with the damage reversed by the D1R antagonist SCH23390.

## Research Summary
UV radiation is encountered transiently or chronically in daily life, and accumulating evidence indicates that it affects not only cutaneous responses such as skin inflammation, photoaging, and immunosuppression, but also brain function via the circulation. Prior studies had shown that some mediators, such as β-endorphin, are involved in UV-induced neurobehavioral changes, but **the specific mechanism of which neurotransmitter mediates these changes had not been elucidated.** In particular, dopamine had been reported to increase in the skin immediately after UV exposure and is involved in diverse brain functions including reward, motivation, and memory; however, the changes in circulating and central dopamine upon chronic UV exposure and their neurobehavioral consequences remained unknown.

To fill this gap, the investigators irradiated 6-week-old female SKH-1 hairless mice with **a total of 3240 mJ/cm² UV (275–320 nm, with UVC blocked by a filter), three times per week for 6 weeks**, and quantified hippocampus-dependent cognitive function, synaptic plasticity, and adult neurogenesis. The UV-irradiated group showed significant memory deficits in object place recognition (OPR), novel object recognition (NOR), and Y-maze alternation behavior; **long-term potentiation (LTP) in the hippocampal CA3–CA1 Schaffer collateral pathway was significantly impaired**; and DCX⁺ and Ki-67⁺ cells (markers of neurogenesis and proliferation) in the hippocampal dentate gyrus (DG) were reduced.

To identify the mechanism, LC-MS/MS-based screening of 28 serum neuropeptides and catecholamines revealed that **dopamine was the most significantly increased substance (approximately 1.44-fold, ~130–145%, P=0.0007)**. Dopamine was also increased in the **skin and adrenal gland** of UV-irradiated mice. In region-specific quantification of the brain, there was no significant change in the VTA, substantia nigra (SN), or hippocampus (HPC), whereas **dopamine was significantly increased in the prefrontal cortex (PFC) and hypothalamus (HT)**.

To verify whether the elevated dopamine was actually the cause of the memory impairment, UV-irradiated mice received intraperitoneal injection of the **dopamine D1 receptor antagonist SCH23390 (0.1 mg/kg)** or the **D2 receptor antagonist raclopride (1 mg/kg)**. SCH23390 significantly rescued the memory deficits in OPR and Y-maze, the LTP impairment, and the reduction of DCX⁺ neurogenesis, whereas **raclopride had no rescue effect.** That is, UV-induced memory impairment occurs primarily through **D1R signaling**. RNA-seq transcriptomic analysis likewise showed that the differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in the UV group were significantly enriched in the **dopaminergic neuron differentiation and dopamine biosynthesis** pathways (key genes Pax5, Foxa2, En1), and SCH23390 treatment suppressed these pathways. Finally, **repeated intraperitoneal injection of peripheral dopamine for 6 weeks (5, 10 mg/kg) reproduced hippocampal memory decline and reduced neurogenesis even without UV**, demonstrating that the elevation of circulating dopamine itself induces cognitive impairment. In conclusion, the study proposes a causal axis of chronic UV exposure → elevated dopamine in skin/adrenal/brain → D1R signaling activation → decreased neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity → memory deficits, and emphasizes the importance of UV protection and the potential neuroprotective value of dopamine receptor-targeting drugs.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Animal model**: 6-week-old female SKH-1 hairless mice (Orient Bio), randomized into 2 groups, approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (No. 20-0271)
- **UV irradiation**: Philips TL20W/12RS lamp (275–320 nm), UVC (<290 nm) blocked with a Kodacel filter, intensity measured with a UV meter (Model 585100). Three times per week for 6 weeks, total 3240 mJ/cm². Eyes shielded and mice anesthetized during irradiation; the control group received sham irradiation after anesthesia
- **Behavioral experiments**: OPR (object place recognition, long-term memory), NOR (novel object recognition), Y-maze (spontaneous alternation) — blinded evaluators, video analysis. Discrimination index = (N−F)/(N+F)
- **Synaptic plasticity (electrophysiology)**: extracellular fEPSP recording in hippocampal slices (CA3–CA1 Schaffer collateral), LTP induced by theta burst stimulation (4 bursts, 4 pulses each, 100 Hz, 200 ms interval), WinLTP software
- **Immunohistochemistry (IHC)**: free-floating technique, primary antibodies DCX (doublecortin, ab18723) and Ki-67 (ab15580), Alexa Fluor 594 secondary antibody, X-CLARITY mounting, DG quantification with confocal microscopy (Nikon A1Rsi)
- **Metabolomic/neuropeptide analysis**: ultra-high-performance liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (UHPLC-MS/MS, ExionLC + 6500+ QTRAP, ESI), Waters Acquity HSS T3 column, multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) in positive and negative ion modes. Screening of 28 serum neuropeptides/catecholamines plus dopamine quantification in skin, adrenal gland, and brain regions (VTA, SN, HPC, PFC, HT)
- **Drug treatments**: D1R antagonist SCH23390 (0.1, 1 mg/kg IP), D2R antagonist raclopride (1 mg/kg IP), dopamine (dopamine hydrochloride, 1/5/10 mg/kg IP, 5 times per week for 6 weeks)
- **Transcriptomics (RNA-Seq)**: oligo-dT mRNA isolation, TruSeq stranded mRNA library, 2100 Bioanalyzer QC, KAPA quantification, Illumina NovaSeq
- **Bioinformatic analysis**: DEG selection (adjusted P<0.05, |log2FC|>0.1), clusterProfiler (v4.6.2)-based GO (biological process) pathway enrichment analysis, Fisher's exact test + Benjamini–Hochberg correction, enrichment expressed as log2(odds ratio)
- **Statistics**: Mann–Whitney U test, one-way ANOVA (Dunn's post hoc), two-way ANOVA (Sidak's), unpaired t-test, GraphPad Prism 9.5.1

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **Dopamine D1 receptor (D1R, DRD1)** — the key receptor on which elevated dopamine acts; mediator of UV-induced impairment of memory, LTP, and neurogenesis; damage reversed when blocked by the antagonist SCH23390
- **Dopamine D2 receptor (D2R, DRD2)** — blocked by raclopride, but not involved in rescuing UV-induced memory impairment (contrasting negative result)
- **Pax5, Foxa2, En1** — key genes of the dopaminergic neuron differentiation pathway; this pathway is enriched in the UV group and suppressed by SCH23390
- **DCX (doublecortin)** — marker of immature neurons/neuroblasts; reduced in the hippocampal DG of the UV and dopamine-injected groups (decreased neurogenesis)
- **Ki-67** — cell proliferation marker; reduced in the DG of the UV group
- **Sgk1, Mid1, Gdf1** — major differentially expressed genes seen in the UV vs UV-SCH comparison (top of the volcano plot)
- **Dopamine** — central mediating substance (neurotransmitter/catecholamine); elevated in serum, skin, adrenal gland, PFC, and HT

## Main Findings
- **Memory deficits**: the UV-irradiated group had significantly lower discrimination indices in OPR (P=0.0002) and NOR (P=0.0078), and significantly reduced Y-maze alternation rate (P<0.0001)
- **Impaired synaptic plasticity**: in the 6-week UV group, hippocampal CA3–CA1 LTP (fEPSP slope at 51–60 min) was significantly reduced compared to controls (P<0.05)
- **Reduced neurogenesis**: DCX⁺ and Ki-67⁺ cell counts in the hippocampal DG were significantly reduced in the UV group (each P<0.05)
- **Systemic elevation of dopamine**: among the 28 serum substances screened, dopamine showed the greatest increase (fold change 1.44, ~130–145%, P=0.0007). Norepinephrine 1.30, 3-MT 1.19, 5-HIAA 1.18, ACh 1.17, putrescine 1.15, and others also increased
- **Increased dopamine in peripheral organs**: dopamine significantly increased in skin (P<0.05) and adrenal gland (P<0.05)
- **Brain region specificity**: dopamine significantly increased in PFC (P<0.05) and hypothalamus (HT, P<0.01), whereas there was no significant change in VTA, SN, or hippocampus
- **D1R-specific causality**: SCH23390 (D1R antagonist, 0.1 mg/kg) rescued the UV-induced OPR and Y-maze memory deficits, LTP impairment, and reduction of DCX⁺ neurogenesis; raclopride (D2R antagonist) had no rescue effect
- **Transcriptomic evidence**: 70 DEGs in UV vs control, 38 downregulated in UV vs UV-SCH. In GO enrichment, **dopaminergic neuron differentiation and dopamine biosynthesis processes** were top pathways (Pax5, Foxa2, En1); SCH23390 suppressed these pathways
- **Reproduction of the UV phenotype by dopamine injection**: repeated injection of dopamine at 5 and 10 mg/kg dose-dependently raised serum dopamine and reproduced hippocampal memory decline and reduced DCX⁺ neurogenesis without UV (P<0.05 to 0.001)
- **Inverted U-shape relationship proposed**: the relationship between dopamine and cognition is interpreted as an inverted U-shape in which both deficiency and excess impair cognition

## Applications
- **Provides a rationale for UV protection in brain health**: experimentally demonstrates that chronic UV exposure can impair cognitive function beyond the skin, supporting the importance of photoaging prevention and UV protection from a neurological perspective
- **Identifies a pharmacological neuroprotective target**: the dopamine D1 receptor antagonist (SCH23390) reverses UV-induced impairment of memory, synapses, and neurogenesis → a rationale for dopamine receptor-targeted intervention in skin–brain axis-related cognitive decline
- **A model for skin–brain axis research**: through the photoaging mouse model, provides a platform for studying mechanisms by which peripherally generated neurotransmitters regulate central function

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- **Validation for human application**: translation of mouse results to humans, and elucidation of individual variability and safety, is needed (the paper itself notes the need for in-depth follow-up studies)
- **Elucidation of inter-brain-region interactions**: dopamine in the hippocampus itself was not increased, yet memory was impaired → further research is needed on how elevated dopamine in other regions such as the hypothalamus and cortex remotely regulates hippocampal function, including consideration of measurement-sensitivity limits
- **Deeper study of mediating pathways**: exploring how indirect effects of UV, including vitamin D signaling, influence dopamine and other neurotransmitters
- **Drug optimization**: validating the human safety and efficacy of dopamine receptor-targeting drugs such as D1R antagonists

## Disease Relevance
- Dopamine signaling abnormalities are associated with brain disorders such as Parkinson's disease, Alzheimer's disease, and schizophrenia, and excess dopamine signaling suggests a pathophysiology resembling the cognitive impairment of hyperdopaminergic states such as schizophrenia
- The finding that chronic UV exposure can cause cognitive decline and reduced neurogenesis suggests a neurological risk for aging, age-related cognitive decline, and photoaging
- The inverted U-shape relationship between dopamine and cognition emphasizes that both dopamine excess and deficiency can lead to pathology, highlighting the clinical importance of precise dopamine regulation

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Whereas prior work had linked only a few mediators such as β-endorphin, urocanic acid, and nitric oxide to UV-induced neurobehavioral changes, this study is the **first to identify dopamine as the key mediating neurotransmitter of UV-induced memory impairment**
- Quantitatively demonstrates the **simultaneous elevation of peripheral and central dopamine and brain region specificity (increase in PFC and HT, no change in hippocampus)**
- By comparing D1R vs D2R antagonists, **specifies the causal role of D1R signaling**, and by directly injecting dopamine to **reproduce the UV phenotype**, provides dual proof of the causality of circulating dopamine
- Through transcriptomic (RNA-seq) analysis, shows that the **dopaminergic neuron differentiation pathway (Pax5/Foxa2/En1)** responds to UV and drug treatment, linking the mechanism down to the molecular level
