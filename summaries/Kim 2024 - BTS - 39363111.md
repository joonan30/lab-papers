# An integrative single-cell atlas for exploring the cellular and temporal specificity of genes related to neurological disorders during human brain development

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Seoyeon Kim, Jihae Lee (co-first authors), In Gyeong Koh, Jungeun Ji, Hyun Jung Kim, Eunha Kim, Jihwan Park, Jong-Eun Park, **Joon-Yong An (corresponding author)** — affiliations: Korea University and others (corresponding author email joonan30@korea.ac.kr)
- **Journal / Year**: Experimental & Molecular Medicine (EMM) 2024, 56:2271–2282 (Springer Nature, Open Access)
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 39363111 / DOI 10.1038/s12276-024-01328-6 (received 2024-04-09, published 2024-10-03)
- **Article Type**: Original / resource article — construction of an integrative single-cell atlas and analysis of disorder-related genes

## One-Line Summary
A study that built an atlas (BTS, brain transcriptome at the single-cell level) integrating 393,060 single cells of the human brain spanning from 7 gestational weeks (fetal) to 90-year-old adults, and used it to characterize in which cell types and at what developmental time points the risk genes for neurological disorders — autism, developmental delay, epilepsy, Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, glioblastoma, and others — are expressed, thereby elucidating their cellular and temporal specificity.

## Research Summary
The human nervous system develops gradually and in a complex manner over decades, from the embryonic period to adulthood, and during this process countless functionally distinct cell types, circuits, and regions are formed. Although recent single-cell technologies have enabled comprehensive transcriptomic censuses across brain regions, the **cellular specificity and temporal (developmental-stage) specificity** of neurological disorders have remained ambiguous because of variation across the developmental process. Existing brain-atlas studies were limited by insufficient donor diversity and by the absence of a developmental-time dimension.

To fill this gap, the study integrated eight previously published single-cell / single-nucleus RNA-sequencing datasets to build a single-cell atlas of human brain development spanning from early fetal life (7 gestational weeks) to late adulthood (90 years). The atlas comprises 114 postmortem brain tissue samples from a total of 80 donors and includes 393,060 single cells, spanning 11 developmental stages. After batch-effect correction and integration, 41 clusters were obtained, which were annotated into 10 major cell types and 22 cell subtypes based on the Allen Brain Institute classification.

The core approach was to map various sets of neurological-disorder risk genes onto this atlas and to systematically analyze in which cell types and at which developmental stages each disorder's risk genes are specifically expressed. Temporal (pseudotime/trajectory) analysis reconstructed three neuronal lineages differentiating from radial glia into excitatory neurons, MGE-derived inhibitory neurons, and CGE-derived inhibitory neurons, and showed that autism and developmental-delay risk genes are expressed in a stage-specific manner within each lineage. In non-neuronal cells (oligodendrocytes and astrocytes) as well, risk genes for disorders such as Parkinson's disease showed distinct expression peaks along cell-maturation trajectories, presenting a **link between cell maturation and disease risk**.

In addition, by analyzing transcription factor, immune-signaling, hormone, and kinase-mediated regulatory mechanisms, the study characterized the regulatory landscape of early brain development and found that neurological-disorder-related regulatory patterns are enriched in fetal cell types (suggesting an influence of the fetal period on disease determination). For example, MEF2C targets 47 autism risk genes and shows increased expression in the third fetal trimester, illustrating coordinated regulation of autism risk genes. IL17RD expression was elevated in fetal radial glia and neuroblasts and showed a sex difference, suggesting a possible link to sex differences in susceptibility to maternal immune activation (MIA).

In conclusion, this atlas enables unbiased comparison of cell-type–disease associations, provides insight into the dynamic changes of risk genes during development, and suggests that disease determination may be strongly influenced particularly at the prenatal stage. The authors argue that, to understand neurological disorders, studies of **temporal specificity** across brain development should be more strongly emphasized.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Data integration**: Integration of raw count matrices from eight previously published scRNA-seq/snRNA-seq datasets. Includes 80 donors, 114 postmortem brain samples, spanning 7 gestational weeks (early fetal) to 90 years, across 11 developmental stages. A total of 393,060 single cells (the schematic in Fig. 1a is labeled 303,060, which differs from the value stated in the main text).
- **Source datasets**: Allen Brain Map Cell Types Database, European Genome-phenome Archive (EGA), GEO, and the Human Cell Atlas Data Portal. Specifically, Allen Brain Human_M1 10X, Braun et al. (n=19), Cameron et al. (n=3), Hardwick et al. (n=2), Herring et al. (n=24), Morabito et al. (n=7), Nagy et al. (n=17), and Zhu et al. (n=6). From the Braun dataset, 50 samples were randomly selected.
- **Quality control / preprocessing**: Standardization of gene names (NCBI, HGNC); removal of cells with a mitochondrial gene proportion >30% or with fewer than 50 expressed genes; doublet removal with Scrublet (v0.2.1).
- **Normalization / HVG**: Normalization and log transformation with Scanpy (v1.8.2); selection of the top 5000 HVGs per sample.
- **Batch correction / integration**: scvi-tools (v1.0.3), with covariate keys (source dataset, assay, 10x version, etc.) specified to correct batch effects. A nearest-neighbor graph was constructed from the latent representation.
- **Clustering**: Leiden algorithm, resolution 0.6 → 41 clusters.
- **Cell-type annotation**: Consensus annotation based on the original studies' annotations plus Allen Brain Institute marker genes. Cluster-specific differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were derived using the Wilcoxon rank-sum test.
- **Disorder risk-gene enrichment test**: Disorder risk-gene sets derived from large-scale exome/GWAS studies were tested for enrichment against cluster-specific DEGs (FDR<0.05, log2FC>0.2) using a **one-sided Fisher's exact test**, with multiple-comparison correction (Bonferroni).
- **Pseudotime/trajectory analysis**: Palantir (v1.3.0) with diffusion maps to reconstruct lineage-specific pseudotime and trajectories for each cell type; the pseudotime axis was split into thirds (Early/Middle/Late) for stage-wise expression and pathway analysis.
- **Developmental-age expression profiles**: decoupler (v1.5.0.2) for pseudobulk aggregation (minimum 10 cells and 1000 counts per sample/cluster); Loess regression (span 0.4) for age-wise expression trend lines.
- **Gene regulatory network (GRN) / signaling pathways**: SEACells (v0.3.3) to aggregate the 393,060 cells into 5000 meta-cells; pySCENIC (v0.12.1) to infer transcription-factor–target regulatory networks (regulons) and compute their activity. Reactome-database-based hormone, kinase, and immune signaling pathway module scores.
- **Label-transfer model**: A CellTypist model was built (publicly released).

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **SLC17A7 (VGLUT1)**: Excitatory neuron marker.
- **GAD1**: Inhibitory neuron marker.
- **AQP4 / GFAP**: Astrocyte markers (GFAP is also enriched in astrocyte-like glioblastoma).
- **TYROBP**: Microglia marker.
- **OPALIN**: Oligodendrocyte marker.
- **PDGFRA**: Oligodendrocyte precursor cell (OPC) marker.
- **NOSTRIN**: Endothelial cell marker.
- **HES1 / SOX2**: Radial glia markers (HES1 is linked to mesenchymal-like, hypoxia-dependent glioblastoma).
- **NHLH1 / NEUROD6**: Neuroblast markers (NEUROD6 is a late lineage-1 IPC marker).
- **LINC00507 / RORB / FEZF2 / THEMIS**: Excitatory neuron cortical-layer markers (layers 2-3 / 3-5 / 4-6).
- **SST / LHX6 / PVALB**: MGE-derived inhibitory neuron (lineage 2) markers.
- **ADARB2 / VIP / LAMP5**: CGE-derived inhibitory neuron (lineage 3) markers.
- **TCF7L2**: Autism risk gene expressed early in lineage 1; mediated by Wnt signaling.
- **FOXP2**: Shared autism/developmental-delay risk gene; highly expressed late (postnatal) in lineage 1.
- **RNASE1, MYCN, RAB14**: Lineage-1 autism/developmental-delay risk genes.
- **CTCF, SATB1, ARID2, SMAD4, H3-3A, PPP2CA**: Lineage-2 developmental-delay risk genes (coordinated expression at the second-fetal-trimester transition).
- **PHIP, RORA, ATP1A2, FGFR2, ETF1, SON, U2AF2, SMC3, HNRNPU**: Lineage-3 developmental-delay risk genes; **PAX5**: lineage-3 middle-stage autism risk gene.
- **PLPP4 / DNAH17**: Parkinson's disease risk genes (expressed at early / late stages, respectively, in the oligodendrocyte lineage).
- **KAT2B**: Risk gene for anxiety disorder and abnormal brain morphology (early OPC expression).
- **SOX10 / PHLDB1**: Expressed at the oligodendrocyte-differentiation stage (developmental-delay related).
- **EHMT1 / CELF4**: Early lineage-1 astrocyte autism risk gene / peak markers.
- **NES (nestin) / THBS1**: Astrocyte lineage-1 markers (intermediate filament / synaptogenesis).
- **TNC / CD44**: Fibrous astrocyte markers (lineage 3).
- **PIK3CA**: Shared autism + developmental-delay risk gene; peaks at the middle stage of astrocyte lineage 3.
- **MEF2C**: Transcription factor with increased expression in the third fetal trimester; regulates 47 autism risk gene targets.
- **NFIX**: Transcription factor targeting 28 autism risk genes (including DIP2C, CSNK1E).
- **NFIA / TCF12**: Canonical transcription factors of astrocyte and oligodendrocyte differentiation, respectively.
- **HMGB3 / YBX1**: Transcription factors enriched in fetal radial glia and neuroblasts; associated with glioblastoma tumorigenesis; target 11 glioblastoma risk genes (including RPL5, IDH1).
- **GRIN2B, SCN2A, CACNA1D**: Autism risk genes whose expression correlates with MEF2C (r=0.65, 0.64, 0.51 respectively; p<2.2e-16).
- **IL17RD**: Elevated in fetal radial glia and neuroblasts; linked to maternal immune activation (MIA) susceptibility and sex differences.
- **BCAN, DLL3, OLIG1, LRRN1, TCF4**: NPC-like glioblastoma subprogram-1 (OPC/oligodendrocyte) signature.

## Main Findings
- Construction of an integrated human brain-development atlas of 393,060 single cells, 41 clusters, 10 major cell types, and 22 subtypes spanning 7 gestational weeks to 90 years; cluster-specific DEGs significantly overlap with cell-type markers from prior single-cell studies (e.g., early fetal excitatory neuron C12 vs. previously reported EN-fetal-early OR=7.81; C29 OR=6.35, etc.).
- Excitatory neurons were characterized into 11 clusters based on developmental stage and cortical layer, and inhibitory neurons into subtypes by MGE/CGE origin. Radial glia, neuroblasts, microglia, oligodendrocytes, and astrocytes were also subdivided by developmental stage (e.g., microglia C13 predominant in adulthood vs. C39 predominant in the first fetal trimester).
- Neurodevelopmental-disorder (autism, developmental delay, epilepsy) risk genes were enriched mainly in neuronal cell types. Autism and developmental-delay genes were enriched in both excitatory and inhibitory neurons, while epilepsy-related genes were specifically enriched in layer 4–6 excitatory neurons (C36) and SST inhibitory neurons (C6).
- Three neuronal lineages branching from radial glia (lineage 1: excitatory; lineage 2: MGE inhibitory; lineage 3: CGE inhibitory) were reconstructed. Within each lineage, autism and developmental-delay risk genes showed distinct stage-specific expression peaks (e.g., TCF7L2, RNASE1 early; FOXP2 postnatal peak). Significant enrichment of the Wnt signaling pathway (TCF7L2-mediated) in lineage 1 (FDR=4.79×10⁻³).
- Stage-specific expression of disorder risk genes was also confirmed in non-neuronal lineages: in the oligodendrocyte lineage, the Parkinson's disease genes PLPP4 (early OPC) and DNAH17 (late); in the astrocyte lineage, the autism genes EHMT1 (earliest) and PIK3CA (lineage-3 middle stage), among others.
- In early brain development, 633 transcription factors were predicted to be active in at least one cell type. MEF2C targets 47 autism risk genes and shows increased expression in the third fetal trimester, rising in coordination with GRIN2B (r=0.65), SCN2A (r=0.64), and CACNA1D (r=0.51). NFIX targets 28 autism risk genes.
- Immune (IL) signaling pathways were enriched not only in microglia but also in radial glia and neuroblasts. IL17RD was elevated in fetal radial glia and neuroblasts and showed a sex difference (males: onset around gestational day 60, peak at days 112–154; females: rise at days 84–105, peak around day 115.5), linking it to sex differences in MIA susceptibility.
- Cell- and stage-specific mapping of glioblastoma subtypes: astrocyte-like (astrocytes C2, C27; AQP4, GFAP), OPC-like (OPC lineage C9, C32, C38; PLP1, PLLP, BCAS1), NPC-like (fetal neurons / fetal cell types; subprogram 2 overlaps with layer 4–6 excitatory neurons C17, C35), and mesenchymal-like (the hypoxia-independent type to astrocytes and vascular cells, the hypoxia-dependent type to HES1-expressing radial glia subtypes C4, C18).

## Applications
- Serves as a reference resource that provides coordinates for **which cell type and developmental stage** neurological-disorder risk genes act in, enabling interpretation of genetic findings for autism, developmental delay, epilepsy, Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and others in a cellular and temporal context.
- Translates disorder-associated genes derived from GWAS/exome studies into biological context (cell type, developmental stage) for prioritization and mechanistic hypothesis generation.
- By elucidating the regulation of autism risk-gene networks by specific transcription factors such as MEF2C and NFIX, it provides a basis for exploring therapeutic targets centered on upstream regulators.
- The correspondence between glioblastoma cell states (astrocyte/OPC/NPC/mesenchymal-like) and normal developmental cell types supports research on tumor cell-of-origin and therapeutic targets.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Provides a foundation for follow-up studies that test the **influence of the prenatal stage on disease determination** across neurodevelopmental and age-related neurological disorders.
- Limitation: the sample is insufficient to capture the full spectrum of individual donors' developmental trajectories → need to expand samples from periods such as the neonatal and infant stages when synaptic pruning and circuit maturation occur.
- Because the datasets are centered on the cerebral cortex, obtaining samples from diverse brain regions is needed to extend region-specific developmental and disease implications.
- Findings related to maternal immune activation, such as the IL17RD sex difference, can be extended through MIA animal models and functional validation. The CellTypist model and public data enable extension to automatic cell-type annotation and disorder-gene re-analysis of new data.

## Disease Relevance
- **Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)**: Risk genes are expressed in a stage-specific manner in excitatory and inhibitory neuron lineages (FOXP2, TCF7L2, PAX5, EHMT1, PIK3CA, etc.). MEF2C and NFIX coordinately regulate many autism genes.
- **Developmental delay**: Broad enrichment in excitatory and inhibitory neurons, with lineage-specific coordinated expression (especially at the second-fetal-trimester transition), suggesting elevated risk at that stage.
- **Epilepsy**: Specific enrichment in layer 4–6 excitatory neurons and SST inhibitory neurons.
- **Parkinson's disease**: Stage-specific expression of risk genes (PLPP4, DNAH17) in the oligodendrocyte lineage → glial and temporal specificity of disease risk.
- **Alzheimer's disease**: Risk genes are predominantly expressed in microglia (C13).
- **Glioblastoma**: Cell- and stage-specific characterization by subtype; despite being adult-derived, similarity to fetal-stage cell states → insights into cell-of-origin and therapeutic targets.
- **Psychiatric disorders** (anxiety, bipolar, major depression, schizophrenia) and the IL17RD sex difference related to maternal immune activation (MIA), providing cellular and temporal coordinates for a wide range of neuropsychiatric disorders.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Existing brain atlases lacked donor diversity and a developmental-time dimension, whereas this study secures the time dimension with an integrated atlas spanning **11 developmental stages from early fetal life (7 weeks) to 90 years**.
- For neurological disorders, it integrated even previously unreported samples to build a resource covering pre- and postnatal development at a scale of 8 datasets, 80 donors, and 393,060 cells.
- Beyond a simple cell-type census, its novelty lies in mapping various disorder risk-gene sets onto cell types and pseudotime to quantify **both cellular specificity and temporal (developmental) specificity simultaneously**.
- It elucidated the temporal specificity of disease risk not only in neurons but also in non-neuronal (astrocyte and oligodendrocyte) lineages, and integrated the analysis to include transcription-factor, immune, hormone, and kinase regulatory networks (e.g., the MEF2C autism network, the IL17RD MIA sex difference).
