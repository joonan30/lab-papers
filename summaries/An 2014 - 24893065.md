# Towards a molecular characterization of autism spectrum disorders: an exome sequencing and systems approach

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: JY An (first author), AS Cristino, Q Zhao, J Edson, SM Williams, D Ravine, J Wray, VM Marshall, A Hunt, AJO Whitehouse, **C Claudianos (corresponding author, Queensland Brain Institute, The University of Queensland)**
- **Journal / Year**: Translational Psychiatry (2014) 4, e394 / 2014 (published online 3 June 2014)
- **PMID / DOI**: 24893065 / 10.1038/tp.2014.38
- **Article Type**: Original Article — exome sequencing plus systems biology (network) approach study

## One-Line Summary
A study that applied the authors' previously proposed AXAS gene network model to whole-exome sequencing data from an Australian ASD cohort, showing that parentally inherited variants (especially from parents with the broader autism phenotype, BAP) and de novo variants converge onto neurodevelopmental/synaptic protein-interaction networks (particularly the L1CAM pathway).

## Research Summary
Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a highly heritable neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by impairments in social interaction and communication together with restricted and repetitive behaviors, and it affects roughly 1–2% of the population. Massively parallel sequencing (particularly whole-exome sequencing, WES) has revealed the contribution of de novo variants (DNVs) (including the increase in DNVs associated with advanced paternal age), the role of rare inherited variants (IVs) (inherited loss-of-function homozygous variants, X-chromosome variants in males, recessive homozygous variants, biallelic variants, etc.), and the impact of variants in non-coding regions. However, because hundreds of rare variants are involved in ASD, single-gene/single-variant functional studies and reductionist approaches have had difficulty explaining how weak alleles accumulate to cross a disease threshold.

Motivated by this problem, the authors applied their previously published **AXAS gene network model**—a hypothesis model that profiles the functional patterns of the heterogeneous DNA variants overrepresented across four mental-health disorders: ASD, X-linked intellectual disability (XLID), ADHD, and schizophrenia (SZ)—to WES data from an Australian ASD cohort in this study. The aim was to reveal, at the level of individual patients and families, which functional processes/pathways the combinations of loss-of-function variants converge upon, and thereby to test the model's potential as a tool for the molecular characterization of ASD.

For the approach, exome-capture sequencing was performed on 40 families (48 patients, 80 parental controls, 142 individuals in total) recruited from the Western Australian Autism Biological Registry. A stringent variant-filtering pipeline (allele frequency; SIFT and PolyPhen2 functional prediction) was used to select putative causal variants, which were then mapped onto the AXAS protein–protein interaction (PPI) network, and the association with disease was quantified using standardized Z-scores based on the binomial distribution. In addition, inherited variants were analyzed separately according to whether the parent had the broader autism phenotype (BAP; based on an Autism-spectrum Quotient, AQ ≥22), and the paternal-age correlation of de novo variants and the family-level convergence pathways were investigated by functional ontology analysis (ClueGO / GO, KEGG, Reactome).

As key results, the candidate-gene variants of ASD children were significantly associated with the ASD-PPI network (P=0.04) whereas the parental control variants were not, and the Z-score for ASD was higher than for the other disorders. In particular, variants inherited from BAP parents were strongly associated with the ASD-PPI network (P=0.002; P=0.02 on a candidate-gene basis) whereas variants from non-BAP parents were not, suggesting that parents with subtle behavioral deficits (subclinical BAP) may transmit ASD risk to their children. Candidate genes in patients carrying de novo variants were also significantly associated with the ASD-PPI network (P=0.01). Convergence-pathway analysis across the 48 patients showed increased frequency of synaptic-development/neurodevelopmental pathways (long-term potentiation LTP, AMPA receptor trafficking, NMDA receptor activation, etc.), and the most striking finding was that **4 of 48 patients (4/48) converged on the L1CAM interaction pathway (REACTOME:22205)**.

In conclusion, the AXAS model can serve as a tool that fine-maps the combination of inherited and de novo variants in individual ASD families and shows how the largely heterozygous, weak alleles converge onto common biological processes (particularly neurodevelopment and synapse formation, such as the L1CAM pathway). The authors concluded that this result is an important advance in the molecular characterization of ASD, useful for analyzing the etiology of individual patient families and, ultimately, for moving toward targeted therapy and personalized medicine.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Cohort/recruitment**: Western Australian Autism Biological Registry (Telethon Institute for Child Health Research, Perth). DSM-IV-based diagnoses (autistic disorder, Asperger's, PDD-NOS), consensus diagnosis by a multidisciplinary team, and administration of the ADOS-G (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule).
- **Sample size**: 40 families, 142 individuals total (48 patients + 80 parental controls, etc.). Final composition of 37 European-ancestry + 3 Asian-ancestry families; 35 simplex + 5 multiplex families. Among the 48 patients: 43 autistic disorder, 2 Asperger's, 3 PDD-NOS. Among the 80 parents: 23 BAP (6 mothers, 17 fathers), 55 non-BAP (34 mothers, 21 fathers), 2 not assessed.
- **Broader autism phenotype (BAP) assessment**: self-report Autism-spectrum Quotient (AQ); a score ≥22 was classified as subclinical BAP.
- **Exome-capture sequencing**: venous blood draw, 1 µg genomic DNA, **NimbleGen SeqCap version 3** capture, **Illumina HiSeq2000**, paired-end 100 bp, Illumina SBS V3. Targeting an ~62 Mb exome.
- **Sequencing metrics**: mean read depth 57.9×, capture efficiency 71.5%, ~50 Mb covered at ≥20× reads. Mean of 38,686 SNPs and 2,420 indels per individual.
- **Variant identification pipeline**: BWA (Burrows-Wheeler Aligner) mapping to hg19 (human_g1k_v37) → Picard duplicate removal → GATK realignment → **GATK UnifiedGenotyper** SNP/indel calling → multisample calling + **VQSR** (HapMap3, 1000 Genomes, dbSNP training sets). Removal of low-quality genotypes (heterozygous male X-chromosome, female Y-chromosome, read <20, base quality <30, multiply mapped indels, etc.).
- **Annotation/functional prediction**: **ANNOVAR** annotation; allele frequency (MAF) from **1000 Genomes (2012.4)** and **NHLBI-6500si Exomes**; missense functional prediction by **SIFT and PolyPhen2**; frameshift indel prediction by **SIFT Indel tool**.
- **De novo / inherited variant selection**: family-informed phasing using the GATK **phase-by-transmission** module; DNV criteria of Transmission Probability ≥20 (error-call probability ≤1%) + read depth ≥20 + alternate-allele ratio in the parental genotype >10%. Ten randomly selected DNVs were validated by custom-primer **Sanger sequencing**.
- **miRNA variant analysis**: miRBase Release 19; target prediction by **miRanda (v3.3a) and RNAhybrid (v2.1.1)**; SNVs in 3'-UTR target sites detected with Bedtools.
- **Protein–protein interaction (PPI) network**: PPI network based on the authors' **AXAS model**; **Cytoscape** visualization; collection of candidate genes and their first-order interacting proteins; overrepresentation testing across the four disorders (ASD/XLID/ADHD/SZ) networks using **the binomial distribution + standardized Z-scores**.
- **Functional ontology analysis**: **ClueGO version 1.8** (Cytoscape plugin); GO, KEGG, and Reactome databases; Fisher's Exact Test + Bonferroni step-down correction; biological-process ontology used (KEGG disease pathways excluded).

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **AXAS model (network)**: PPI-based hypothesis network model that profiles the variant-overrepresentation patterns of the four disorders ASD/XLID/ADHD/SZ (the core analytic framework of this study).
- **L1CAM pathway–related**: **L1CAM** (L1 cell adhesion molecule; a key hub pathway for neuronal migration, axon guidance, and synapse formation); **NFASC** (neurofascin, a cell adhesion molecule found in 14 families); **DCX** (doublecortin, neuronal migration); **NrCAM** (neuronal cell adhesion molecule); **SPRK2** (serine/threonine protein kinase 2, interacts with VAV2); **VAV2** (guanine nucleotide exchange factor); **ERBB2IP**; **MACF1** (microtubule actin cross-linking factor 1).
- **NPEPPS** (puromycin-sensitive aminopeptidase): found in 33 families, interacts with RPS6KA3, associated with ASD and epileptic encephalopathy.
- **DLG4** (disks large homolog 4, PSD-95): postsynaptic density protein, interacts with ITGA4 (integrin alpha 4), involved in the L1CAM pathway.
- **SCN2A** (voltage-gated sodium channel type II α subunit): de novo variant found; a known ASD candidate gene that interacts with ANK3 (ankyrin 3).
- **Neurexin trans-synaptic complex (NTSC)–related**: **CNTNAP3 / CNTNAP5** (neurexin family), **NLGN** (neuroligin), **SHANK**, **NRXN**, **DLGAP2**, **LPHN2** (latrophilin-2), **SPTAN1** (spectrin alpha, non-erythrocytic 1), **CASK**, **PDZD2**, **DDX24**, **SIPA1L1**, **NXPH3** (neurexophilin 3), **LRRTM4**, **CNTN3 / CNTN4** (contactin 3/4).

## Main Findings
- Mean of 38,686 SNPs and 2,420 indels per individual; of these, 13,919 protein-coding SNPs and 155 coding indels, and 24,767 non-coding SNPs and 2,265 non-coding indels. Exonic SNPs accounted for 36.0% of the total (13,919).
- Within the predicted miRNA target sites in 3'-UTRs (2,624,853 predicted sites in total), there were **9,650 SNVs**.
- Most variants were inherited; **44 de novo variants (DNVs) in total occurred across 28 ASD children** (29 protein-coding, 9 intronic, 4 intergenic, 2 in 3'-UTR). DNV frequency showed a **significant positive correlation with paternal age**.
- Stringent filtering pipeline: 41,535 variants → 4,039 missense/nonsense/frameshift → 633 after removing 1000 Genomes MAF>1% → 594 after removing ESP6500 MAF>1% → 187 SIFT/SIFT-Indel damaging → 125 PolyPhen2 possibly/probably damaging → final **115 genes**.
- Candidate-gene variants of ASD children were significantly associated with the ASD-PPI network (**P=0.04**); parental control variants were not significant. The Z-score for ASD was higher than for the other disorders.
- **Inherited variants from BAP parents were strongly associated with the ASD-PPI network (P=0.002; P=0.02 on a candidate-gene basis)** whereas non-BAP parental variants were not significant → suggesting that parents with a weak (subclinical) behavioral phenotype may be more likely to transmit ASD risk to their children.
- **Candidate genes of patients carrying de novo variants were also significantly associated with the ASD-PPI network (P=0.01)**.
- Convergence-pathway analysis across the 48 ASD cases yielded **116 GO, 33 KEGG, and 94 Reactome** terms. Synaptic-development/neurodevelopmental pathways (long-term potentiation KEGG:04720, AMPA receptor trafficking REACTOME:18307, NMDA receptor activation REACTOME:20563, etc.) increased in frequency.
- **4 of 48 patients (4/48) converged on the L1CAM interaction pathway (REACTOME:22205)** — the most striking association. NFASC (14 families) and NPEPPS (33 families), among others, were recurrently detected.
- Representative pathways in Table 1: neuronal migration (GO:0001764), social behavior (GO:0035176), vocalization behavior (GO:0071625), long-term potentiation (KEGG:04720), neurotrophin signaling (KEGG:04722), AMPA receptor trafficking (REACTOME:18307), axon guidance (REACTOME:18266), NMDA receptor activation and postsynaptic events (REACTOME:20563).

## Applications
- A tool that fine-maps which biological pathways the combination of inherited and de novo variants converge upon at the level of individual ASD families/patients, thereby establishing individual genotype–phenotype linkage hypotheses.
- A family-based risk-profiling approach that quantitatively assesses parental subclinical autism phenotype (BAP) and genetic risk transmission.
- A systems-genetics framework that prioritizes candidate genes/pathways based on **network- and pathway-level convergence** rather than single variants, in a heterogeneous disorder in which many weak heterozygous variants accumulate.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Because WES detects only SNPs and small indels in exonic regions, high-density array genotyping and whole-genome sequencing (WGS) should be used in combination to also encompass **large copy-number variants (CNVs; contributing to ~10% of ASD) and regulatory-sequence variants**.
- To exclude DNVs that may have arisen somatically in blood immune-cell lineages, **parent-of-origin validation using DNA from a second (non-immune) cell type** is needed.
- More specific follow-up studies on non-coding/miRNA target-site variants that may affect miRNA precursor stability and target binding.
- **Expanded family-based genetic screening** to establish the statistical significance of non-BAP parental inherited variants and DNVs.
- Ultimately, extension toward **targeted therapy and personalized medicine** through analysis of each family's unique etiology.

## Disease Relevance
- Provides molecular evidence for interpreting the heterogeneous genetic architecture of ASD not from a single-gene perspective but as **functional convergence onto neurodevelopmental/synaptic pathways**.
- Emphasizes that the **L1CAM interaction pathway** (neuronal migration, axon guidance, synapse formation; NFASC, DCX, NrCAM, SPRK2/VAV2, ERBB2IP, MACF1, etc.) and the **neurexin trans-synaptic complex** are core hubs of ASD pathophysiology — L1CAM inherited variants are also associated with a broad range of neurodevelopmental and mental-health disorders.
- Re-confirms established ASD/epileptic-encephalopathy candidate genes such as SCN2A, DLG4, and NPEPPS at the network level.
- The finding that subclinical-BAP parents transmit genetic risk supports the notion that ASD is inherited as a continuous within-family spectrum.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- The first study to apply and validate the authors' previously proposed **AXAS network model (a hypothesis) on actual cohort WES data**.
- Demonstrates, at the level of individual family pedigrees using PPI networks, the systems-level hypothesis that **combinations of multiple weak heterozygous variants converge onto common functional pathways**, rather than single-variant/single-gene associations.
- The separation of inherited variants into **BAP vs non-BAP parents** to analyze the phenotypic basis of risk transmission, and the fine-mapping of the convergence of inherited and de novo variants onto specific pathways such as the L1CAM pathway, are novel.
