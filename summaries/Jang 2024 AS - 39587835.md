# RNA N⁶-Methyladenosine-Binding Protein YTHDFs Redundantly Attenuate Cancer Immunity by Downregulating IFN-γ Signaling in Gastric Cancer

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Dongjun Jang (first author), Chanwoong Hwa, Seoyeon Kim, Jaeik Oh, Seungjae Shin, Soo-Jin Lee, Jiwon Kim, Sang Eun Lee, Yoojin Yang, Dohee Kim, Seoho Lee, Hae Rim Jung, Yumi Oh, Kyunggon Kim, Hye Seung Lee, **Joon-Yong An (corresponding author, joonan30@korea.ac.kr)**, **Sung-Yup Cho (corresponding author, csybio@snu.ac.kr)**
- **Journal / Year**: Advanced Science, 2025, Vol. 12, Article 2410806 (Wiley-VCH, Open Access). Published online November 25, 2024 (received September 5, 2024; revised October 31, 2024)
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 39587835 / DOI 10.1002/advs.202410806
- **Article Type**: Research Article — a mechanism-focused study integrating experimental work, omics, and patient cohorts

## One-Line Summary
A study showing that the m6A reader protein YTHDF1 (and, redundantly, YTHDF2 and YTHDF3), which is overexpressed in gastric cancer, destabilizes IRF1 mRNA—the master regulator of IFN-γ signaling—in an m6A-dependent manner to suppress anti-cancer immunity, and demonstrating that inhibition of YTHDF/METTL3 restores the immune response, thereby presenting YTHDF/METTL3 as a potential immunotherapy target.

## Research Summary
Gastric cancer (GC) is a major public health problem with high incidence and mortality, especially in East Asia. For advanced gastric cancer, immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs; anti-PD-1/PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4) have been introduced alongside chemotherapy and targeted therapy, but a substantial fraction of patients do not respond, and this resistance remains a major obstacle. One mechanism implicated in resistance is a defect in interferon-γ (IFN-γ) signaling. IFN-γ acts through the JAK–STAT pathway to phosphorylate STAT1 and induce its nuclear translocation, thereby driving the expression of IFN-γ–regulated genes—particularly the master transcriptional regulator IRF1—which increases antigen presentation and immunogenicity. Meanwhile, N⁶-methyladenosine (m6A), the most common chemical modification of RNA, is regulated by writers (METTL3/METTL14/WTAP), erasers (FTO/ALKBH5), and readers (YTHDF1–3, YTHDC1–2, IGF2BP1–3), and is involved in RNA metabolism, immune responses, and carcinogenesis. The authors set out to determine how the m6A reader YTHDF proteins regulate anti-cancer immunity in gastric cancer, particularly IFN-γ responsiveness.

In their approach, the authors (1) analyzed YTHDF1 gene amplification, overexpression, and clinical prognosis across multiple genomic and transcriptomic datasets, including their own gastric cancer cohort (aCGH, 103 pairs), TCGA GC, CCLE, and GEO (GSE27342); (2) performed, in gastric cancer cell lines (SNU638, MKN74, MKN1, etc.), YTHDF1 overexpression experiments with wild-type and m6A-binding-deficient mutant constructs (YTHDF1-MUT; K395A, Y397A), siRNA/CRISPR knockdown, RNA-seq and mass spectrometry–based proteomics, RIP-seq/RIP-qPCR, MeRIP-qPCR, RNA pull-down, mRNA half-life measurement using actinomycin D, and 5-EU nascent RNA experiments; (3) used MC38 mouse colon cancer cells in syngeneic (C57BL/6) and immunodeficient (NSG) tumor models together with IFN-γ blocking antibody experiments; and (4) validated YTHDF1 protein expression and its correlation with HER2 by immunohistochemistry (IHC) in tissues from 400 gastric cancer patients.

As key results, YTHDF1 was overexpressed together with DNA copy number amplification in gastric cancer and was associated with poor overall and progression-free survival. Its expression was negatively correlated with immune-related gene sets (such as IFN-γ response, allograft rejection, and chemokine signaling) and with CD8⁺ T-cell and NK-cell infiltration. At the cellular level, YTHDF1 overexpression had little effect on STAT1 phosphorylation itself but reduced the expression of downstream IFN-γ–induced genes (IRF1, TAP1, TAP2, TAPBP, PD-L1) and diminished IFN-γ–mediated growth inhibition; this effect was absent in the m6A-binding-deficient mutant, indicating m6A-binding dependence. RIP-seq showed that YTHDF1 bound directly to the protein-coding region of IRF1 mRNA via the DRACH (UGGAC) motif and shortened the half-life of IRF1 mRNA, thereby lowering its stability. Combining YTHDF2 and YTHDF3 knockdown with YTHDF1 knockdown further increased the IFN-γ response and IRF1 stability (additive/synergistic effect), confirming functional redundancy among the three YTHDFs. Inhibiting METTL3 with siRNA or the catalytic inhibitor STM2457 reduced m6A on the IRF1 transcript, decreased YTHDF–IRF1 binding, and enhanced the IFN-γ response.

In vivo, Ythdf1-knockdown MC38 cells significantly suppressed tumor growth in immunocompetent C57BL/6 mice and increased intratumoral infiltration of total and activated T cells, helper T cells, cytotoxic T cells, and NK cells, while raising Irf1 protein expression. This anti-tumor effect was partially offset by IFN-γ blocking antibody, supporting dependence on IFN-γ signaling. In addition, in the 400-patient cohort, high YTHDF1 protein expression was significantly associated with female sex, older age, lymph node metastasis, and high HER2 expression; treatment with the HER2 inhibitor lapatinib reduced ERK/AKT phosphorylation together with YTHDF1 protein, suggesting that HER2 signaling positively regulates YTHDF1 expression at the level of protein stability. In conclusion, the authors propose that YTHDF proteins attenuate anti-cancer immunity by suppressing IFN-γ signaling through m6A-dependent destabilization of IRF1 mRNA, and that the YTHDF/METTL3 axis may be a promising therapeutic target for improving immunotherapy responsiveness in gastric cancer.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Patient genomic/transcriptomic analysis**: YTHDF1 copy number, mRNA expression, and survival analyses (Kaplan–Meier, log-rank) across the authors' own gastric cancer cohort aCGH (103 matched normal–tumor pairs; 1M copy number CNV genotyping array, GISTIC 2.0), TCGA GC (n = 410), CCLE (all cell lines n = 916; GC cell lines n = 47), and GEO GSE27342 microarray (80 normal, 80 tumor)
- **Immune cell infiltration estimation**: CIBERSORT (LM22 matrix) applied to TCGA RNA-seq, quantifying proportions of CD8⁺ T cells, activated NK cells, and M0 macrophages; Spearman correlation analysis
- **Cell line models**: Human gastric cancer cell lines MKN1, MKN74, SNU638, SNU668, MKN45, YCC38, mouse colon cancer MC38, and 293FT; YTHDF1 overexpression (FLAG-tagged) and m6A-binding-deficient mutant YTHDF1-MUT (K395A, Y397A) introduced via lentivirus/plasmid
- **Gene silencing**: siRNA transfection (YTHDF2, YTHDF3, METTL3), stable YTHDF1 knockdown using CRISPR/Cas9 sgRNA (sgDF1), with human/mouse sgRNA sequences
- **Omics**: RNA-seq (Illumina NovaSeq, SMARTer Stranded RNA library) and GSEA (Hallmark, KEGG, GO); mass spectrometry–based proteomics (nascent protein labeling with AHA/L-azidohomoalanine, Click Chemistry, LC-MS/MS, Proteome Discoverer 2.2; DEG/DEP analysis)
- **m6A/RNA-binding experiments**: RIP-seq (Magna RIP kit; HOMER motif analysis → DRACH/UGGAC), RIP-qPCR, MeRIP-qPCR (anti-m6A), RNA pull-down (biotin-labeled poly(A)25 / IRF1 probe), Integrative Genomics Viewer visualization
- **RNA stability/translation experiments**: IRF1 mRNA half-life (t½) measurement after actinomycin D treatment, 5-ethynyl uridine (EU) nascent RNA capture (Click-iT), STM2457 (METTL3 catalytic inhibitor) treatment
- **Protein/functional experiments**: Western blot (p-STAT1, STAT1, IRF1, YTHDF1-3, METTL3, HER2, p-ERK/ERK, p-AKT/AKT, etc.), qRT-PCR (SYBR Green, GAPDH normalization), protein stability assays (lapatinib, puromycin), cell proliferation assays (trypan blue, LUNA counter), co-culture (Jurkat T cells + gastric cancer cells, anti-CD3/CD28 stimulation, CD69 activation marker)
- **In vivo models**: MC38 syngeneic (C57BL/6) and immunodeficient (NSG) subcutaneous xenografts, tumor volume/weight measurement, IFN-γ blocking antibody (anti-mouse IFN-γ, 250 µg) experiments, TIL analysis, flow cytometry (CD3/CD4/CD8/Foxp3/NK1.1/CD44/CD62L, etc.), IHC (Ythdf1, Irf1, Cd4, Cd8; QuPath 0.4.4)
- **Patient IHC**: 400 consecutive resected stage II–III gastric cancer tissues from Seoul National University Bundang Hospital (2006–2013) (TMA, anti-YTHDF1, anti-HER2), expression evaluated separately at the tumor center and invasive margin, with chi-square tests
- **Single-cell RNA-seq**: Cell-type-specific YTHDF–IRF1 correlation analysis in a public gastric cancer scRNA-seq dataset of 6 patients (10 samples; 3 primary, 6 metastatic, 1 adjacent normal; MAGIC imputation)
- **Statistics**: unpaired/paired t-test, one-way ANOVA, log-rank, Spearman correlation; GraphPad Prism 8.0, SPSS 22.0

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **YTHDF1 (YTH N6-methyladenosine RNA binding protein F1)**: The central m6A reader of this study. Amplified/overexpressed in gastric cancer; binds directly to IRF1 mRNA to destabilize it, thereby suppressing the IFN-γ response and anti-cancer immunity
- **YTHDF2, YTHDF3**: m6A readers functionally redundant with YTHDF1. Combined knockdown further increases the IFN-γ response and IRF1 stability
- **IRF1 (Interferon Regulatory Factor 1)**: The master transcriptional regulator of IFN-γ signaling. A direct target mRNA of the YTHDFs, downregulated via m6A-dependent degradation
- **STAT1**: Transcription factor of the IFN-γ–JAK–STAT pathway. YTHDF1 regulates events downstream of STAT1 rather than STAT1 phosphorylation itself
- **METTL3 (m6A writer catalytic subunit)**: Deposits m6A on the IRF1 transcript. Its inhibition reduces YTHDF–IRF1 binding and enhances the IFN-γ response
- **IFN-γ (Interferon-gamma)**: A cytokine that increases antigen presentation and immunogenicity; the upstream signal of this study's signaling axis
- **TAP1, TAP2, TAPBP (TAP-binding protein/tapasin)**: IFN-γ–induced genes related to antigen processing, suppressed by YTHDF1
- **PD-L1 (CD274)**: Immune checkpoint ligand, an IFN-γ–induced gene and a YTHDF regulatory target
- **HER2 (ERBB2)**: Gastric cancer target, strongly positively correlated with YTHDF1 protein expression. Regulates YTHDF1 protein stability via the ERK/AKT pathway
- **CD8A / GZMA / GZMB / CD69, etc.**: Cytotoxic T-cell and activation markers. Negatively correlated with YTHDF1; indicators of immune infiltration

## Main Findings
- The YTHDF1 gene has a high amplification/gain rate in gastric cancer (frequent in TCGA GC), and its mRNA and protein are significantly overexpressed relative to normal tissue, with copy number positively correlated with expression
- In TCGA GC, the YTHDF1-high group had significantly worse overall survival (HR 1.33, 95% CI 1.1–1.61, log-rank P = 0.0037) and progression-free survival (HR 1.52, 1.23–1.88, P = 0.00012)
- YTHDF1 expression was negatively correlated with immune gene sets such as IFN-γ response, allograft rejection, and chemokine signaling (Spearman r < −0.3, P < 0.0001, n = 642 genes), negatively correlated with CD8⁺ T cells and activated NK cells (with CD8A, r = −0.14, P = 0.0044), and positively correlated with M0 macrophages
- YTHDF1 overexpression reduced the expression of IFN-γ–induced IRF1/TAP1/TAP2/TAPBP/PD-L1 and IFN-γ–mediated growth inhibition, whereas the m6A-binding-deficient YTHDF1-MUT had no effect → m6A-binding dependent
- Proteomics: YTHDF1 overexpression downregulated 131 proteins and upregulated 80 proteins (DEP); allograft rejection, NK cytotoxicity, and positive regulation of NF-κB pathways were downregulated, while DNA repair– and cell cycle–related pathways were upregulated
- RIP-seq: YTHDF1 bound mainly via the DRACH (UGGAC) motif (P = 1e-143) and to coding genes (89.4%); among the 19 genes in the intersection of RIP-seq peaks, downregulated DEPs, and IFN-γ response genes, IRF1 was included
- YTHDF1 (and YTHDF2, YTHDF3) bound directly to IRF1 mRNA (RIP-qPCR, RNA pull-down), and wild-type YTHDF1 overexpression shortened the IRF1 mRNA half-life (e.g., SNU638 t½ 1.68→1.02 h), while YTHDF1 knockdown extended the half-life; combined YTHDF2/YTHDF3 knockdown further increased stability (MKN1 t½ 1.08→2.35 h)
- Nascent (EU-labeled) IRF1 mRNA levels were unchanged, confirming that YTHDF1 regulates IRF1 mRNA stability (degradation) rather than IRF1 transcription
- METTL3 inhibition (siRNA/STM2457): reduced m6A on the IRF1 transcript, decreased YTHDF–IRF1 binding, stabilized IRF1 mRNA, and enhanced the IFN-γ response and growth inhibition
- In vivo: Ythdf1-knockdown MC38 tumors were significantly suppressed in growth, weight, and volume in immunocompetent mice, with increased intratumoral T-cell, cytotoxic T-cell, and NK-cell infiltration and increased Irf1 IHC expression; IFN-γ blocking antibody partially offset this effect
- Patient IHC (400 patients): 63.5% showed high YTHDF1 expression in the tumor center; high expression was significantly associated with female sex, older age, lymph node metastasis, and high HER2 expression (center P < 0.0001, margin P = 0.003). Lapatinib treatment reduced ERK/AKT phosphorylation and YTHDF1 protein (regulation at the level of protein stability)

## Applications
- **Immunotherapy response prediction biomarker**: Because YTHDF1 (or YTHDF1–3, IRF1, m6A level) expression is linked to the integrity of IFN-γ signaling and ICI responsiveness, it may be used as an indicator for stratifying and predicting prognosis in gastric cancer patients
- **Therapeutic target**: Targeting YTHDF proteins or METTL3 (e.g., small-molecule inhibitors such as STM2457) to stabilize IRF1 and restore the IFN-γ response and antigen presentation, presenting a strategy to enhance immunotherapy efficacy in combination with immune checkpoint inhibitors
- **Link with HER2-positive gastric cancer**: The HER2–YTHDF1 correlation and the reduction of YTHDF1 by lapatinib suggest a point of contact between HER2-targeted therapy (e.g., trastuzumab, lapatinib) and immune modulation

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- The precise molecular function of each YTHDF protein (YTHDF1/2/3) may vary with m6A position, stoichiometry, and neighboring RNA-binding proteins, so paralog- and context-specific functional characterization is needed (noted by the authors)
- The hypothesis that HER2 activity increases YTHDF1 protein stability in a phosphorylation-dependent manner requires additional experimental validation (noted by the authors)
- Can be extended to preclinical/clinical validation of the efficacy of combining YTHDF/METTL3 inhibitors with immune checkpoint inhibitors
- Generalization to IFN-γ response targets other than IRF1 and to other cancer types (this study confirmed cross-species reproducibility in human gastric cancer cells and mouse colon cancer MC38)

## Disease Relevance
The study presents a novel mechanism explaining IFN-γ signaling defects—one axis of immunotherapy resistance in gastric cancer—from the perspective of m6A post-transcriptional regulation. Because YTHDF overexpression lowers antigen presentation and immunogenicity through IRF1 degradation and suppresses CD8⁺ T-cell and NK-cell infiltration, thereby creating a "cold tumor" microenvironment, targeting this axis is a potential therapeutic strategy to restore immunogenicity and improve ICI responsiveness. In addition, its association with HER2-positive gastric cancer carries clinical implications bridging targeted therapy and immunotherapy.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Previous reports suggested that YTHDF2 is mainly involved in mRNA degradation and YTHDF1/3 in promoting translation (with conflicting views), or proposed that in mouse colon cancer, Mettl3/14–Ythdf2 degrades Stat1/Irf1 mRNA to regulate immunity. This study (1) reproduces the phenomenon in **human gastric cancer cells**; (2) demonstrates that YTHDF-mediated regulation occurs primarily at the level of **IRF1 mRNA stability** rather than STAT1; (3) reveals the **functional redundancy of not only YTHDF1 but also YTHDF2 and YTHDF3** and the additive/synergistic effect of combined knockdown; (4) establishes IRF1 as a **direct target** of the YTHDFs by RIP-seq/RIP-qPCR/pull-down; and (5) links the HER2–YTHDF1 protein stability axis in a patient cohort—these constitute the novelty
