# Short tandem repeat expansions in cortical layer-specific genes implicate in phenotypic severity and adaptability of autism spectrum disorder

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Jae Hyun Kim (first author) and multiple co-authors; **corresponding authors**: Hee Jeong Yoo (hjyoo@snu.ac.kr) and Joon-Yong An (joonan30@korea.ac.kr). A paper from the An Lab (Korea University, corresponding author Joon-Yong An).
- **Journal / Year**: Psychiatry and Clinical Neurosciences (PCN) 78: 405–415, 2024
- **PMID / DOI**: 38751214 / 10.1111/pcn.13676
- **Article Type**: Regular Article (an original genomic association / resource-oriented study)

## One-Line Summary
By evaluating 12,929 short tandem repeat (STR) loci in whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data from 634 Korean autism spectrum disorder (ASD) families, this study is the first (in a non-European population) to show that rare STR expansions occurring in early cortical layer-specific genes and regulatory elements are associated with ASD and, by disrupting regulatory activity, increase symptom severity and reduce adaptive ability.

## Research Summary
Short tandem repeats (STRs) are highly mutable repetitive DNA in which a 1–6 bp repeat unit recurs; they make up about 6.8% of the human genome and contribute to phenotypic variation by mediating regulatory functions such as gene expression and DNA methylation. Greatly expanded STRs cause a range of neurodevelopmental disorders through mechanisms such as gene silencing and repeat-associated non-AUG (RAN) translation. Recent WGS-based studies have reported STR expansions in neurodevelopmental disorders including ASD and schizophrenia (e.g., Trost et al. 2020, Annear et al. 2022), but prior work was largely confined to European cohorts, and the role of STRs in ASD remained largely unexplored.

This study used high-depth WGS data from 634 Korean ASD families (2,104 individuals in total; 641 ASD probands, 195 unaffected sibling controls, 634 fathers, and 634 mothers) to evaluate 12,929 STR loci across the genome. Known and novel STR expansions were profiled with STRling, and DBSCAN-based outlier analysis was used to identify rare STR expansions present in only a few samples. In addition, the authors integrated single-nucleus RNA-seq (snRNA-Seq) and ATAC-seq data of the prefrontal cortex from postmortem brain (Herring et al.) to characterize how genes carrying STR expansions are expressed and regulated across developmental stages and cortical cell types, and they used deep-learning models (JARVIS, SpliceAI, Sei) to predict the functional impact of the expansions.

Critically, in ASD probands rare STR expansions exceeded normal repeat lengths and occurred preferentially in excitatory-neuron marker genes and cis-regulatory elements (CREs) of early developmental cortical layer-specific cell types (particularly layer 2/3 CUX2 excitatory neurons, layer 4, and layer 5/6 TLE4). These expansions were located in regions sensitive to gene regulation (highly constrained, evolutionarily conserved, haploinsufficient), and deep-learning predictions indicated that they perturb the regulatory activity of enhancers and promoters. In burden analysis, ASD probands carried more rare STR expansions than control siblings in genic regions and protein-coding genes (consistent with findings from European cohorts).

Clinically, individuals carrying expansions in cortical layer 4-specific CREs had significantly higher Social Responsiveness Scale (SRS) scores (more severe symptoms) and lower Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales (VABS) scores (reduced adaptability). This phenotypic association was observed only for STR expansions and was not seen for well-known risk factors such as de novo protein-truncating variants (PTVs) or dnDIS. The authors conclude that STR expansions in cortical layer-specific genes may be a genetic risk factor for ASD and a novel genetic factor explaining the clinical heterogeneity of ASD (severity and adaptability). This is the first large-scale study to present an association between STR expansions and ASD in a previously neglected (non-European) population.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Sequencing / dataset**: WGS of 634 Korean ASD families (2,104 individuals total; 641 ASD, 195 control siblings, 634 fathers, 634 mothers). TruSeq DNA Prep Kit, Illumina HiSeq X. 1,846 samples processed by Macrogen (batch 1) and 258 samples processed by Theragen Bio (batch 2). Aligned to GRCh38 with BWA-mem. Because of a batch effect, downstream analyses used only batch 1 (n=1,846).
- **STR detection**: **STRling (v0.5.1)** — k-mer-based detection of known and novel STR expansions, estimation of STR loci from well-mapped read mates, and estimation of allele size (repeat count) via a linear model. **Hail** was used for QC (excluding read depth <5 or >100, excluding loci with fewer than 2 supporting reads, and excluding homopolymer 1 bp STRs).
- **Rare STR expansion (outlier) analysis**: scikit-learn **DBSCAN** (density-based clustering). Normalized count Cnorm = Craw × 40/D. Parameters eps = 4×σ, min_samples = log2N. Loci tagged as noise were selected as outlier expansions. PCA / PC loading analysis was used to check for batch effects.
- **De novo variants**: Hail 'de_novo' function (gnomAD v3.1 non-neuro subset MAF <0.1% filter). Damaging dnDIS were prioritized as LoF in genes with LOEUF <0.37 or missense with MPC ≥2.
- **Burden analysis**: logistic regression logit(y) = β0 + β1·Nexp + β2·dnDIS + β3·Sex. Fisher's exact test (FET), permutation testing, and Wilcoxon rank-sum test were used in combination.
- **Gene sets**: Fu et al. ASD risk genes (185 high-confidence ASD, 664 neurodevelopmental), **SFARI Gene DB**, developmental delay genes, haploinsufficient genes (LOEUF <0.37, 3,152 total), and 1,100 Gene Ontology sets (Bonferroni correction).
- **Brain single-cell integration**: Herring et al. human prefrontal cortex **snRNA-Seq / snATAC-Seq**. Three developmental stages — early (gestational week 22 to postnatal day 301), childhood (1–8 years), and late (10–40 years). Gene / genomic-region annotation with ANNOVAR. cis-regulatory elements (CREs) were defined per cell type and developmental stage from snATAC-Seq / snRNA-Seq. Cell-type-specific highly differentially expressed genes (FC >10%, FDR <0.05) were also used.
- **Conservation / constraint / splicing prediction**: **JARVIS** (deep-learning sequence-conservation prediction), 100-way **phastCons**, **SpliceAI** (splicing-alteration Δ score; aberrant splicing at Δ >0.2), and the gene-level constraint metric **LOEUF**.
- **Regulatory-activity prediction (deep learning)**: **Sei model** — computes variant effect scores at the sequence-class level; |score| >2 was defined as regulatory-activity disruption. Positive/negative effects were used to judge the direction (increase/decrease) of regulatory activity for enhancers, promoters, etc.
- **Validation experiments**: For known pathogenic loci (ATXN3, DMPK), repeat sizes were validated by microsatellite marker analysis, **RP-PCR**, and **Nanopore long-read sequencing**.
- **Clinical assessment tools**: ADOS, ADI-R, **SRS** (Social Responsiveness Scale), **VABS** (Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales), Leiter IQ, FSIQ.

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **ATXN3**: CAG (glutamine) repeat within exon 10; expansion causes spinocerebellar ataxia type 3 (SCA3). A 63-repeat expansion was observed in 2 ASD children (with accompanying language delay).
- **DMPK**: CTG (AGC) repeat in the 3′ UTR; expansion causes myotonic dystrophy type 1. Paternally inherited expansions of >100 repeats (127 and 143 repeats) were found in an ASD proband and sibling, with language delay and myotonic dystrophy phenotypes. Associated with ASD.
- **CDH13**: Related to cell adhesion; increased burden of intronic outlier expansions. Associated with ASD, ADHD, and depression.
- **TENM2**: Increased burden of intronic expansions. Associated with ADHD and depression.
- **OPCML**: Increased burden of intronic expansions (P=0.043). Associated with schizophrenia.
- **STAG1**: Cohesin component; an early L2/3-CUX2-specific "microtubule-based process" (GO:0007017) gene with 9 intronic STR expansions in ASD cases. Related to intellectual disability.
- **RFX3**: Transcription factor; an early L2/3-CUX2-specific gene with 1 intronic STR expansion in ASD subjects. Related to ASD.
- **NOVA2**: An RNA-binding protein predominantly expressed in brain that regulates alternative splicing of synaptic genes. A nearby gene regulated by the DMPK AGC expansion.
- **SIX5 / DMPK**: A CTCF-binding site (insulator) near the DMPK 3′UTR AGC expansion regulates the DMPK–SIX5 promoter–enhancer interaction; expansion disrupts insulator activity.
- **CUX2**: Marker gene of cortical layer 2/3 (L2/3) excitatory neurons.
- **TLE4**: Marker gene of cortical layer 5/6 (L5/6) excitatory neurons.
- (Examples flagged as regulatory-disrupting expansions by deep learning: **STRN, CASC3, RUNX1** (L4 CRE), **DLG2, C11orf80** (L2/3 genes), etc.)

## Main Findings
- Across 2,104 individuals, **12,929 autosomal STR loci** with 2–6 bp motifs contained at least one expansion. Dinucleotide STRs were most common (61.5%), with representative motifs AT (32.9%), AC (22.1%), and AAAG (19.3%). Most were in intergenic (55.6%) and intronic (35.3%) regions. STRs clustered within 1,000 bp upstream of protein-coding genes (P=0.014, FET) and were sparse in exons / coding sequence (P<2.2×10^-16).
- **Known pathogenic expansions**: Among 641 ASD, 4 loci exceeded pathogenic thresholds. ATXN3 (2 individuals, 63 repeats) and DMPK (2 individuals, 127 and 143 repeats, paternally inherited) — with language-delay and myotonic-dystrophy phenotypes. Validated by RP-PCR and Nanopore.
- **Rare STR expansions**: In 1,846 individuals, **25,535** autosomal rare STR expansions (mean 13.7 per genome). Genome-wide association testing across 555 ASD + 195 siblings found no single locus reaching genome-wide significance (limited by sample size).
- **Gene-level collapsing analysis**: 3 genes — **CDH13, TENM2, OPCML** — showed elevated burden of intronic outlier expansions in ASD (FET P<0.05, permutation P<0.05).
- **Burden analysis**: ASD probands carried more rare STR expansions in genic regions (OR=1.07, P=0.022) and protein-coding genes (OR=1.09, P=0.017). Enrichment in haploinsufficient genes (OR=1.18, P=0.010). ASD-specific expansions were closer to transcription start sites (TSS) (one-sided Wilcoxon P=0.018).
- **Cell-type specificity**: In ASD probands, expansions were enriched in excitatory-neuron marker genes — early **L2/3-CUX2** (OR=1.23, P=1.9×10^-4, adj P=0.006), childhood L2/3-CUX2 and **L5/6-TLE4** (OR=1.18 and 1.19 respectively, P=7.8×10^-4, adj P=0.025). Expansions overlapping **L4-specific CREs** were more frequent in ASD (OR=1.64, P=0.006, adj P=0.13). CRE-overlapping expansions regulated distal genes rather than the nearest gene (40 of 114).
- **Constraint / conservation / splicing**: Enrichment in JARVIS-constrained regions — L2/3 genes (OR=1.81, P=1.8×10^-6), L4 CREs (OR=9.48, P<2.2×10^-16). 100-way phastCons >0.9 conservation — L2/3 (OR=1.60), L4 CREs (OR=4.01, P=1.1×10^-9). Genes carrying early L2/3 expansions had significantly lower LOEUF (Wilcoxon P=3.2×10^-42). SpliceAI flagged 80/860 intronic expansions as potentially causing aberrant splicing.
- **DMPK 3′UTR AGC expansion**: Predicted deleterious by JARVIS, PhastCons, and SpliceAI (1, 1, 0.72). Overlaps early L4-specific CRE, H3K27ac, DNase, and CTCF; regulates DMPK, SIX5, and NOVA2.
- **Deep learning (Sei) regulatory disruption**: Among 204 L4 CRE expansions, ASD showed 10 positive and 14 negative class-specific regulatory activities (vs. 3 positive and 1 negative among 42 in control siblings). ASD carried more regulatory-disrupting expansions (8.8% vs 3.6%; OR=2.60, P=0.017, FET). The disrupted sequence classes were mainly related to multi-tissue enhancers and promoters.
- **Clinical phenotype**: L4 CRE expansions were associated with SRS (β=2.86, P=0.002, n=359) and VABS (β=−3.93, P=0.013, n=441). Carriers had elevated SRS (76.5 vs 71.9, P=4.9×10^-4), with further elevation when carrying multiple expansions (ANOVA P=5.9×10^-3), and reduced VABS (62.7 vs 68.0, P=0.012; P=0.05 when carrying multiple expansions). De novo dnDIS showed no differences in SRS or VABS (P=0.13, 0.96) → the phenotypic association is specific to STR expansions.

## Applications
- Provides a basis for incorporating **STR expansions as a new class of genetic risk factor** in genetic diagnosis and risk prediction of ASD, beyond SNVs, indels, and CNVs.
- Clinically, carrier status for cortical layer-specific (especially L4 CRE) STR expansions could be used as a **genetic indicator (stratification marker) of symptom severity (SRS↑) and reduced adaptability (VABS↓)**.
- The **functional-interpretation pipeline for noncoding / regulatory-element STR expansions**, combining snRNA / snATAC integration with deep-learning regulatory-activity prediction, is transferable to research on other neurodevelopmental disorders.
- Construction of a non-European (Korean) population STR map — a foundation for discovering population-specific STR associations.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- European (Trost et al.) and Korean data need to be processed together within a **harmonized framework** to identify STR associations more comprehensively (currently only limited replication was confirmed in L5/6).
- Discovering genome-wide significance for a single STR locus is estimated to require **at least 10,000 cases and controls** — calling for large-scale collaborative cohorts.
- Expanded statistical and functional validation reflecting the characteristics of rare STR expansions, which have far smaller effect sizes than de novo PTVs.
- Experimental functional validation of the regulatory disruption (insulator / enhancer / splicing) caused by L4 CRE and L2/3 gene expansions (e.g., the DMPK–SIX5–NOVA2 axis).

## Disease Relevance
- Presents a new genetic mechanism explaining the phenotypic heterogeneity of ASD (severity and adaptive ability): STR expansions in early cortical layer-specific genes and regulatory elements → disruption of enhancer / promoter / insulator regulatory activity → disturbed neurodevelopment.
- Known repeat-expansion disorder loci such as ATXN3 (SCA3) and DMPK (myotonic dystrophy type 1) were also present at pathogenic size in ASD children, coexisting with neurodevelopmental phenotypes such as language delay — suggesting a genetic intersection between ASD and other repeat-expansion disorders.
- Expansion burden concentrated in psychiatric-disorder-associated genes such as CDH13 and TENM2 (ASD/ADHD/depression) and OPCML (schizophrenia) — supporting genetic convergence across psychiatric disorders.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- **The first large-scale WGS study to present an association between STR expansions and ASD in a non-European (Korean) population.** Prior STR–ASD studies were mostly centered on European cohorts.
- Goes beyond simple burden analysis by using **snRNA / snATAC integration to assign developmental-stage and cortical-layer cell-type specificity**, and by **quantifying the function (regulatory-activity disruption) of noncoding / regulatory-element expansions with deep learning (JARVIS / SpliceAI / Sei)**.
- Shows contrastively that the **phenotypic (severity / adaptability) association is specific to STR expansions** and does not appear for the well-known de novo PTVs / dnDIS — contributing to explaining clinical heterogeneity despite smaller effect sizes than de novo PTVs.
