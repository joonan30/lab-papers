# Multiomics in cancer biomarker discovery and cancer subtyping

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Seunghwan Choi (first author), Joon-Yong An (corresponding author, e-mail: joonan30@korea.ac.kr) — both affiliated with Korea University
- **Journal / Year**: Advances in Clinical Chemistry, Volume 124 (2025), pp. 161–195 (Chapter Five)
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 39818436 / DOI 10.1016/bs.acc.2024.10.004
- **Article Type**: Review (a review in book-chapter format)

## One-Line Summary
A review that comprehensively synthesizes—together with the accompanying computational methodology—how multiomics (particularly proteogenomics), which integrates next-generation sequencing-based genomics/transcriptomics with mass spectrometry-based proteomics, dissects the molecular heterogeneity of cancer, discovers novel biomarkers and therapeutic targets, and defines clinically meaningful molecular subtypes across 10 cancer types to enable precision oncology.

## Research Summary
Cancer exhibits substantial subtype-specific heterogeneity even within the same tissue type, which necessitates precision oncology that reflects the intrinsic molecular characteristics of a tumor. This review presents **multiomics** as the core methodology for delineating this heterogeneity. Multiomics is an approach that integrates next-generation sequencing (NGS)-based genomics/transcriptomics with mass spectrometry (MS)-based proteomics, to which emerging technologies such as the epigenome (microRNA, DNA methylation), metabolomics, and post-translational modifications (phosphorylation/acetylation proteomics) are added. The authors argue that combining these biologically diverse omics technologies with deep learning approaches is essential for identifying new therapeutic targets in precision oncology.

As background, before multiomics, nucleic acid sequencing-based omics (genomics/transcriptomics) accounted for most high-throughput data. However, several limitations became apparent: (1) RNA expression does not reliably predict protein levels (phenotype); (2) a substantial fraction of significant variants lack targeted therapeutics; (3) even patients carrying the same variant show divergent outcomes due to drug resistance; and (4) protein function is regulated by post-translational modifications such as phosphorylation, glycosylation, and ubiquitination. Against this background, proteogenomics—which examines the proteome together with its post-translational modifications—became important.

On the methodological side, the review addresses three axes. First, **clustering for subtype discovery** compares early integration (which merges multiple omics into one before clustering; e.g., NMF, iCluster, LRAcluster) with late integration (which clusters each dataset individually before integration; e.g., SNF, MONET). Second, **inference of molecular features/activity** introduces computational tools that predict kinase-mediated signaling and phosphorylation sites (GPS 6.0, PhosphoPredict, PHONEMeS). Third, **pathway enrichment analysis (PEA)** covers GSEA and single-sample-level ssGSEA, emphasizing that these are especially useful in proteomics and phosphoproteomics.

As key results, the review organizes case by case how multiomics revealed novel molecular subtypes—invisible to any single omics layer—across **10 cancer types**: endometrial cancer, ovarian cancer, colorectal cancer, renal cancer, breast cancer, pancreatic cancer, glioblastoma, head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, lung adenocarcinoma, and lung squamous cell carcinoma (Table 2). A recurring conclusion is that multiomics further refines subtypes based on conventional single omics (mostly mRNA expression) and reveals hidden features linked to clinical prognosis and drug response (e.g., the Olaparib sensitivity of a PARP1-amplified subtype in endometrial cancer).

In conclusion, the authors summarize that multiomics represents a paradigm shift in cancer research, enabling the delineation of the multifaceted characteristics of tumors that is impossible with a single-omics strategy, thereby opening the way to precision oncology and personalized medicine. Looking ahead, they anticipate that single-cell-level ATAC-seq, methylation sequencing, and m6A RNA methylation will more precisely elucidate the molecular networks of cancer subtypes.

## Methods & Technologies
> (Because this paper is a review, the following are the major technologies/methods/tools that the paper reviewed and organized.)

- **Genomics/Transcriptomics**: NGS (next-generation sequencing), whole-exome sequencing, mRNA expression arrays/sequencing, miRNA sequencing, DNA methylation profiling, tumor mutational burden (TMB), microsatellite instability (MSI), and somatic copy-number alteration (SCNA/CNV) analysis
- **Proteomics**: mass spectrometry (MS), shotgun proteomics (quantitative, untargeted bottom-up, data-dependent acquisition/DDA), liquid chromatography-tandem MS (LC-MS/MS), library-search-based peptide-spectrum match, reference FASTA databases and **customized protein sequence databases** that incorporate genomic variants
- **Post-translational modification omics**: phosphoproteomics, acetylproteomics, glycoproteomics, reverse-phase protein array (RPPA)
- **Clustering (subtype discovery)**: early integration — NMF (non-negative matrix factorization), iCluster/iCluster+, LRAcluster / late integration — SNF (Similarity Network Fusion, a KNN-based patient similarity network), MONET (edge-weighted graph-based module discovery)
- **List of multiomics clustering tools in Table 1**: iCluster (R), iOmicsPASS (C++), SNF (python), NEMO (R/MATLAB), MONET (R/python), NMF (R), LRAcluster (R), MuVI (Python), mixedCCA (R, sparse CCA), MEFISTO (Python), MultiPower (R, sample-size/power estimation)
- **Kinase activity / phosphorylation site prediction**: GPS 6.0 (penalized logistic regression + deep neural network + light gradient boosting; pretrained on 490,762 sites/71,407 proteins, predicting 185 kinases across 44,046 PK), PhosphoPredict (targeting 12 human PK/PK families, including ATM, CDK, MAPK, etc.), PHONEMeS (redesigned with integer linear programming/ILP to improve the efficiency of signaling-network modeling)
- **Pathway enrichment analysis (PEA)**: GSEA (Gene Set Enrichment Analysis), ssGSEA (single-sample GSEA), Gene Ontology (GO) analysis
- **Major consortia / data resources**: TCGA (The Cancer Genome Atlas), CPTAC (Clinical Proteomic Tumor Analysis Consortium), CRCSC (colorectal cancer subtyping consortium)

## Key Genes / Proteins
> (Representative genes/proteins mentioned throughout the review as subtype characteristics of each cancer type)

- **ALK / EML4-ALK**: an oncogenic fusion protein generated by gene rearrangement in a subset of NSCLC; target of ALK inhibitors such as Crizotinib
- **EGFR**: amplification/mutation target of lung adenocarcinoma and glioblastoma subtypes
- **KRAS**: frequently mutated in endometrial (MSI-H), colorectal (CIMP-L), and lung subtypes
- **TP53**: a core mutation of many subtypes, including endometrial CNV-high (serous-like), breast Basal-I, colorectal, and lung adenocarcinoma
- **POLE**: exonuclease-domain mutation of the endometrial ultra-mutated (POLE) subtype; favorable prognosis
- **MLH1 / MSH2 / PMS1**: mismatch repair (MMR) genes, associated with MSI/CIMP phenotypes
- **CTNNB1**: hotspot mutation of endometrial cluster3, an MSS tumor
- **FBXW7, PPP2R1A**: frequent mutations of the endometrial CNV-high subtype
- **PARP1**: increased at mRNA, protein, phosphorylation, and acetylation levels in the endometrial 1q gain subtype; linked to Olaparib (PARP inhibitor) sensitivity
- **PBRM1, CDKN2A, PTEN, BAP1**: characteristic mutations of the clear cell renal cell carcinoma (ccRCC) m1–m4 subtypes
- **PD1**: immune evasion marker of the ccRCC CD8+ inflamed subtype
- **PIK3CA, MAP3K1, MAP2K4**: low mutation frequency in the breast cancer methylation Group3 subtype
- **STK11, KEAP1, NFE2L2, RB1, NF1, SOX2**: subtype-specific genomic mutations in lung adenocarcinoma / lung squamous cell carcinoma
- **IDH1**: associated with the glioblastoma proneural subtype and G-CIMP; favorable prognosis
- **PDGFRA**: amplified in the glioblastoma proneural subtype
- **HNF4A (HNF4a)**: colorectal cancer CMS2/subtype E, associated with HNF amplification
- **BRAF**: frequent mutation of the colorectal cancer CIMP-H / hypermutated subtype
- **Major kinases (mTOR, AKT, ERK, receptor tyrosine kinases)**: increased activity in subtypes such as pancreatic cancer C2 and lung squamous cell carcinoma EMT-enriched — candidate targets for kinase inhibitors

## Main Findings
- It was repeatedly confirmed across 10 cancer types that multiomics-based subtyping **further refines** single-omics (mostly mRNA) subtypes.
- **Endometrial cancer**: integrating CNV, mRNA, protein, and phosphorylation/acetylation yielded cluster1–4. The conventional CNV-low subtype was refined into the immune-active cluster2 and cluster3 (CTNNB1 hotspot, 1q gain, downregulated immune pathways). 1q gain is associated with reduced disease-free survival and significantly enriched protein glycosylation; PARP1-amplified cell lines show increased Olaparib sensitivity.
- **Ovarian cancer (HGSC)**: HGSC accounts for 70% of all ovarian cancers. The four mRNA-based subtypes (immunoreactive/differentiated/proliferative/mesenchymal) show no survival difference. Five proteome-based subtypes revealed a novel **stromal subtype** (ECM, complement, low tumor purity). Three glycoproteome-based IGP subtypes (IGP cluster1 = immunoreactive, cluster3 = complement/coagulation pathways).
- **Colorectal cancer (CRC)**: three major pathological pathways (CIN 70–85%, CIMP, MSI). Five CPTAC proteome subtypes (A–E) and CRCSC's CMS1–4 (consensus molecular subtypes). The metastatic-CRC proteome subtype CC3 is enriched for metabolic pathways and has poor prognosis.
- **Renal cancer (ccRCC, ~75% of all RCC)**: four TCGA mRNA subtypes (m1–4), four immune-based subtypes (CD8+/CD8− inflamed, VEGF/metabolic immune desert), and three NMF subtypes (NMF1 with the worst prognosis). BAP1 mutation and 9p/9q loss are poor-prognosis features.
- **Breast cancer**: five intrinsic subtypes (Luminal A/B, HER2+, basal, normal-like). Four proteogenomic NMF subtypes (LumA-I, LumB-I, HER2-I, Basal-I). It is shown that conventional clinical subtypes such as PAM50 do not capture all biological distinctions.
- **Pancreatic cancer (PDAC, >90% of pancreatic cancers)**: low tumor purity (desmoplastic) hampers molecular characterization. A Korean multiomics study refined it into six subtypes (Sub1–6); Sub4 is enriched for cell-cycle proteins, with high proliferative capacity and poor prognosis.
- **Glioblastoma (GBM, grade IV)**: four expression subtypes (classical/mesenchymal/proneural/neural). The G-CIMP phenotype and proneural+IDH1 mutation confer favorable prognosis. Three CPTAC NMF subtypes (NMF1 = proneural, NMF2 = mesenchymal, NMF3 = classical-like); mixed subtypes have shortened survival.
- **Head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC)**: HPV-negative cases account for ~75% and have poor prognosis. CPTAC proteogenomic integration yields three subtypes (CIN/basal/immune); the increase in epigenome regulators in the CIN subtype was a previously undescribed finding.
- **Lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD)**: three transcriptional subtypes (TRU/PI/PP). Four CPTAC multiomic clusters (C1–C4) show differences in variant profiles by region/ethnicity (C2 Western/TP53·EGFR wild-type, C3 Vietnam/STK11, C4 Chinese women/EGFR·EML4-ALK).
- **Lung squamous cell carcinoma (LSCC)**: five subtypes from SCNA, mRNA, protein, phosphorylation, and acetylation. The basal subtype splits into basal-inclusive and EMT-enriched; EMT-enriched shows receptor tyrosine kinase activation and therapeutic-target potential; the SOX2-amplified group has the worst prognosis.

## Applications
- Patient stratification by cancer subtype to guide targeted-therapy selection and clinical-trial enrollment
- Discovery of novel biomarkers (diagnostic, prognostic, therapeutic-target) missed by single omics
- Subtype-specific therapeutic strategies, e.g., PARP inhibitor (Olaparib) application in PARP1-amplified endometrial cancer, and kinase inhibitor application in EMT-enriched lung squamous cell carcinoma and pancreatic cancer C2
- Signaling-based drug-target prioritization through kinase signaling-network and phosphorylation-site prediction

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- Integrating single-cell-level ATAC sequencing and methylation sequencing to more precisely delineate the molecular networks of cancer subtypes
- Elucidating the post-transcriptional regulatory trajectory of the tumor microenvironment (TME) via m6A RNA methylation
- Broader integration of epigenome datasets
- Accelerating clinical translation through collaboration among scientists, clinicians, and bioinformaticians
- Emphasis on the need to develop sophisticated computational tools capable of handling the complexity of multiomic data

## Disease Relevance
- The target diseases are **cancer broadly** (10 types, including endometrial, ovarian, colorectal, renal, breast, pancreatic, glioblastoma, head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, lung adenocarcinoma, and lung squamous cell carcinoma). The review presents case by case how multiomics subtyping carries direct clinical implications for prognostic prediction, therapeutic-response prediction, and targeted-therapy selection in each cancer type. It is positioned as a key basis for realizing precision oncology and personalized medicine.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- It clearly delineates the limitations of single-omics subtyping (RNA→protein prediction mismatch, failure to capture post-translational modifications, etc.) and systematically organizes how proteogenomics-centered multiomics complements them.
- The novelty lies in integrating, within a single review, the **computational-methodology axis** (clustering—early vs. late integration—kinase activity inference, pathway enrichment) with **real-world multiomics subtyping cases from 10 cancer types**, providing a broad overview of the latest proteogenomic research (TCGA·CPTAC).
- It demonstrates cross-cancer that multiomics repeatedly refines conventional subtypes and discovers clinically meaningful novel subtypes.
