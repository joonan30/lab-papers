# Whole genome sequencing analysis identifies sex differences of familial pattern contributing to phenotypic diversity in autism

## Bibliographic Information
- **Authors**: Soo-Whee Kim and Hyeji Lee (co-first authors), among many others. Corresponding author: **Joon-Yong An (joonan30@korea.ac.kr, Korea University)**. Co-senior (supervising) authors listed as Eunjoon Kim, Donna M. Werling, Hee Jeong Yoo, and Joon-Yong An. Co-authors include international collaborators such as F. Kyle Satterstrom, Santhosh Girirajan, Anders D. Børglum, and Jakob Grove.
- **Journal / Year**: Genome Medicine, 2024, 16:114 (published online September 27, 2024)
- **PMID / DOI**: PMID 39334436 / https://doi.org/10.1186/s13073-024-01385-6
- **Article Type**: Research article — a genomic/phenotypic analysis resource/study based on a large-scale WGS cohort

## One-Line Summary
By comparing the largest East Asian (Korean) autism family WGS and deep-phenotyping dataset against European-ancestry cohorts (SSC, SPARK), this study shows that sex differences in the genetic burden of autism (de novo PTVs and polygenic scores) are modulated by comorbid intellectual disability (ID) and core-symptom severity, and that within families females carry a higher liability threshold for autism.

## Research Summary
Autism shows a pronounced sex bias, with a male-to-female prevalence ratio of about 4:1. This has been explained by the hypothesis that females require a higher genetic liability threshold to manifest autism (the "female protective effect"). Consistent with this, large-scale WGS studies have reported that autistic females carry a greater burden of de novo protein-truncating variants (PTVs) than males, and that among individuals without intellectual disability (ID) females carry higher polygenic scores (PS) than males. However, most of this evidence has been confined to European-ancestry populations (SSC, SPARK, MSSNG, etc.), and the within-family mechanisms by which genetic variation translates into autism-related traits in a sex-dependent manner remain poorly understood.

To address this, the authors present WGS data from Korean autism families (2,255 individuals from 673 families) together with a Korean general-population sample (KoGES, 2,500 individuals). This represents the largest East Asian autism WGS dataset to date, and it is paired with extensively collected family-level phenotype data (on the order of 3,730 individuals). The authors compared these data against European-ancestry cohorts (SSC and SPARK; totaling ~15,839 individuals) to analyze sex differences in genetic burden and phenotypic scores, as well as within-family gene–phenotype associations.

As a key result, the female enrichment of de novo PTVs in autistic individuals was robustly observed across all three cohorts. In contrast, sex differences in polygenic burden varied across cohorts; the authors show that this variability is driven primarily by phenotypic heterogeneity—specifically comorbid ID and the severity of core autism symptoms. After adjusting for FSIQ and SRS, the sex difference in PS disappeared. Furthermore, the male-to-female ratio was reduced in subgroups with comorbid ID or high total symptom severity.

In the family-level analysis, the male siblings of autistic females showed the most severe social-communication deficits. Female siblings, despite carrying a higher polygenic burden than male siblings, showed less severe clinical phenotypes. Mothers likewise showed a higher polygenic burden than fathers yet had milder symptoms. These observations support the existence of a higher liability threshold in females.

In conclusion, the genetic predisposition to autism is dependent on both sex and phenotype, and the combinatorial effect of comorbid ID and total symptom severity shapes the sex differences in genetic burden. The authors propose that family-based assessment of sex differences helps to elucidate the mechanisms of sex-differential liability in autism.

## Methods & Technologies
- **Sequencing**:
 - Korean autism cohort: Illumina HiSeq X, 30x depth, whole-blood DNA. **DRAGEN germline pipeline v4.0.3**, alignment to GRCh38, multi-sample joint genotyping with the iterative gVCF genotyper.
 - KoGES (Korean general population): Illumina NovaSeq 6000, mean 30x, GATK Best Practices v4.2.4.1, VQSR.
 - SSC: Illumina HiSeq X10, GATK Best Practices v3.5, VQSR (removal of low-quality calls with GQ<20, DP<10).
 - SPARK: PCR-free, Illumina NovaSeq 6000, GATK Best Practices v3.5, GLnexus v1.4.1 joint genotyping.
- **Datasets / cohort sizes**:
 - Korean autism WGS: 673 families / 2,255 individuals (696 autistic [590 male, 106 female; ratio 5.6], 213 non-autistic siblings [90 male, 123 female], 1,346 parents). Multiplex families: 21 (3.1%).
 - Korean general population (KoGES): 2,500 individuals (1,272 female, 1,228 male; aged ≥40, from Ansan and Anseong).
 - Korean phenotype data: approximately 1,400–1,499 families / 3,730 individuals.
 - SSC (European ancestry): genetic-burden analysis 1,855 families / 6,976 individuals, de novo analysis 4,318 trios; phenotype 2,644 families / 10,456 individuals.
 - SPARK (European ancestry): genetic burden 2,434 families / 8,863 individuals, de novo WGS 5,683 trios; additionally 25,325 trios from SPARK WES v2; phenotype 108,266 families / 149,547 individuals.
- **Quality control (QC)**: Sample- and variant-level QC using **Hail 0.2** and **Peddy** (ti/tv ratio, genotype quality/depth, relatedness, inferred biological sex and ancestry). Exclusion of low-complexity regions (LCR), splitting of multiallelic sites, retention of DRAGEN hard-filter "PASS." Detailed cutoffs optimized for HQ rare/common variants (QUAL, GQmean, DPmean, AB, Hardy-Weinberg, etc.).
- **De novo variant (DNV) detection**: Hail's built-in **de_novo** function (internal AF<0.001, gnomAD v3.1 AF<0.001). For the Korean cohort, the de novo probability formula was modified to account for partial origin, and the probability threshold was tuned to the characteristics of the DRAGEN GQ distribution. A positive correlation between paternal age and DNV count was confirmed (0.13 DNV per month of paternal age, P<2.2×10⁻¹⁶).
- **Variant annotation**: Hail **vep** function with **Ensembl VEP v109.3**. High-confidence PTVs classified using the **LOFTEE** plugin. Variants classified as PTV / missense (MIS) / synonymous.
- **Polygenic score (PS) calculation**: **PRScs** (primary), plus **SBayesR, LDpred2, and PRSice** (four methods total). GWAS summary statistics from Grove et al. (SSC+iPSYCH, n=46,350) and iPSYCH alone (n=58,948). GRCh38→GRCh37 liftover, HapMap3 European LD reference. Allele frequencies (AF) harmonized for the Korean and KoGES cohorts using chi-square tests. Mean correlation across the four methods was 0.8.
- **Sex-specific gene analysis**: **TADA (Transmitted And De novo Association)** Bayesian algorithm to identify autism-associated genes for females and males separately (de novo PTVs + damaging MIS). **Gene Ontology set enrichment analysis** and pathway-network visualization with **clusterProfiler v4.11.1 (R)**.
- **DNV association testing**: prioritized de novo PTVs in LOEUF<0.37 genes and MPC≥2 MIS. One-sided exact binomial test (burden comparison), Fisher's exact test (comparison of PTV-carrier proportions).
- **Polygenic-burden and phenotype statistics**: two-sample t-test, two-way ANOVA + Tukey multiple comparison, logistic regression (odds ratios). Clinical phenotyping instruments: **ADOS-2** (CSS, SA/RRB), **SRS**, **SCQ**, **ADI-R** (ADIR A social interaction, ADIR B verbal/nonverbal communication, ADIR C repetitive behavior), **WPPSI/WISC/Wechsler adult, Leiter-R** (nonverbal IQ), **VABS-II** (adaptive behavior), **AQ (Autism Quotient), BAPQ** (parental self-report).

## Key Genes / Proteins
This is not a paper that names and finely dissects individual risk genes; rather, it addresses the relationship between genetic "burden" (de novo variants, polygenic scores) and sex/phenotype, so specific gene names are not emphasized in the main text. Instead, the analysis operates at the level of gene sets and functional categories.
- **LOEUF<0.37 constrained genes**: the core targets of the de novo PTV/MIS burden analysis—genes with low tolerance to loss of function.
- **TADA sex-specific gene sets**: 98 female-associated autism genes and 461 male-associated genes (58 overlapping).
- **Functional pathways**: female-specific genes were enriched for **chromatin regulation / histone modification**, while male-specific genes were strongly enriched for **synaptic functions**. Commonly, pathways related to chromatin regulation, histone modification, synaptic function, and cytoskeleton were enriched. However, no pathway group was exclusively confined to female- or male-specific genes.

## Main Findings
- **Robust female enrichment of de novo PTVs (autistic vs. siblings)**: Korea RR=4.3 (P=2.5×10⁻¹¹), SSC RR=3.8 (P=2.3×10⁻¹¹), SPARK RR=2.4 (P=3.4×10⁻⁴).
- **De novo PTVs female>male within autistic individuals**: Korea RR=2.0 (female 0.066 / male 0.033, P=0.11), SSC RR=1.8 (female 0.104 / male 0.058, P=1.1×10⁻²), SPARK RR=1.2 (female 0.052 / male 0.045, P=0.32). Significant only in SSC, but the direction was consistent, suggesting a higher liability threshold in females.
- **Polygenic-burden continuum**: an increasing risk gradient from KoGES adults (0.013 SD) < non-autistic siblings (0.15 SD, P=3.3×10⁻²) < parents (0.16 SD, P=8.6×10⁻⁷) < autistic individuals (0.315 SD, P<0.001). Autistic children had significantly higher PS than KoGES adults (0.32 SD, P=3.74×10⁻¹³) and than siblings (0.31 SD, P=5.2×10⁻⁵).
- **Sex differences in PS were inconsistent across cohorts**: in Korea, autistic males were higher than females (0.23 SD, P=2.3×10⁻²); SSC showed the opposite tendency (0.078 SD, P=0.24); SPARK showed no sex difference (0.002 SD, P=0.97).
- **Effect of comorbid ID**: the ID comorbidity rate in autistic females was higher than in males in all three cohorts. The degree of female enrichment of de novo PTVs correlated with the presence of comorbid ID. SSC had the strongest female enrichment for ID comorbidity (OR=1.9) and the strongest de novo PTV female enrichment, whereas SPARK had the weakest female enrichment for ID (OR=1.3), and its de novo PTV female enrichment was not significant.
- **Total symptom severity vs. polygenic burden**: the polygenic burden of autistic individuals relative to siblings increased with higher total symptom severity (Korea: normal range OR=1.26 → moderate OR=1.30 → severe OR=1.40). It decreased with more severe comorbid ID (lower IQ) (FSIQ≥90 OR=1.53 → no ID OR=1.42 → ID present OR=1.27). Consistent in SSC. **After adjusting for FSIQ and SRS, the sex difference in PS disappeared** → ID and total symptom severity drive the cross-cohort sex differences.
- **Changes in the male-to-female ratio**: the ratio decreased with comorbid ID—Korea 5.3 (no ID) → 4.2 (ID present, OR=1.26), SSC 8.0 → 4.5 (OR=1.79); SPARK was an exception, showing a slight increase (3.2 → 3.6, OR=0.89). The ratio decreased with higher total symptom severity—SSC normal 19.3 → severe 5.4 (OR=3.6). The ratio was lowest in the ID + high-severity combination (Korea 3.3, SSC 4.0).
- **Within-family tolerance of females to polygenic burden**: the male siblings of autistic females (Mₛ-Fc) showed the most severe phenotype—ADI-R social interaction (P=9.6×10⁻³), verbal communication (P=9.4×10⁻⁴). Male siblings scored higher than female siblings on 7 of 9 core symptoms and lower on 5 of 7 cognitive/adaptive measures (i.e., more autistic). Nevertheless, female siblings carried a higher polygenic burden (SSC 0.12 SD, P=1.9×10⁻²; Korea 0.091 SD, P=0.49; SPARK 0.0015 SD).
- **Parental analysis**: fathers scored higher than mothers on 6 of 8 core symptoms (except BAPQ PL and aloof). Both parents had elevated PS relative to the general population (0.16 SD, P=1.6×10⁻⁶). Mothers had higher PS than fathers (Korea 0.05 SD, P=0.34; SSC 0.10 SD, P=2.7×10⁻³). Maternal PS was significantly higher than that of control females (P=3.8×10⁻⁴) and control males (P=1.1×10⁻³).
- **Transferability of European-derived PS to East Asians**: European-based PS successfully distinguished autism in East Asian ancestry as well (0.32 SD).

## Applications
- **Sex- and phenotype-based stratification in autism diagnosis and counseling**: the interpretation of genetic burden (de novo PTVs, PS) should consider not only sex but also comorbid ID and total symptom severity → phenotypic heterogeneity should be incorporated into clinical genetic counseling and risk assessment.
- **A family-based assessment framework**: a family-level approach that jointly examines the phenotypes and genetic burden of siblings and parents provides a methodological framework for elucidating the sex-differential liability-threshold model.
- **A multi-ancestry autism-genetics resource**: it validates the generalizability of the previously European-centric GWAS/PS in East Asian ancestry, serving as a reference resource for future multi-ancestry studies.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- **Need for larger female samples and balanced GWAS**: because the GWAS used for PS calculation is male-dominant, the PS of autistic females may be underestimated → large-scale data collection that includes both clinically diagnosed and sub-diagnostic females is needed to elucidate female-specific autism biology.
- **Use of SPARK female probands**: leverage SPARK's large number of female probands to more precisely investigate the sex-differential liability threshold.
- **Phenotype deconvolution**: refine and disaggregate diverse clinical scales and comorbidities beyond SRS and IQ to clarify the relationship between neurodiversity and genetic predisposition. (SRS was not measured in SPARK, which limits cross-cohort comparison.)
- **Examination of alternative liability-threshold models**: because the PS sex difference disappears after adjusting for FSIQ and SRS, alternative models—such as not only a higher threshold in females but also greater genetic variability in males—need to be tested.

## Disease Relevance
- Supports, using family-level genetic and phenotypic data, that a **higher liability threshold in females (female protective effect)** underlies the male-biased prevalence of autism (4:1).
- Identifies **comorbid ID and core-symptom severity as latent factors in sex-differential genetic burden** → emphasizes that autism should be understood not as a single entity but as a spectrum with phenotypic heterogeneity.
- Suggests the possible existence of a "buffering" mechanism whereby females (mothers, female siblings) carry a high genetic burden yet show a milder clinical phenotype, providing a rationale for research into sex-specific neurobiology.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- **The first large-scale East Asian autism WGS and gene–phenotype dataset**: prior evidence was centered on European ancestry (SSC, SPARK, MSSNG) and the US/Canada, and this is the first East Asian sex-difference genetic analysis. Its scale surpasses a previously published Chinese autism cohort (354 individuals).
- **Sex-difference analysis of the whole family (siblings and parents)**: rather than probands alone, the phenotypes and polygenic burden of non-autistic siblings and parents were decomposed by sex and by sibling sex pairs (Fₛ-Fc, Mₛ-Fc, etc.) to characterize sex differences in familial genetic predisposition.
- **Explaining cross-cohort inconsistency in PS sex differences via phenotypic heterogeneity**: it provides an integrated explanation that the previously contradictory reports of PS sex differences arise from phenotypic differences in comorbid ID and total symptom severity.
- **Integration of deep phenotyping (19 traits) with genetic burden**: it combines deep phenotypic measurement (roughly twice the level of SSC), encompassing siblings and parents, with genetic burden.
