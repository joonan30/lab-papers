# Whole-genome doubling is a double-edged sword: the heterogeneous role of whole-genome doubling in various cancer types

## One-Line Summary
This is a pan-cancer review that comprehensively synthesizes the origins, the dual (double-edged) tumor-promoting and tumor-suppressive effects, the cancer-type heterogeneity, and the clinical significance of Whole-Genome Doubling (WGD), arguing that WGD is central to cancer biology and to the development of personalized therapeutic strategies.

## Research Summary
Whole-genome doubling (WGD) is a phenomenon in which the entire chromosome set is duplicated and the genome becomes polyploid; it is a common genomic event observed in roughly 30-40% of patients across different cancer types. Whereas earlier genomic studies focused mainly on chromosomal changes such as aneuploidy or somatic copy number alteration (SCNA), WGD is a more specific phenomenon in which whole-chromosome-scale amplification occurs in particular patients and cancer subtypes. WGD plays a pivotal role in tumorigenesis by buffering the deleterious effects of mutations and by promoting the rapid accumulation of genetic abnormalities, but at the same time it produces the genomic instability that can trigger apoptosis, senescence, and immune-mediated elimination. In other words, WGD confers both advantages and disadvantages on cancer cells and is therefore a "double-edged sword."

The authors divide the origins of WGD into two categories. (1) Doubling within a single nucleus — endoreduplication (mitotic bypass) and endomitosis (mitotic slippage). (2) Doubling via multiple nuclei — cell fusion, cytokinesis failure, and entosis (cell cannibalism). These are triggered through diverse routes including replication stress, telomere shortening (in p53-deficient cells), CCNE1 amplification, cyclin B degradation following activation of the spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC), virus-induced cell fusion, and E-cadherin-mediated entosis.

The mechanisms by which WGD promotes tumorigenesis include expansion of genetic material and genetic diversity (duplicated genes provide evolutionary flexibility), induction of chromosomal instability (CIN), generation of multinucleated giant cells with proliferative capacity and stem-cell-like properties, and reorganization of three-dimensional chromatin structure (loss of the boundary between the active-transcription compartment A and the repressive compartment B, so that oncogenes move into A and tumor-suppressor genes move into B). Conversely, WGD cells already accumulate substantial DNA damage during the first S phase, and in response they activate p53 (p21^WAF1-mediated cell-cycle arrest) and the Hippo tumor-suppressor pathway (the LATS2 kinase inactivates YAP/TAZ and, in cooperation with ASPP1, stabilizes p53) to suppress the proliferation of polyploid cells. However, cancer cells bypass these tumor-suppressor pathways through TP53 mutation, MDM2/CCNE1/CCND1 amplification, CDKN2A deletion, E2F-mediated G1→S transition, and similar mechanisms.

The paper emphasizes that the incidence, genetic associations, and prognostic impact of WGD vary greatly across cancer types, and it summarizes both large-scale pan-cancer genomic studies (TCGA, metastatic-cancer cohorts, etc.) and the latest findings for lung, breast, colorectal, pancreatic cancers, and glioblastoma individually (Table 1). Finally, it discusses clinical implications, including therapeutic targets aimed at WGD vulnerabilities (KIF18A, PLK1, CDK4/6 inhibitors, etc.), the potential of WGD as a predictive marker of immune-checkpoint-blockade response, and possible applications in early diagnosis, concluding that a comprehensive understanding of WGD is essential for elucidating tumorigenic mechanisms and advancing personalized medicine.

## Methods & Technologies
(Because this is a review, the technologies, datasets, and computational tools covered in the cited and summarized studies are compiled here.)
- **Large-scale genomic cohorts**: TCGA (~10,000 primary tumors, 32 cancer types; separately 9,873 tumors, 33 cancer types), metastatic-cancer panel sequencing (9,692 samples), NSCLC multi-region sequencing (TRACERx, 401 and more patients), metastatic-cancer WGS/WES/targeted sequencing (2,520 pairs of metastatic solid-tumor and normal tissue).
- **Sequencing types**: whole-genome sequencing (WGS), whole-exome sequencing (WES), targeted/panel sequencing, single-cell DNA sequencing, single-cell RNA-seq, multi-region exome sequencing.
- **WGD detection / copy-number computation algorithms and tools**: ABSOLUTE (estimation of the genome-wide WGD fraction), FACETS (allele-specific copy number and clonal-heterogeneity analysis for WGS/WES/targeted sequencing), PURPLE (purity/ploidy estimation for WGS; WGD-positive is defined when major allele copy number > 2 in more than half of the autosome), GRIDSS/LINX (integrated structural-variant and copy-number analysis).
- **Copy-number signatures and mutational signatures**: copy number signatures (CN2, CN3 = high ploidy; CN10, CN11 = focal LOH; CN12 = CIN + a single WGD; CN8 = chromosome 7 in glioblastoma; CN14, CN15, CN16 = chromosomal/arm-scale loss preceding WGD), single-base-substitution signature SBS4 (DNA damage caused by smoking carcinogens), APOBEC signature.
- **Imaging / spatial techniques**: fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH), single-cell spatial mapping, quantitative proteomics/phosphoproteomics analysis, multi-omics (genome + transcriptome + proteome) integrated analysis.
- **Evolutionary / modeling approaches**: timing-model-based reconstruction of evolutionary history, simulation models (analysis of the order and correlation of LOH and WGD), circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) detection analysis.
- **Figure-preparation tool**: BioRender (used for Fig. 1).

## Key Genes / Proteins
- **TP53 / p53**: The central tumor suppressor that blocks the proliferation of WGD cells (cell-cycle arrest via p21^WAF1). Its mutation is the most representative genetic change enabling WGD and is significantly enriched in pan-cancer WGD-positive tumors.
- **p21^WAF1 (CDKN1A)**: A transcriptional product of p53 that induces G1/G2 arrest and prevents the spread of mitotic errors through Skp2 inhibition, pRb hypophosphorylation, and cyclin E accumulation.
- **CCNE1 / cyclin E**: When amplified, induces replication stress and promotes endoreduplication; associated with defective E2F-mediated G1 arrest.
- **RB1, BAP1**: Mutations are enriched in TP53-wild-type WGD tumors and cause defective E2F-mediated G1 arrest.
- **KIF18A**: A mitotic kinesin essential for the survival of WGD-positive cells; a promising therapeutic target in WGD tumors.
- **PPP2R1A (PPPR21A)**: A mutated gene enriched, together with TP53, in WGD-positive tumors pan-cancer.
- **MDM2, CCND1, CDKN2A**: Amplification (MDM2, CCND1) and deletion (CDKN2A) targets that enable WGD even in a TP53-wild-type context; associated with the E2F-mediated G1→S transition.
- **LATS2, YAP/TAZ, ASPP1**: Components of the Hippo tumor-suppressor pathway. LATS2 promotes the death of polyploid cells by inactivating YAP/TAZ and stabilizing p53.
- **Geminin, Wip1, APC/Cdh1, Skp2**: Cell-cycle/replication inhibitory factors (their degradation/activity regulation is involved in endoreduplication and tetraploid G1 arrest).
- **EGFR, RB1, TP53 (triple mutation)** and **KRAS, RBM10**: Driver mutations preceding WGD in lung cancer (particularly non-smoker NSCLC / EGFR-mutant Asian LUAD).
- **PIK3CA, GATA3, AKT1, ERBB2 (HER2)**: Oncogenic mutations occurring before WGD in breast cancer. PIK3CA activation induces centrosome amplification and increased tolerance of spontaneous WGD.
- **MYC, IRS2, EGFR, APC, SPINT2, USP28**: Associated with WGD in colorectal cancer (CRC). SPINT2 loss → bypass of G1 arrest; USP28 deletion → centrosome clustering and promotion of proliferation.
- **KIF20A, TPX2, AURKA, PLK1**: Mitotic proteins with increased phosphorylation in WGD cells of colorectal adenocarcinoma. PLK1 is a candidate therapeutic target.
- **IDH1, CDK4, MDM2, MDM4, PDGFRA**: Associated with WGD, chromosome 12q amplification, and chromothripsis in glioblastoma (including a case of response to a CDK4/6 inhibitor).

## Main Findings
- WGD is a common event that occurs in roughly **30-40%** of patients across different cancer types.
- Pan-cancer analysis (TCGA ~10,000 primary tumors, 32 cancer types): about **36%** of primary tumors experienced at least one WGD during their evolution, and WGD-positive tumors are significantly enriched for **TP53 and PPP2R1A** mutations.
- Metastatic-cancer panel sequencing (9,692 samples): about **30%** are WGD-positive and associated with TP53 mutation. However, TP53-wild-type WGD tumors are enriched for RB1/BAP1 mutations and CCNE1 amplification (suggesting defective E2F-mediated G1 arrest).
- The PURPLE algorithm detected WGD in **56%** of 2,520 metastatic solid-tumor pairs.
- **Lung cancer** has the highest WGD frequency among all cancer types. **77%** of NSCLC patients experienced at least one WGD during tumor progression, of which **19%** harbored subclonal WGD (suggesting that WGD is predominantly an early event in NSCLC). WGD prevalence in LUAD and LSCC is **59%** and **55%**, respectively. LOH precedes WGD, and subclonal WGD is a negative prognostic factor in NSCLC.
- **Breast cancer**: WGD observed in roughly **44%** of primary and **58%** of metastatic breast cancers. TNBC and HER2-positive tumors have higher WGD frequency than hormone-receptor-positive tumors. WGD is an independent prognostic predictor significantly associated with poor prognosis, particularly in ER-positive/HER2-negative, TP53-wild-type tumors.
- **Colorectal cancer (CRC)**: WGD itself predicts prognosis better than accompanying chromosomal abnormalities and is a useful prognostic marker in high-risk CRC. Early-onset CRC (particularly the non-hypermutated type) has a high WGD frequency. WGD-positive CRC shows roughly **53%** increased likelihood of ctDNA detection.
- **Pancreatic cancer (PDAC)**: WGD is positively correlated with metastatic potential. KRAS imbalance is associated with ploidy, and stepwise genomic evolution of TP53 LOH → deletion → WGD → amplification is observed. When chromothripsis co-occurs, overall survival is worse.
- **Glioblastoma**: The fraction of patients with WGD is small, but WGD is strongly associated with amplification of chromosome 7 (location of EGFR) and 12q (CDK4, MDM2). EGFR amplification appears as ecDNA/large-amplicon (tyfonas) forms. A case was reported in which a patient with 12q amplification showed considerable remission on a CDK4/6 inhibitor (combined with nelfinavir and leflunomide).
- **Immunotherapy prediction**: Metastatic melanoma patients with WGD showed better response to immune checkpoint blockade → suggesting the potential of WGD as a predictive marker of treatment response.

## Applications
- **Therapeutic-target discovery**: Selective therapy aimed at the vulnerabilities of WGD-positive tumors. Representative examples include KIF18A (essential for WGD-cell survival), PLK1 inhibitors (WGD-positive colorectal cancer), and CDK4/6 inhibitors (12q-amplified glioblastoma).
- **Prognostic / risk-stratification markers**: Using WGD status for cancer-type-specific prognostic prediction and subtype classification (high-risk CRC, TP53-wild-type ER+/HER2- breast cancer, subclonal-WGD lung cancer, etc.).
- **Early diagnosis and liquid biopsy**: Improved ctDNA detection sensitivity in WGD-positive CRC → aiding early detection.
- **Immunotherapy response prediction**: Using WGD as a biomarker for predicting immune-checkpoint-inhibitor response (metastatic melanoma).
- **Personalized medicine**: Defining a proliferative subtype with upregulated CIN/proliferation pathways through multi-omics integration, and establishing patient-specific therapeutic strategies.

## Extensibility / Future Directions
- In-depth study is needed of the impact of WGD on the tumor microenvironment and on intratumoral heterogeneity.
- Using single-cell DNA/RNA-seq to elucidate how WGD acts on these aspects of cancer biology.
- Because WGD occurs early in cancer, expanding research on using WGD for the **early detection** of tumors.
- Elucidating the key phosphorylation-signaling pathways of WGD subtypes and developing targeted therapies against them.
- Further study of the still-unclear relationship between WGD and overall survival (particularly in lung cancer).

## Disease Relevance
Cancer is the central topic. WGD promotes or suppresses tumorigenesis across a variety of solid tumors—lung, breast, colorectal, pancreatic cancers, and glioblastoma—and directly affects prognosis, metastasis, and treatment response. In particular, through its association with a TP53-mutant background, LOH precedence, APOBEC/smoking signatures, and its relationship to early-onset cancers, WGD provides practical implications for understanding tumor evolution, clinical risk stratification, and therapeutic-target and response prediction.

## Novelty vs. Prior Work
- Moving beyond the conventional genomic perspective centered on aneuploidy and SCNA, the review positions WGD as a distinct, specific phenomenon and integrates its **origins, dual (promoting/suppressing) effects, cancer-type heterogeneity, and clinical applications** into a single framework.
- By defining WGD as a "double-edged sword," it juxtaposes the tumor-promoting mechanisms with the tumor-suppressive mechanisms (p53 and Hippo pathways).
- Its novelty lies in being a mini review that synthesizes large-scale pan-cancer genomic/transcriptomic studies, the latest findings for five major cancer types, and the WGD-detection bioinformatics tools (ABSOLUTE, FACETS, PURPLE, GRIDSS/LINX) in a table (Table 1), reframing them from the perspective of personalized medicine.
